Colombia-Costa Rica Free Trade Agreement


Preamble

The Government of the Republic of Colombia and the Government of the Republic of Costa Rica, hereinafter referred to as the "Parties", determined to:

STRENGTHEN the traditional ties of friendship and the spirit of cooperation between their peoples;

PROMOTE economic development consistent with the protection and conservation of the environment, as well as sustainable development;

PROMOTE the creation of employment opportunities and improve the living standards of their peoples in their respective territories in order to reduce poverty;

CREATE a broader and more secure market for goods and services produced in their respective territories;

CONTRIBUTE to regional economic integration;

ESTABLISH clear and mutually beneficial rules for the commercial exchange of their goods and services, as well as for the promotion and protection of investments in their territories;

RECOGNIZE that the promotion and protection of investments of one Party in the territory of the other Party will contribute to increasing the flow of investments and stimulate mutually beneficial commercial activity;

AVOID distortions in their reciprocal trade;

PROMOTE the competitiveness of their companies in global markets;

STIMULATE creativity and innovation and promote trade in the innovative sectors of their economies;

EASEtrade by promoting efficient, transparent and predictable customs procedures for its importers and exporters;

PROMOTE transparency in international trade and investment;

PRESERVE its ability to safeguard the welfare and public order;

DEVELOP their respective rights and obligations derived from the Marrakech Agreement establishing the World Trade Organization , as well as other treaties to which they are party; and

RECOGNIZE that Colombia, as a member of the Andean Community, and Costa Rica, as a member of the Central American Integration System, are subject to rights and obligations within the framework of their respective integration processes,

HAVE AGREED , in pursuit of the foregoing, to conclude the following Free Trade Agreement (hereinafter referred to as the "Agreement"):

CHAPTER 1
INITIAL PROVISIONS AND GENERAL DEFINITIONS

SECTION A: INITIAL PROVISIONS

ARTICLE 1.1: ESTABLISHMENT OF THE FREE TRADE ZONE

The Parties to this Agreement, in accordance with the provisions of Article XXIV of the 1994 WTO General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade and Article V of the WTO General Agreement on Trade in Services , establish a free trade area .

ARTICLE 1.2: RELATIONSHIP WITH OTHER INTERNATIONAL AGREEMENTS

The Parties confirm the existing rights and obligations between them under the WTO Agreement and other agreements to which they are party.

In the event of any inconsistency between this Agreement and the agreements referred to in paragraph 1, this Agreement shall control to the extent of the inconsistency, except as otherwise provided in this Agreement.
ARTICLE 1.3: SCOPE OF OBLIGATIONS

Each Party shall ensure the adoption of all necessary measures to comply with the provisions of this Agreement in its territory and at all levels of government.

EPISODE 2
NATIONAL TREATMENT AND ACCESS OF GOODS TO THE MARKET

ARTICLE 2.1: SCOPE OF APPLICATION

Except as otherwise provided in this Agreement, this Chapter applies to a Party's trade in goods.

SECTION A: NATIONAL TREATMENT

ARTICLE 2.2: NATIONAL TREATMENT

Each Party shall grant national treatment to the goods of the other Party, in accordance with Article III of the GATT 1994, including its interpretative notes, and to that end, Article III of the GATT 1994 and its interpretative notes are incorporated into this Agreement. and form an integral part thereof, mutatis mutandis .

Paragraph 1 shall not apply to the measures listed in Annex 2-A.
SECTION B: TARIFF ELIMINATION

ARTICLE 2.3: TARIFF ELIMINATION

Except as otherwise provided in this Agreement, neither Party may increase any existing customs duty, or adopt any new customs duty, on an originating good of the other Party.

Except as otherwise provided in this Agreement, each Party shall eliminate its customs duties on originating goods of the other Party, in accordance with Annex 2-B.

The tariff elimination program provided for in this Chapter will not apply to used merchandise, even those that are identified as such in headings or subheadings of the Harmonized System. Used merchandise also includes reconstructed, refurbished, recovered, remanufactured merchandise, or any other similar name given to merchandise that, after having been used, has undergone a process to restore its original characteristics or specifications, or to restore its original condition. functionality they had when new.

At the request of either Party, consultations shall be held to consider improving the tariff conditions for market access in accordance with Annex 2-B.

Notwithstanding Article 20.1 (The Free Trade Commission), an agreement between the Parties to improve the tariff conditions of market access for a good shall prevail over any customs duty or category defined in Annex 2-B for such good, when be approved by the Parties in accordance with their applicable legal procedures.

For greater certainty, a Party may:

after a unilateral reduction, increase a customs duty to the level set out in Annex 2-B; either

maintain or increase a customs duty when authorized by the Dispute Settlement Body of the WTO.
SECTION C: SPECIAL REGIMENS

ARTICLE 2.4: EXEMPTION FROM CUSTOMS DUTIES

No Party may adopt a new exemption from customs duties, or extend the application of an exemption from existing customs duties in respect of current beneficiaries, or extend it to new beneficiaries, when the exemption is conditioned, explicitly or implicitly, on the fulfillment of a requirement of performance.

No Party may condition, explicitly or implicitly, the continuation of any existing customs duty exemption on the fulfillment of a performance requirement.
ARTICLE 2.5: TEMPORARY ADMISSION OF GOODS

Each Party shall authorize the temporary admission free of customs duties for the following goods, regardless of their origin:

professional equipment, including equipment for scientific research, medical activities, press or television, computer programs and broadcasting and cinematography equipment necessary for the exercise of the business activity, trade or profession of a person who qualifies for temporary entry according to with the legislation of the importing Party;

goods intended for display or demonstration at exhibitions, fairs, meetings or similar events;

commercial samples, advertising films and recordings; and

merchandise admitted for sporting purposes.

Each Party, upon request of the person concerned and for reasons that its customs authority considers valid, shall extend the term for temporary admission beyond the period initially set in accordance with its legislation.

No Party may condition the temporary admission free of customs duties to a good indicated in paragraph 1, to conditions other than that the good:

is used solely by or under the personal supervision of a national or resident of the other Party in the exercise of that person's business, trade, professional or sporting activity;

is not sold or leased while in its territory;

be accompanied by a bond or guarantee in an amount that does not exceed the charges that would be owed in your case for the final entry or importation, refundable at the time of departure of the merchandise;

is capable of identification when exported;

is exported upon the departure of the person referred to in paragraph (a), or within a term corresponding to the purpose of temporary admission that the Party may establish, or within one year, unless extended;

is admitted in quantities not greater than what is reasonable according to the intended use; and

is otherwise admissible into the territory of the Party in accordance with its law.

If any of the conditions imposed by a Party under paragraph 3 have not been met, the Party may apply the customs duty and any other charges that would normally be due for the good, plus any other charges or penalties established in accordance with its law. .

Each Party shall adopt and maintain procedures that facilitate expedited clearance of the goods admitted under this Article. To the extent possible, such procedures shall provide that when such merchandise accompanies a national or resident of the other Party that is requesting temporary entry, the merchandise must be released simultaneously with the entry of that national or resident.

Each Party shall allow a good temporarily admitted under this Article to be exported through a customs port other than the port through which it was admitted.

Each Party shall provide that the importer or other person responsible for a good admitted in accordance with this Article, shall not be responsible for the impossibility of exporting the good, upon presenting satisfactory evidence to the importing Party that the good has been destroyed, within the original deadline set for temporary admission or any lawful extension.

Subject to Chapter 12 (Investment) and Chapter 13 (Cross-Border Trade in Services), neither Party may:

prevent a vehicle or container used in international transportation that has entered its territory from the other Party from leaving its territory by any route that is reasonably related to the prompt and economic departure of such vehicle or container;

require a bond or impose any sanction or charge solely because the port of entry of the vehicle or container is different from the port of exit;

condition the release of any obligation, including any bond, that it has applied to the entry of a vehicle or container into its territory, on its departure through a particular port; and

require that the vehicle or carrier that brings a container to its territory from the territory of the other Party be the same vehicle or carrier that takes it to the territory of the other Party.

For purposes of paragraph 8, vehicle means a truck, truck tractor, tractor, trailer or trailer unit, locomotive or railcar, or other railroad equipment.
ARTICLE 2.6: GOODS RE-IMPORTED AFTER REPAIR OR ALTERATION

Neither Party may apply a customs duty to a good, regardless of its origin, that has been re-entered into its territory, after having been temporarily exported from its territory to the territory of the other Party to be repaired or altered, regardless of whether said repairs or alterations may have been made in the territory of the Party from which the good was exported for repair or alteration.

Neither Party may apply a customs duty to a good that, regardless of its origin, is temporarily admitted from the territory of the other Party, to be repaired or altered.

For the purposes of this Article, "repair or alteration" does not include an operation or process that:

destroys the essential characteristics of a good or creates a new or commercially different good; either

transform an unfinished good into a finished good.
ARTICLE 2.7: DUTY-FREE IMPORT FOR COMMERCIAL SAMPLES OF NEIGHBOR VALUE AND PRINTED ADVERTISING MATERIALS

Each Party shall authorize the duty-free import of commercial samples of negligible value and printed advertising materials imported from the territory of the other Party, regardless of their origin, but may require that:
such samples are imported only for the purpose of soliciting orders for goods or services provided from the territory of the other Party or from another non-Party country; either

such advertising materials are imported in packages that each contain no more than one printed copy and that neither the materials nor the packages are part of a larger consignment.
SECTION D: NON-TARIFF MEASURES

ARTICLE 2.8: IMPORT AND EXPORT RESTRICTIONS

Except as otherwise provided in this Agreement, neither Party may adopt or maintain any non-tariff measure that prohibits or restricts the import of any good of the other Party or the export or sale for export of any good destined for the territory of the other Party, except as provided in Article XI of the GATT 1994 and its interpretative notes, and for this purpose, Article XI of the GATT 1994 and its interpretative notes, are incorporated into this Agreement and form an integral part thereof, mutatis mutandis .

The Parties understand that the rights and obligations of the GATT 1994 incorporated by paragraph 1 prohibit, in any circumstance where any other type of restriction is prohibited, a Party from adopting or maintaining:

export and import price requirements, except as permitted for the enforcement of antidumping and countervailing duty provisions and commitments;

granting of import licenses conditional on meeting a performance requirement; either

voluntary export restrictions inconsistent with Article VI of the GATT 1994, implemented under the provisions of Article 18 of the Agreement on Subsidies and Article 8.1 of the Anti-Dumping Agreement.

Paragraphs 1 and 2 shall not apply to the measures set out in Annex 2-A.

Neither Party may require that, as a condition of an import commitment or for the importation of a good, a person of the other Party establishes or maintains a contractual or other type of relationship with a distributor in its territory.

For the purposes of paragraph 4, distributor means a person of a Party who is responsible for the commercial distribution, agency, concession or representation in the territory of that Party, of goods of the other Party.
ARTICLE 2.9: IMPORT LICENSES

No Party shall maintain or adopt any measure that is inconsistent with the WTO Agreement on Import Licensing Procedures (hereinafter referred to as the “Import Licensing Agreement”). For this purpose, the Import Licensing Agreement and its interpretative notes are incorporated into this Agreement and form an integral part thereof, mutatis mutandis .

Upon entry into force of this Agreement, each Party shall notify the other Party of any existing import licensing procedures.

Each Party shall notify the other Party of any new import licensing procedure and any modification to its existing import licensing procedures, within 60 days prior to its effective date. A notice provided under this Article:

it must include the information established in Article 5 of the Agreement on Import Licensing; and

it shall not prejudge whether the import licensing procedure is consistent with this Agreement.

Neither Party may apply an import licensing procedure to a good of the other Party without having provided a notification in accordance with paragraph 2 or 3, as appropriate.
ARTICLE 2.10: CHARGES AND ADMINISTRATIVE FORMALITIES

Each Party shall ensure, in accordance with Article VIII of the GATT 1994 and its interpretative notes, that all fees and charges of whatever nature (other than customs duties, charges equivalent to an internal tax or other internal charges applied in accordance with Article III:2 of the GATT 1994, and anti-dumping and countervailing duties), taxes on imports or exports or in relation to them, are limited to the approximate cost of the services rendered and do not represent indirect protection for national merchandise , nor a tax on imports or exports for fiscal purposes. For this purpose, Article VIII of the GATT 1994 and its interpretative notes are incorporated into this Agreement and form an integral part thereof, mutatis mutandis..

Neither Party shall require consular transactions, including related fees and charges, in connection with the importation of any good of the other Party.

Each Party shall make available and maintain, via the Internet, an up-to-date list of fees or charges imposed in connection with importation or exportation.
ARTICLE 2.11: TAXES AND OTHER EXPORT CHARGES

Except as provided in Annex 2-C, neither Party shall adopt or maintain a tax, levy or other charge on the export of any good to the territory of the other Party.

SECTION E: OTHER MEASURES


ARTICLE 2.12: STATE TRADING ENTERPRISES

The rights and obligations of the Parties with respect to State trading enterprises shall be governed by Article XVII of the GATT 1994, its interpretative notes and the Understanding Regarding the Interpretation of Article XVII of the GATT 1994 , which are incorporated and form an integral part of this Agreement, mutatis mutandis .

ARTICLE 2.13: CUSTOMS VALUATION

The Customs Valuation Agreement and any successor agreement shall govern the customs valuation rules applied by the Parties in their reciprocal trade. For this purpose, the Customs Valuation Agreement and any successor agreement are incorporated and form an integral part of this Agreement, mutatis mutandis .

The customs legislation of each Party shall comply with Article VII of the GATT 1994 and the Customs Valuation Agreement.
SECTION F: AGRICULTURE

ARTICLE 2.14: SCOPE OF APPLICATION AND COVERAGE

This Section applies to measures adopted or maintained by a Party related to trade in agricultural goods.

ARTICLE 2.15: AGRICULTURAL EXPORT SUBSIDIES

The Parties share the objective of the multilateral elimination of export subsidies for agricultural goods and should work together towards an agreement in the WTO to eliminate such subsidies and avoid their reintroduction in any form.

Neither Party may adopt or maintain any export subsidy on any agricultural good destined for the territory of the other Party.

Notwithstanding the provisions of the preceding paragraph, if a Party maintains, introduces, or reintroduces a subsidy for the export of a good included in Annex 2-B, the importing Party shall request the exporting Party in writing to start consultations to verify the existence or not of the export subsidy. If after 90 days of the request for consultations, the existence of the subsidy is confirmed and the exporting Party does not suspend it, the importing Party may increase the tariff rate on imports to the applied MFN tariff for the period in which the subsidy is maintained. the export. In order for the additional tariff to be eliminated, the other Party shall provide detailed information on the applied subsidy that demonstrates that it complies with the provisions of this Article. SECTION G: INSTITUTIONAL PROVISIONS

ARTICLE 2.16: COMMITTEE ON TRADE IN GOODS

The Parties establish the Committee on Trade in Goods (hereinafter referred to as the “Committee”), composed of representatives of each Party.

The meetings of the Committee, and of any Ad Hoc Working Group , will be chaired by representatives of the Ministry of Commerce, Industry and Tourism of Colombia and the Ministry of Foreign Trade of Costa Rica.
, or their successors.
The functions of the Committee will include, inter alia :

monitor the implementation and administration of this Chapter;

report to the Commission on the implementation and administration of this Chapter, when applicable;

promote trade in goods between the Parties, including through consultations on the expansion and acceleration of tariff elimination under this Agreement, and other matters as appropriate;

address obstacles to trade in goods between the Parties, especially those related to the application of non-tariff measures and, if appropriate, submit these matters to the Commission for its consideration;

provide the Commission with advice and recommendations on technical assistance needs in matters related to this Chapter;

review the conversion to the current Harmonized System nomenclature to ensure that the obligations of each Party under this Agreement are not altered, and hold consultations to resolve any conflict between:

the current Harmonized System nomenclature and Annex 2-B; and

national nomenclatures and Annex 2-B;

consult and make the best efforts to resolve any difference that may arise between the Parties, on matters related to the classification of merchandise under the Harmonized System;

establish Ad-Hoc Working Groups with specific mandates; and

deal with any other matter related to this Chapter.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will meet at least once a year, on the date and according to the previously agreed agenda. The first meeting of the Committee shall be held no later than one year after the date of entry into force of this Agreement. By mutual agreement, the Parties may hold extraordinary meetings.

The meetings may be held by any means agreed by the Parties. When they are face-to-face, they will be held alternately in the territory of each Party, and it will be up to the Host Party to organize the meeting.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will be permanent and will develop its work rules.

All decisions of the Committee must be taken by mutual agreement.

The Parties establish the Ad-Hoc Working Group on Trade in Agricultural Goods, which will report to the Committee. In order to discuss any matter related to market access for agricultural goods, this group will meet at the request of a Party, no later than 30 days after the request is submitted.

CHAPTER 3
RULES OF ORIGIN AND ORIGIN PROCEDURES

SECTION A: RULES OF ORIGIN

ARTICLE 3.1: ORIGINATING GOODS

Except as otherwise provided in this Chapter, a good is originating when:
is wholly obtained or produced entirely in the territory of one or both Parties, as defined in Article 3.2;

is produced in the territory of one or both Parties, from non-originating materials that comply with the change in tariff classification, the regional value content or other specific rules of origin contained in Annex 3-A; either

is produced in the territory of one or both Parties, exclusively from originating materials, and complies with the other provisions of this Chapter.
ARTICLE 3.2: WHOLELY OBTAINED OR PRODUCED GOODS

For the purposes of Article 3.1(a), the following goods shall be considered wholly obtained or wholly produced in the territory of one or both Parties:
plants and plant products harvested or collected in the territory of one or both Parties;

live animals, born and raised in the territory of one or both Parties;

goods obtained from live animals raised in the territory of one or both Parties;

goods obtained from hunting, trapping, fishing or aquaculture in the territory of one or both Parties;

fish, crustaceans and other marine species obtained from the sea or the seabed, outside the territory of a Party, by a vessel registered or enrolled in a Party and flying its flag;

goods produced on board factory ships registered or enrolled in a Party and flying its flag, exclusively from the goods indicated in paragraph (e);

minerals and other inanimate natural resources, extracted from the soil, water, seabed or subsoil in the territory of one or both Parties;

goods other than fish, crustaceans and other live marine species, obtained or extracted by a Party from the waters, seabed or subsoil outside the territory of a Party, provided that Party has rights to exploit such waters, seabed or subsoil;

waste and scrap derived from:

manufacturing operations conducted in the territory of one or both Parties; either

used goods collected in the territory of one or both Parties,

provided that said waste or waste is used only for the recovery of raw materials; and

goods produced in one or both Parties exclusively from the goods indicated in paragraphs (a) to (i).

ARTICLE 3.4: OPERATIONS OR MINIMUM PROCESSES

The operations or processes that, individually or in combination with each other, do not confer origin to a good are the following:

operations to ensure the preservation of merchandise in good condition during transport and storage;

grouping or fractioning of packages;

packing, unpacking or repackaging operations for retail sale;

animal sacrifice;

washing, cleaning and removing dust, rust, oil, paint or other coatings;

ironing or pressing of textiles;

simple 1painting and polishing operations;

shelling, total or partial bleaching, polishing and glazing of cereals and rice;

simple packaging in bottles, cans, jars, bags, cases and boxes and placement on cardboard or boards, and any other simple packaging operation;

placement of brands, labels, logos and other similar distinctive signs on the merchandise or its packaging;

simple mix of products; sugar mixture with any material; coloring or flavoring operations on sugar or sugar cubes; the total or partial grinding of sugar crystals;

simple assembly of parts of the merchandise to constitute a complete merchandise or the disassembly of merchandise into its pieces;

shelling, seeding and peeling of fruits, nuts and vegetables;

sharpened, simple crushed or simple cut; either

sieving, screening, selection, grading, grading, preparation (including kitting).

The provisions of this Article shall prevail over the specific rules of origin contained in Annex 3-A.
ARTICLE 3.5: INTERMEDIATE MATERIAL

When an intermediate material is used in the production of a good, the non-originating materials contained in said intermediate material will not be taken into account for the purpose of determining the origin of the good.

ARTICLE 3.6: ACCUMULATION

Goods or materials originating in the territory of a Party, incorporated into a good in the territory of the other Party, shall be considered as originating in the territory of that other Party.

A good will be considered originating, when it is produced in the territory of one or both Parties, by one or more producers, provided that the good meets the requirements established in Article 3.1 and all other applicable requirements of this Chapter.

When each Party has established a preferential trade agreement with the same country or the same group of non-Party countries, the goods or materials of said country or group of non-Party countries, incorporated into the territory of a Party, may be considered as originating in the territory of that Party, provided that the applicable rules of origin for that good or material under this Agreement are complied with.

For the application of paragraph 3, each Party must have agreed provisions equivalent to those indicated in said paragraph with the non-Party country or group of countries, as well as the conditions that the Parties consider necessary for the purposes of its application.
ARTICLE 3.7: DE MINIMIS

A good will be considered originating if the value of all non-originating materials used in its production, which do not comply with the change in tariff classification in accordance with Annex 3-A, does not exceed 10% of the FOB value of the good.

When the good mentioned in paragraph 1 is subject to a change in tariff classification and regional value content requirement, the value of all non-originating materials shall be included in the calculation of the regional value content of the good.

Paragraph 1 shall not apply to non-originating materials used in the production of goods classified in Chapters 1 through 24 of the Harmonized System, unless they are classified under a different subheading from the goods whose origin is determined under this Article. .

Paragraph 1 shall not apply to non-originating materials classified in Chapter 15 of the Harmonized System that are used in the production of goods classified in headings 15.01 to 15.08 or 15.11 to 15.15 of the Harmonized System.

Notwithstanding paragraph 1, a good from the textile and clothing sector classified in Chapters 50 to 63 of the Harmonized System, which is not originating because certain fibers or yarns used in the production of the component of the good that determines its tariff classification do not undergo the change in tariff classification established in Annex 3-A, it will be considered an originating good if the total weight of all these fibers or yarns in that component does not exceed 10% of the total weight of said component.

In all cases, the merchandise must comply with all other applicable requirements of this Chapter.
ARTICLE 3.8: CONSUMABLE GOODS AND MATERIALS

In order to determine whether a good is originating, any fungible good or material will be determined by:

a physical separation of the goods or materials; either

an inventory management method recognized in the Generally Accepted Accounting Principles of the exporting Party.

The inventory management method selected, in accordance with paragraph 1, for a particular good or fungible material, will continue to be used for those goods or materials, during the fiscal year of the person who selected the inventory management method.
ARTICLE 3.9: ACCESSORIES, SPARE PARTS AND TOOLS

The accessories, spare parts or tools delivered with the merchandise must be treated as originating if the merchandise is originating and will not be taken into account to determine if all non-originating materials used in the production of the merchandise comply with the corresponding change in tariff classification. as long as:

the accessories, spare parts or tools are classified with the merchandise and have not been invoiced separately, regardless of whether each is identified separately on the invoice itself; and

the quantities and value of said accessories, spare parts or tools are the usual ones for the merchandise.

If a good is subject to a regional value content requirement, the value of the accessories, spare parts or tools described in paragraph 1 shall be considered as originating or non-originating materials, as the case may be, when calculating the regional value content of the merchandise.
ARTICLE 3.10: GAMES OR ASSORTMENTS OF MERCHANDISE

If the goods are classified as a set or assortment as a result of the application of General Interpretive Rule 3 of the Harmonized System, the set or assortment will be considered as originating only if each good in the set or assortment is originating, and both the set or assortment as long as the goods meet all other applicable requirements of this Chapter.

Notwithstanding paragraph 1, a set or assortment of goods is originating if the value of all non-originating goods in the set or assortment does not exceed 15% of the FOB value of the set or assortment.
ARTICLE 3.11: CONTAINERS AND PACKAGING MATERIAL FOR RETAIL SALE

When containers and packaging materials for retail sale are classified with the merchandise, they will not be taken into account to determine the origin of the merchandise.

When the goods are subject to a regional value content requirement, the value of the packaging materials and containers for retail sale will be taken into account for the determination of the origin of the goods, as the case may be.
ARTICLE 3.12: CONTAINERS AND PACKAGING MATERIALS FOR SHIPMENT

Shipping containers and packing materials will not be taken into account to determine the origin of the merchandise.

ARTICLE 3.13: INDIRECT MATERIALS

For the purpose of determining whether a good is originating, indirect materials will be considered as originating regardless of the place of their production.

Indirect materials means items used in the production of a good that are not physically incorporated into or part of the good, including:

fuel, power, catalysts and solvents;

equipment, apparatus and accessories used for the verification or inspection of merchandise;

gloves, glasses, footwear, clothing, safety equipment and accessories;

tools, dies and molds;

spare parts and materials used in the maintenance of equipment and buildings;

lubricants, greases, compound materials, and other materials used in production, equipment operation, or building maintenance; and

any other good that is not incorporated into the good, but whose use in the production of the good can adequately be shown to be a part of that production.
ARTICLE 3.14: TRANSIT AND TRANSSHIPMENT

Each Party shall provide that a good shall not be considered an originating good if the good:
undergoes further processing or is subject to any other operation, outside the territory of the Parties, except unloading, reloading or any other operation necessary to keep the merchandise in good condition or to transport it to the territory of a Party; either

does not remain under the control of the customs authorities in the territory of a non-Party country.
SECTION B: ORIGIN PROCEDURES

ARTICLE 3.15: CERTIFICATION OF ORIGIN

The importer may request preferential tariff treatment based on a written or electronic certificate of origin, established in Annex 3-B, issued by the competent authority of the exporting Party at the request of the exporter.

The certificate of origin referred to in paragraph 1 shall be valid for one year from the date of its issuance.

The certificate of origin will cover one or more goods from a single shipment.

The exporter of the merchandise that requests a certificate of origin must present all the necessary documents that prove the originating character of the merchandise in question, as required by the competent authority. Likewise, the exporter must undertake to comply with the other requirements applicable to this Chapter.

In case of theft, loss or destruction of a certificate of origin, the exporter may request in writing to the competent authority that issued it, a duplicate of the original certificate on the basis of the export invoice or any other proof that would have served as a basis. for the issuance of the original certificate of origin, held by the exporter.

The duplicate issued in accordance with the previous paragraph must have in the observations field the phrase "DUPLICATE of the Certificate of Origin number [...] of date [...]", in order for the validity period to be counted from the indicated day .
ARTICLE 3.16: NOTIFICATIONS

Upon the entry into force of this Agreement, each Party shall provide the other Party with a record of the names of the officials accredited to issue certificates of origin, as well as samples of the signatures and impressions of the seals used by the competent authority for the issuance. of certificates of origin.

Any change in the registry indicated in paragraph 1 shall be notified in writing to the other Party. The change will become effective 15 days after receipt of the notification or a later period indicated in said notification.
ARTICLE 3.17: OBLIGATIONS RELATED TO IMPORTS

Except as otherwise provided in this Chapter, each Party shall require that an importer, who claims preferential tariff treatment in its territory:

declare in the customs import document, on the basis of a certificate of origin, that the good qualifies as originating in the other Party;

have the certificate of origin in their possession at the time the declaration referred to in paragraph (a) is made;

have in their possession documents certifying that the requirements established in Article 3.14 have been met; and

Provide the certificate of origin, as well as all the documentation indicated in paragraph (c) to the customs authority, when required.

Each Party shall provide that when the importer does not comply with any of the requirements established in paragraph 1, preferential tariff treatment shall be denied to the good imported from the territory of the other Party for which preference has been requested.

When the certificate of origin presents errors in such a way that there are no doubts regarding the accuracy of the information included in it, such as typing errors, they may be accepted by the customs authority of the importing Party.

When a certificate of origin is not accepted by the customs authority of the importing Party at the time of importation, due to omissions in its completion or errors other than those that do not affect compliance with origin or tariff preference, said customs authority will not deny preferential tariff treatment. In this case, the customs authority of the importing Party will request the importer, once and non-extendable, to present a new certificate of origin within a period of 15 days, counted from the day following the date of receipt of the certificate of origin. the notification of said omission or error and may authorize the release, after adopting the measures it deems necessary to guarantee the fiscal interest, in accordance with its legislation.

Once the period established in paragraph 4 has expired, if a new certificate of origin has not been presented correctly issued, the importing Party will deny the preferential tariff treatment, and if measures have been adopted to guarantee the fiscal interest, it will proceed to execute them.

In the event of presenting a new certificate of origin correctly issued and measures have been adopted to guarantee the fiscal interest, the measures will be lifted within a period of no more than 90 days, counted from the day following the presentation of the application for release of the measures by the importer to the customs authority of the importing Party, and may be extended for up to 30 additional days in exceptional cases.
ARTICLE 3.18: OBLIGATIONS RELATED TO EXPORTS

The exporter who has applied for a certificate of origin must provide or arrange for the producer to provide, at the request of the customs authority of the importing Party, all information used by said producer in the manufacture of the goods within the framework of a verification process in accordance with Article 3.24.

Each Party shall provide that:

An exporter to whom a certificate of origin has been issued shall promptly notify the customs authority of the importing Party in writing, with a copy to the competent authority of the exporting Party and the importer, of any changes that may affect the accuracy or validity of the certificate of origin. that certificate; and

If an exporter delivered a certificate of origin that contains or is based on false information and with it, goods qualified as originating to the territory of the other Party were exported, they will be subject to equivalent sanctions, with the appropriate modifications, to those that would be applied to an importer in its territory for violating its customs laws and regulations by making false declarations and claims in connection with an importation.

No Party shall impose sanctions on an exporter for providing a certificate of origin that contains or is based on incorrect information if it voluntarily communicates it in writing to the customs authority of the importing Party, with a copy to the competent authority of the exporting Party and to the importer, prior to the customs authority of the importing Party having begun to exercise its powers of verification and control, in accordance with the legislation of each Party.
ARTICLE 3.19: REIMBURSEMENT OF CUSTOMS DUTIES

When an originating good is imported into the territory of a Party, without the importer of the good having requested preferential tariff treatment at the time of importation, the importer may request, no later than one year after the date of acceptance of the declaration import customs, reimbursement of any duty paid in excess, as a result of not having requested preferential tariff treatment, presenting to the customs authority:
the certificate of origin, which must comply with the provisions established in Article 3.15; and

other documentation related to the importation of the merchandise, in accordance with the legislation of the importing Party.
ARTICLE 3.20: SUPPORTING DOCUMENTS

The documents used to demonstrate that the goods covered by a certificate of origin are considered originating goods and comply with the requirements of this Chapter, may include, inter alia , the following:
direct evidence of the processes carried out by the exporter or producer to obtain the referred goods, contained for example in their accounts or internal accounting;

documents proving the originating condition of the materials used;

documents proving the work or processing of the materials used; either

certificates of origin that prove the condition of origin of the materials used.
ARTICLE 3.21: PRESERVATION OF THE CERTIFICATE OF ORIGIN AND SUPPORTING DOCUMENTS

An exporter who requests the issuance of a certificate of origin must keep the documents referred to in Article 3.20 for a period of at least five years, counted from the date of its issuance.

The competent authority of the exporting Party issuing the certificate of origin shall keep a copy of the certificate of origin for a period of at least five years, counted from the date of its issuance.

An importer who requests preferential treatment for a good must keep, for a period of at least five years from the date of importation of the good, the documentation related to the import, including the certificate of origin.
ARTICLE 3.22: EXCEPTIONS TO THE OBLIGATION TO PRESENT THE CERTIFICATE OF ORIGIN

The Parties will not require a certificate of origin that demonstrates that a good is originating in the case of:

an import of goods whose customs value does not exceed one thousand United States dollars (US$ 1,000) or its equivalent in national currency or a greater amount established by the Party; either

an importation of goods for which the importing Party has waived the requirement to present the certificate of origin.

Paragraph 1 shall not apply to imports, including fractional ones, that are made or are intended to be made with the purpose of evading compliance with the certification requirements of this Chapter.
ARTICLE 3.23: COOPERATION BETWEEN AUTHORITIES

In the event that doubts arise about the authenticity or other elements related to the completion of the certificate of origin, including the origin criteria, the customs authority of the importing Party may request, by written request, information from the competent authority of the exporting Party. , in order to verify the above. The response to the request will be based on the information provided by the exporter at the time of issuance of the certificate of origin.

In this case, the competent authority of the exporting Party will have a period of 30 days following the date of receipt of the request, to provide the requested information.

In the event that the customs authority of the importing Party does not receive the requested information within the established term or doubts persist regarding the elements of the certificate of origin, it may initiate a verification process in accordance with Article 3.24.

In the event that the competent authority of the exporting Party does not recognize the authenticity of the certificate of origin, the customs authority of the importing Party may deny preferential tariff treatment to the goods covered by the certificate of origin under review.
ARTICLE 3.24: VERIFICATION PROCESS

In order to determine if a good imported by a Party from the other Party qualifies as an originating good, the competent authority of the importing Party may carry out a verification of origin through:

written requests for information to the exporter or producer;

written questionnaires addressed to the exporter or producer;

visits to the facilities of an exporter or producer in the territory of the other Party, with the purpose of observing the facilities and the production process of the merchandise and reviewing the records related to the origin, including accounting books and any type of supporting documents indicated in Article 3.20. The competent authority of the exporting Party may participate in these visits, as an observer; either

any other procedure that the Parties agree.

The customs authority of the importing Party shall notify the exporter, producer and importer of the initiation of the verification process and send a copy of the notification to the competent authority of the exporting Party.

For the purposes of this Article, the customs authority of the importing Party that carries out the verification of origin shall notify by certified mail with acknowledgment of receipt or by any means that records the receipt of the notification and the written requests for information, questionnaires and visits to exporters or producers.

For the purposes of paragraphs 1(a) and 1(b), the exporter or producer must respond to the request for information or questionnaire made by the customs authority of the importing Party, within a period of 30 days counted from its date of reception. During said term, the exporter or producer may, for a single time, request in writing to the customs authority of the importing Party the extension of the same, which cannot exceed 30 additional days. If within the established period, the exporter or producer does not duly complete the questionnaire, does not provide the requested information or does not respond, the customs authority of the importing Party will deny preferential tariff treatment for the merchandise in question.

When the customs authority of the importing Party has received the response to the written request for information or the questionnaire referred to in paragraphs 1(a) and 1(b), within the corresponding term, and considers that it requires more information to verify the origin of the merchandise subject to verification, may make a new request to the exporter or producer, which must be sent within a period not exceeding 30 days from the date of receipt of the request for additional information.

The importer, within a period of 30 days from the notification of initiation of the origin verification process, may provide the documents, evidence or statements that they consider pertinent. Additionally, he may request a one-time written extension to the customs authority of the importing Party, which may not exceed 30 days. The mere fact that the importer does not provide documents, evidence or statements will not be sufficient reason for the customs authority to deny preferential tariff treatment.

For the purposes of paragraph 1(c), the customs authority of the importing Party shall notify such request in writing, at least 30 days before the verification visit to the exporter or producer. In the event that the exporter or producer does not grant their written consent for the visit within a period of 15 days from the date of receipt of the notification, the customs authority of the importing Party will deny preferential tariff treatment to the merchandise in question. The request for the visit shall be communicated to the competent authority of the exporting Party.

When the exporter or producer receives a notification in accordance with paragraph 7, they may request, once only, within 15 days following the date of receipt of the notification, the postponement of the proposed verification visit for a period not exceeding 30 days from the date the notification was received, or for a longer term agreed upon by the customs authority of the importing Party and the exporter or producer. For these purposes, the customs authority of the importing Party must communicate the postponement of the visit to the competent authority of the exporting Party.

A Party shall not deny preferential tariff treatment based solely on the postponement of the verification visit.

The customs authority of the importing Party will draw up a record of the visit, which will contain the facts verified by it, and if applicable, a list of the information or documentation collected. Said record may be signed by the producer or exporter. In the event that the producer or exporter refuses to sign the record, this fact will be recorded, not affecting the validity of the visit.

The customs authority of the importing Party shall, within a period not exceeding 365 days from the date of receipt of the notification of the initiation of the verification process, notify the exporter or producer in writing of the results of the determination of origin of the merchandise, including the factual and legal grounds of the determination. You must also send a copy to the competent authority of the exporting Party.

Once the period established in paragraph 11 has elapsed, without the customs authority of the importing Party having issued a determination of origin, the preferential tariff treatment corresponding to the merchandise subject to verification shall be accepted.

The customs authority of the importing Party shall notify the importer in writing of the result of the origin verification process, which must be accompanied by the factual and legal grounds for the determination, respecting the confidentiality of the information provided by the exporter or producer.

If, as a result of an origin verification process, in accordance with this Article, the customs authority of the importing Party determines that the merchandise does not qualify as originating, it will deny preferential tariff treatment to the merchandise subject to verification. Likewise, said customs authority of the importing Party may suspend preferential tariff treatment to any subsequent importation of identical goods that have been produced by the same producer, until it is demonstrated before the customs authority of the importing Party that the goods qualify as originating according to the provisions of this Chapter.

The suspension of preferential tariff treatment, in accordance with paragraph 14, shall be communicated by the customs authority of the importing Party to the exporter or producer, importer and the competent authority of the exporting Party, stating the factual and legal grounds that justify its suspension. determination, and respecting the confidentiality of the information.
ARTICLE 3.25: SANCTIONS

Each Party will maintain or adopt criminal, civil or administrative sanctions for violations related to the provisions of this Chapter, in accordance with its legislation.

ARTICLE 3.26: RESOURCES FOR REVIEW AND APPEAL

Each Party shall ensure, with respect to its administrative acts related to the determination of origin, that importers, exporters or producers have access to:
a level of administrative review independent of the official or agency that issued the administrative act; and

a level of judicial review of the administrative act.
ARTICLE 3.27: CONFIDENTIALITY

Each Party shall maintain, in accordance with its legislation, the confidentiality of the information provided in the framework of an origin verification process.

Said information must not be disclosed without the express consent of the person delivering it, except in the case that it is required in the context of a judicial or administrative process.

Any violation of the confidentiality of the information must be treated in accordance with the legislation of each Party.
ARTICLE 3.28: BILLING BY A THIRD COUNTRY

In the case of an importation of originating goods in accordance with the provisions of this Chapter, the invoice presented at the time of importation may be issued by a person located in the territory of a non-Party country. In this case, this must be reflected in the certificate of origin, in accordance with the provisions of Annex 3-B.

ARTICLE 3.29: UNIFORM REGULATIONS

The Parties may establish, on the date of entry into force of this Agreement or on any other date that the Parties agree, uniform regulations regarding the interpretation, application and administration of this Chapter, which may be adopted by the Commission.

Once the uniform regulations have been agreed upon, each Party will put them into force, including any modification or addition to them, no later than 180 days after the respective agreement between the Parties, or in any other term that they agree.
ARTICLE 3.30: SENDING AND RECEIPT OF ELECTRONIC CERTIFICATES OF ORIGIN

The electronic certificate of origin:

It will only be valid if it is digitally signed by an official of the competent authority, designated for that purpose;

it will have a unique number with which each certificate can be individually identified; and

It will be exchanged between the competent authorities by a previously agreed electronic means.

The procedure for sending and receiving the electronic certificate of origin is included in Annex 3-C.
ARTICLE 3.31: DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this Chapter:

aquaculture means the cultivation or rearing of aquatic species, including, among others: fish, molluscs, crustaceans, other invertebrates and plants, covering their complete or partial life cycle, from seeds such as eggs, fish immatures, fry and larvae. It is carried out in a selected and controlled medium, in natural or artificial water environments, both in marine, fresh or brackish waters. The activities of population or sowing, and repopulation or replanting, cultivation, as well as the research activities and the processing of the products coming from said activity are included;

customs authority means:
in the case of Colombia, the Directorate of National Taxes and Customs ;
and

in the case of Costa Rica, the National Customs Service ;

or their successors;

competent authority means:
in the case of Colombia, the Directorate of National Taxes and Customs ;
and

in the case of Costa Rica, the Costa Rican Foreign Trade Promoter (PROCOMER);

or her successors;

CIF means the transaction value of the imported merchandise, including insurance and freight costs to the port or place of entry in the importing country, regardless of the means of transportation;

shipping containers and packing materials means goods used to protect a good during its transportation and does not include the containers and materials in which the good is packaged for retail sale;

exporter means a person located in the territory of a Party from which the good is exported;

FOB means the value of the merchandise free on board, including the cost of transportation to the port or place of final shipment, regardless of the means of transportation;

importer means a person located in the territory of a Party into which the good is imported;

material means a good that is used in the production of another good, including any component, ingredient, raw material, part or piece;

intermediate material means an originating material that is produced by the producer of a good and used in the production of that good;

merchandise means any product, article, or material;

non-originating good or non-originating material means a good or material that is not originating in accordance with this Chapter;

consumable goods or materialsmeans goods or materials that are interchangeable for commercial purposes and whose properties are essentially identical and that it is not possible to differentiate one from the other, by simple visual examination;

identical merchandise means identical merchandise, as defined in the Customs Valuation Agreement;

Generally Accepted Accounting Principles means recognized consensus or substantial support authorized and adopted in the territory of a Party with respect to the recording of income, expenses, costs, assets and liabilities, the disclosure of information and the preparation of financial statements. Generally Accepted Accounting Principles may encompass broad guidance of general application, as well as detailed standards, practices, and procedures;

production means the growing, extracting, harvesting, fishing, raising, trapping, hunting, manufacturing, processing, or assembling of a good; and

producer means a person who is involved in the production of a good in the territory of a Party.

CHAPTER 4
TRADE FACILITATION AND CUSTOMS PROCEDURES

ARTICLE 4.1: PUBLICATION

Each Party shall publish its customs legislation, regulations, and procedures, physically or online.

Each Party shall designate and maintain one or more consultation points to attend to the concerns of persons interested in customs matters, and shall make available on the Internet information regarding the procedures that must be followed to formulate such consultations.

To the extent possible, each Party shall publish in advance any regulation of general application on customs matters that it proposes to adopt, and shall provide interested persons the opportunity to comment prior to their adoption.

Each Party shall endeavor to ensure that its customs laws, regulations, and procedures are transparent, facilitate trade, and are non-discriminatory.

Each Party shall endeavor to publish, physically or online, information regarding duties and charges, established by customs and other government agencies, for services rendered in connection with the import and export of goods.
ARTICLE 4.2: DISPATCH OF GOODS

In order to facilitate trade between the Parties, each Party shall adopt or maintain simplified customs procedures for the efficient clearance of goods.

Pursuant to paragraph 1, each Party shall adopt or maintain procedures that:

provide that the clearance of merchandise is made within a period no longer than that required to ensure compliance with their customs legislation, and to the extent possible, within 48 hours of arrival;

allow the goods to be dispatched at the point of arrival, without mandatory transfer to warehouses or other facilities, except when the customs authority needs to carry out additional controls or for infrastructure reasons; and

allow importers, in accordance with their laws, to remove goods from customs before all applicable customs duties, taxes and fees have been paid. For this purpose, they may require the prior presentation of a sufficient guarantee to cover the full payment of customs duties, taxes and applicable rates, in relation to the importation.

Each Party shall ensure that all the competent administrative entities involved in the control and physical inspection of the merchandise subject to import or export, whenever possible, act simultaneously, in a single place and time.
ARTICLE 4.3: AUTOMATION

Each Party shall endeavor to use information technology that makes the procedures for the clearance of goods expeditious and efficient. When choosing the information technology to be used for such purpose, each Party:

will make efforts to use internationally recognized norms, standards and practices;

make electronic systems accessible to users of its customs;

will allow the submission and electronic processing of information and data before the arrival of the merchandise, in order to allow its clearance in accordance with the provisions of Article 4.2;

will use electronic and/or automated systems for risk analysis and management;

work on the development of compatible electronic systems between the customs authorities of the Parties, in order to facilitate the exchange of international trade data between them;

work to develop the set of common data elements and processes in accordance with the World Customs Organization Customs Data Model and related recommendations and guidelines of the World Customs Organization (hereinafter referred to as the “WCO”); and

will support customs operations, in the context of commercial transactions, without paper, to the extent possible, taking into account the evolution in these matters within the WCO.

Each Party shall adopt or maintain, to the extent possible, procedures that allow expeditious control of the means of transport of goods leaving or entering its territory.
ARTICLE 4.4: ADMINISTRATION OR MANAGEMENT OF RISKS

Each Party shall maintain risk administration or management systems that allow its customs authority to focus its inspection activities on high-risk merchandise, and that simplify the clearance and movement of low-risk merchandise, respecting the confidential nature of the information obtained. through such activities, in accordance with its legislation.

When applying risk administration or management, each Party shall inspect imported goods based on appropriate selectivity criteria, avoiding physical inspection of all the goods entering its territory, and to the extent possible, with the help of non-intrusive inspection instruments.
ARTICLE 4.5: EXPRESS DELIVERY SHIPMENTS

Each Party shall adopt or maintain special customs procedures for express delivery shipments, while maintaining adequate control and selectivity systems in accordance with the nature of these goods.

The procedures referred to in paragraph 1 shall:

provide for separate and expedited customs procedures for express delivery shipments;

provide for the submission and processing of information necessary for the clearance of an express delivery shipment, prior to the arrival of such shipment;

allow the presentation of a single cargo manifest that covers all the merchandise contained in a shipment transported by an express delivery service, through electronic means;

provide for the clearance of certain merchandise with a minimum of documentation; and

Under normal circumstances, expect expedited shipments to be cleared within six hours of presentation of the necessary customs documents, provided the shipment has arrived.
ARTICLE 4.6: AUTHORIZED ECONOMIC OPERATOR

The Parties will promote the implementation of Authorized Economic Operator programs in accordance with the WCO Framework of Regulations to Secure and Facilitate Global Trade (“SAFE Framework of Regulations”).

The obligations, requirements and formalities of the programs, as well as the benefits that will be offered to companies that meet the requirements, will be established in accordance with the legislation of each Party.

The Parties will promote negotiations to reach an agreement for mutual recognition of the Authorized Economic Operator programs.
ARTICLE 4.7: INTEROPERABILITY OF SINGLE WINDOWS FOR FOREIGN TRADE

The Parties will endeavor to implement the interconnection between their Foreign Trade Single Windows (hereinafter referred to as "VUCE"), for this, no later than six months after the entry into force of this Agreement, they will adopt the necessary actions to achieve the VUCE interoperability by providing electronic services that facilitate export and import procedures under this Agreement. These actions will promote a set of general principles that should characterize the electronic services provided at the binational level, including: accessibility, security, confidentiality, and use of open standards. Likewise, the Parties will adopt an interoperability framework that guides the technical work and establishes the elements or data that will be exchanged,

ARTICLE 4.8: CONFIDENTIALITY

Each Party will maintain, in accordance with the provisions of its legislation, the confidentiality of the information of such nature and that has been collected pursuant to this Chapter, and will protect it from any disclosure.

Confidential information received pursuant to this Chapter may not be used for purposes other than those provided for herein, except with the authorization of the Party that provided it.
ARTICLE 4.9: REVIEW AND APPEAL

Each Party shall ensure, with respect to customs administrative acts, that natural or legal persons subject to such acts have access to:
a level of administrative review, which is independent of the official or office that issued said act; and

(b) at least one level of judicial review.
ARTICLE 4.10: SANCTIONS

Each Party shall adopt or maintain measures that allow the imposition of administrative sanctions and, when appropriate, criminal sanctions, for the violation of its customs legislation and regulations, including those that govern tariff classification, customs valuation, origin, and requests for preferential treatment under this Agreement.

ARTICLE 4.11: ADVANCE RULINGS

Each Party must issue a written advance ruling prior to the importation of a good into its territory, when an importer in its territory, or an exporter or producer in the territory of the other Party 11 has requested it in writing, regarding:

tariff classification;

the application of customs valuation criteria for a particular case, in accordance with the application of the provisions contained in the Customs Valuation Agreement 2;

if a good is originating according to Chapter 3 (Rules of Origin and Procedures of Origin); and

Other matters that the Parties agree.

Each Party shall issue the advance ruling within 90 days of the submission of the application, provided that the applicant has submitted all the information that the Party requires, including, if requested, a sample of the merchandise for which the applicant You are requesting an advance ruling. The previous term may be extended in the events contemplated by the legislation of a Party. When issuing the advance ruling, the Party shall take into account the facts and circumstances that the applicant has presented.

Each Party shall provide that advance rulings take effect from the date of their issuance, or another date specified in the ruling provided that the facts or circumstances on which the ruling is based have not changed.

The Party issuing the advance ruling may modify or revoke it after notifying the applicant, when the criteria, facts, circumstances, laws, regulations or rules that served as the basis for its issuance change, or when it has been based on incorrect or false information. In the event that the aforementioned modification or revocation is based on a change in the criteria, facts, circumstances, laws, regulations or norms that served as a basis, said modification or revocation may be applied from the date on which said criteria, facts, circumstances, laws, regulations or norms take effect. In the event of incorrect or false information, said modification or revocation may be applied from the date of issuance of the aforementioned advance resolution.

Subject to the confidentiality requirements under its law, each Party may make its advance rulings publicly available.

If an applicant provides false information or omits relevant facts or circumstances related to the advance ruling, or does not act in accordance with the terms and conditions thereof, the Parties may apply the relevant measures, including civil, criminal and administrative actions in accordance with the legislation of each Party.
ARTICLE 4.12: COMMITTEE ON RULES OF ORIGIN, TRADE FACILITATION AND TECHNICAL COOPERATION AND MUTUAL ASSISTANCE IN CUSTOMS MATTERS

The Parties establish the Committee on Rules of Origin, Trade Facilitation and Technical Cooperation and Mutual Assistance in Customs Matters (hereinafter referred to as the "Committee"), made up of representatives of each Party.

The functions of the Committee will include, inter alia :

monitor the implementation and administration of Chapter 3 (Rules of Origin and Origin Procedures), Chapter 4 and Chapter 5 (Technical Cooperation and Mutual Assistance in Customs Matters);

report to the Commission on the implementation and administration of the chapters referred to in paragraph (a), when applicable;

cooperate in the effective, uniform and consistent administration of the chapters referred to in paragraph (a);

deal promptly with the matters that a Party proposes regarding the development, adoption, application or execution of the provisions of the chapters referred to in paragraph (a);

review and recommend to the Commission any modification to Annex 3-A (Specific Rules of Origin), including when amendments are made to the Harmonized System;

promote the joint cooperation of the Parties in the development, application, execution and improvement of all issues concerning the Chapters referred to in paragraph (a), including, in particular, customs procedures, customs valuation, customs regimes and tariffs, customs nomenclature, customs cooperation, matters related to customs matters, mutual administrative assistance in customs matters, as well as providing a forum for consultation and discussion for said issues;

at the request of a Party, resolve any matter that arises under the chapters referred to in paragraph (a), within a period of 30 days, counted from the presentation of the request, extendable by mutual agreement for a maximum of two equal periods;

in the matters that are required, propose to the Commission alternative solutions to the obstacles or inconveniences related to the chapters referred to in paragraph (a) that arise between the Parties; and

deal with any other matter related to the chapters referred to in paragraph (a).

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will meet at least once a year, on the date and according to the previously agreed agenda. The first meeting of the Committee shall be held no later than one year after the date of entry into force of this Agreement. By mutual agreement, the Parties may hold extraordinary meetings.

The meetings may be held by any means agreed by the Parties. When they are face-to-face, they will be held alternately in the territory of each Party, and it will be up to the Host Party to organize the meeting.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will be permanent and will develop its work rules.

All decisions of the Committee must be taken by mutual agreement.

A Party that considers that one or more provisions of the chapters referred to in paragraph 2(a) should be modified, may submit a modification proposal together with the technical support and the studies that support it, for the consideration of the Commission. The Commission may refer the matter to this Committee, so that it may prepare a report including conclusions and recommendations in this regard.
CHAPTER 5
TECHNICAL COOPERATION AND MUTUAL ASSISTANCE IN CUSTOMS MATTERS

ARTICLE 5.1: SCOPE OF APPLICATION

The provisions of this Chapter are intended to regulate technical cooperation and mutual assistance in customs matters between the Parties, in accordance with what is established herein and the legislation of each Party.

The Parties, through their competent authorities, will provide technical cooperation and mutual assistance in order to ensure the proper application of customs legislation, the facilitation of customs procedures, and the prevention, investigation, and sanction of customs infractions.

Technical cooperation includes the exchange of information, legislation, good practices in customs matters, as well as the exchange of experiences, training and any kind of technical or material support suitable for strengthening the customs management of the Parties.

Mutual assistance includes the cooperation provided for in this Chapter, for the prevention, investigation and punishment of customs offenses.

Compliance with this Chapter must be without prejudice to mutual assistance established in other international agreements entered into between the Parties. If technical cooperation or mutual assistance is provided under other international agreements in force, the requested Party shall indicate the name of the respective agreement and the name of the relevant authorities involved.

Assistance in the collection of duties, taxes or fines is not covered by this Chapter.
ARTICLE 5.2: TECHNICAL COOPERATION

Each Party, in accordance with its legislation and within the scope of its competence and available resources, shall promote and facilitate technical cooperation with the respective competent authority of the other Party in order to ensure the application of customs legislation and especially for:

facilitate and expedite the flow of goods between the Parties;

promote mutual understanding of each Party's customs legislation, procedures, best practices and techniques; and

provide statistics, in accordance with the legislation of each Party.

Technical cooperation will cover, among other things, the following:

training for the development of specialized skills of its customs officials;

the exchange of professional, scientific and technical information, in new technologies and methods, related to customs legislation and applicable procedures;

the exchange of information to facilitate trade, simplify customs procedures, the movement of means of transport and their merchandise and with respect to restrictions and/or prohibitions on exports and imports applied, in accordance with the legislation of the Parties;

cooperation in the areas of research, development and testing of new customs procedures; either

the development of initiatives in mutually agreed areas.

A request for technical cooperation must be dealt with as soon as possible in consideration of the competence and available resources of the requested authority.

Technical cooperation will promote the development, application, execution, and improvement of all issues related to this Chapter, in particular, customs control, customs procedures, customs value, customs regimes, and tariff nomenclature.
ARTICLE 5.3: MUTUAL ASSISTANCE

The Parties will provide mutual assistance, in the areas of their competence, in the manner and under the conditions provided for in this Chapter, to guarantee the correct application of customs legislation, in particular for the prevention, investigation and sanction of customs infractions. .

ARTICLE 5.4: COMMUNICATION OF MUTUAL ASSISTANCE INFORMATION

Upon request and in accordance with its legislation, the requested authority must provide the requested information and any other pertinent information on investigations related to a customs offense in the territory of the requesting authority.

The information exchanged by the competent authorities in accordance with their legislation will deal with:

people related to a request for information;

goods destined for the customs territory of the requesting authority, or sent in international transit with or without temporary storage, for subsequent transit to said territory;

customs operations that have been carried out in its territory and means of transport used, related to the request; either

Customs offenses that may be of interest to the other Party in the context of the request.
ARTICLE 5.5: ATTENTION TO REQUESTS FOR MUTUAL ASSISTANCE

Upon request, the requested authority shall send the requesting authority information on:

the correspondence of the documents produced to support the customs declaration made to the requesting authority;

whether the goods exported from the territory of the requesting authority have been lawfully imported into the territory of the requested authority;

whether the goods imported into the territory of the requesting authority have actually been exported from the territory of the requested authority; and

information on the determination of customs value.

At the request of the requesting authority, the requested authority will adopt, within the framework of its legal or regulatory provisions, the necessary measures to ensure special vigilance over:

the goods transported or that may be transported in such a way that there are indications that they are destined to be used in the commission of infractions of the customs legislation; either

the means of transport that are being or may be used in such a way that there are indications that they are intended to be used in the commission of customs offences.
ARTICLE 5.6: FORM AND CONTENT OF REQUESTS FOR MUTUAL ASSISTANCE

Requests for mutual assistance under this Chapter must be addressed in writing to the authority requested by the requesting authority, physically or electronically, attaching the applicable documents. The requested authority may request confirmation of the requests in physical form when they are made electronically.

In urgent cases, a request may be made orally. Such request must be confirmed in writing, physically or electronically as soon as possible.

Requests made in accordance with paragraph 1 must include the following details:

the name, signature and position of the official making the request;

the information required and the reason for the request;

a brief description of the matter including a summary of the pertinent facts and of the investigations already carried out, if applicable, as well as the legal elements and the nature of the customs procedure;

the names, addresses, identification documents or any other information that is known and relevant to the persons to whom the request relates; and

the necessary information available to identify the goods or the customs declaration related to the application.

The information referred to in this Chapter will be communicated to the officials specially designated for this purpose by each competent authority. To this end, each Party must keep the competent authority of the other Party informed of the updated list of designated officials.

If a request does not meet the formal requirements established above, the requested authority may request that it be corrected or completed; meanwhile, appropriate surveillance measures may be adopted in accordance with the relevant Party's legislation.
ARTICLE 5.7: EXECUTION OF REQUESTS FOR MUTUAL ASSISTANCE

The complete response to the request for mutual assistance must be provided within a maximum period of 60 days, counted from the receipt of the written request.

Upon request, the requested authority may carry out an investigation in accordance with the powers established in the legislation, to obtain information related to a customs infraction and will provide the requesting authority with the results of said investigation and all related information that it deems pertinent.

If the requested information is available in an electronic version, the requested authority may provide it by electronic means to the requesting authority, unless the requesting authority has requested otherwise.

Duly authorized officials of a Party may, with the agreement of the requested authority and subject to the conditions, laws and other legal instruments established by the latter, be present at the offices and performances of the requested authority, in order to obtain information relevant in the context of an investigation aimed at establishing a customs offence.
ARTICLE 5.8: EXCEPTIONS TO THE OBLIGATION TO PROVIDE MUTUAL ASSISTANCE

Mutual assistance may be denied or subject to compliance with certain conditions or requirements in cases where a Party considers that assistance under this Chapter could:

be prejudicial to the sovereignty of the Party from which assistance has been requested;

be detrimental to public order and security;

violate an industrial, commercial or professional secret, duly protected by its legislation; either

be unconstitutional or contrary to its legislation.

The requested authority may defer assistance when it considers that it may interfere with an ongoing investigation, criminal proceedings or administrative proceedings. In such a case, the requested authority shall consult the requesting authority to determine whether it is appropriate to provide the assistance subsequently.

When the requesting authority requests assistance that it itself would not be in a position to provide if requested, it shall state this fact in its request. It will then be up to the requested authority to decide how it should respond to such a request.

In the cases referred to in paragraphs 1 and 2, the decision of the requested authority and its reasons must be communicated without delay to the requesting authority.
ARTICLE 5.9: SPONTANEOUS ASSISTANCE

For the correct application of customs legislation, to the extent of its possibilities and competences, each Party will provide assistance on its own initiative, providing information in accordance with its legislation, related to:
cases involving damage to the economy, public health, public safety or other vital interest of one of the Parties;

new means or practices used in the commission of customs offenses; either

in the other cases contemplated in Article 5.4.
ARTICLE 5.10: VALIDITY OF INFORMATION

Upon request, the requested authority can certify the copies of the requested documents. 2. The documents provided by virtue of this Chapter will not need for their probative validity additional certification, authentication, or any other type of solemnity than that provided by the competent authority and will be considered authentic and valid.

Any information to be exchanged under this Chapter may be accompanied by additional information that is relevant to interpret or use it.
ARTICLE 5.11: USE OF INFORMATION

The information, documents and other materials will be used solely for the purposes established in this Chapter, and subject to the restrictions that may be established by the Party, consistent with the provisions of its legislation, including in cases where it is required. within the framework of administrative procedures, judicial or investigative processes carried out by the competent authority or whoever corresponds.

ARTICLE 5.12: CONFIDENTIALITY

If requested by the competent authority of a Party, the information, documents and other materials obtained in the framework of technical cooperation must be treated confidentially.

Information, documents and other materials exchanged within the framework of mutual assistance will always be treated as confidential by the Parties.

In the cases provided for in paragraphs 1 and 2, each Party shall grant a level of protection, in terms of confidentiality, similar to that applied to the same type of information, documents and other materials in its territory.
ARTICLE 5.13: EXPERTS OR EXPERTS

The delegated officials of the requested Party may be authorized to appear, within the limits of said authorization and in accordance with its legislation, as experts or experts in administrative procedures or judicial proceedings, regarding the matters regulated in this Chapter and to produce the objects, documents or certified copies thereof that may be necessary for the procedures. The request for appearance must specifically indicate the judicial or administrative authority before which the official must appear, and on what matters and by virtue of what title or capacity the official will be questioned.

ARTICLE 5.14: COSTS

The competent authorities will waive any claim for reimbursement of costs and/or expenses incurred in the execution of the requests provided for in this Chapter, except those related to experts or experts, which will be assumed by the requesting authority.

ARTICLE 5.15: DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this Chapter:

competent authority means:
in the case of Colombia, the Directorate of National Taxes and Customs (DIAN); and

in the case of Costa Rica, the National Customs Service,

or their successors;
requested authority means the competent authority from which cooperation or assistance is requested;

requesting authority means the competent authority requesting cooperation or assistance;

information means documents, reports or other communications in any format, including electronic, as well as certified copies thereof;

customs infraction means any transgression or attempted transgression of customs legislation, which may be administrative, tax or any other in accordance with the legislation of each Party; and

customs legislationmeans any law, regulation or other legal instrument applicable in the territory of each Party that regulates the entry, exit, transit of goods, and their inclusion in any customs regime, including prohibition, restriction and control measures, among others.
CHAPTER 6
SANITARY AND PHYTOSANITARY MEASURES

ARTICLE 6.1: OBJECTIVES

The objectives of this Chapter are:
protect the life and health of people and animals and preserve plant health in the territories of the Parties;

ensure that sanitary and phytosanitary measures (hereinafter referred to as “SPS”) do not constitute unjustified obstacles to trade between the Parties;

establish mechanisms and procedures aimed at promptly resolving problems that arise between the Parties as a consequence of the development and implementation of the SPS;

strengthen communication and collaboration between the competent authorities of the Parties in sanitary and phytosanitary matters aimed at improving the mutual understanding and application of the SPS measures of the Parties; and

collaborate in the further implementation of the SPS Agreement.
ARTICLE 6.2: SCOPE OF APPLICATION

This Chapter applies to all SPS measures that directly or indirectly affect or may affect trade between the Parties.

ARTICLE 6.3: RIGHTS AND OBLIGATIONS OF THE PARTIES

The Parties reaffirm their rights and obligations in accordance with the SPS Agreement, taking as reference the guidelines, procedures and standards of the Codex Alimentarius, the International Plant Protection Convention (hereinafter referred to as "IPPC") and the World Organization for Animal Health ( hereinafter referred to as “OIE”).

ARTICLE 6.4: RISK ASSESSMENT AND DETERMINATION OF THE APPROPRIATE LEVEL OF SANITARY AND PHYTOSANITARY PROTECTION

The SPS measures applied by the Parties will be based on an evaluation appropriate to the circumstances of the existing risks to the life and health of people, animals and for the preservation of plant health, taking into account the technical-scientific information generated by the Parties, as well as the standards, guidelines and recommendations of international reference organizations.

The Parties will prioritize, in accordance with their legislation, the performance of the risk assessment for the establishment of the SPS. For these purposes, the competent authorities of the Parties will maintain close communication, at each stage of the risk analysis process, in order to expedite it and avoid undue delays.

The establishment of SPS measures derived from a risk assessment may not become disguised restrictions on trade between the Parties.
ARTICLE 6.5: ADAPTATION TO REGIONAL CONDITIONS, INCLUDING AREAS FREE OF PESTS OR DISEASES AND AREAS WITH LOW PREVALENCE OF PESTS OR DISEASES

The Parties shall recognize pest- or disease-free areas and areas of low pest or disease prevalence, in accordance with the SPS Agreement, OIE and IPPC standards, recommendations or guidelines.

The Parties acknowledge the recommendations expressed in the standards on compartmentalization established by the OIE and on freedom from pests established by the IPPC.

If a Party does not recognize the determination of areas free of pests or diseases or areas of low prevalence of pests or diseases made by the other Party, it must justify the technical and/or scientific reasons for such refusal in a timely manner, in order to evaluate a possible alternative solution.
ARTICLE 6.6: CONTROL, INSPECTION AND APPROVAL PROCEDURES

The Parties shall establish the inspection, control and approval procedures taking into account Article 8 and Annex C of the SPS Agreement.

ARTICLE 6.7: TRANSPARENCY

The Parties shall be governed in accordance with the provisions of Annex B of the SPS Agreement.

Additionally:

they will notify the competent authority of the information on product rejections, including the justification on which they were based;

they shall notify the competent authority of the exporting Party of non-compliance situations in the certifications of export products subject to the application of MSF, including as much information as possible, as well as the reasons for their rejection;

immediately notify any change in their sanitary and phytosanitary status, including findings of epidemiological importance that may affect trade between the Parties;

they will inform, at the request of a Party, the SPS of specific products and/or the status of the processes and measures in process regarding the requests for the access of products; and

they will notify the SPS and the approval of the products and exporting establishments in a timely manner.

Notifications shall be made in writing to the contact points established in accordance with this Chapter. Written notification shall be understood to mean notifications by postal mail, fax or email.

When a modification of an SPS measure has a significant effect on trade between the Parties, the Parties shall define by mutual agreement mechanisms to prevent the interruption of the flow of trade while such modifications are adopted and implemented.

Likewise, the Parties shall make their best efforts to improve mutual understanding of SPS and their application, and shall exchange information on matters related to the development and application of SPS that affect or may affect trade between the Parties, with a view to minimizing their effects. Negative in trade.
ARTICLE 6.8: EQUIVALENCE

The recognition of SPS equivalence may be given considering the norms, guidelines and recommendations established by the competent international organizations and the decisions adopted by the Committee on Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures of the WTO on the matter.

ARTICLE 6.9: COOPERATION AND TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE

The Parties may develop actions and/or programs of common interest, in terms of cooperation and technical assistance.

ARTICLE 6.10: TECHNICAL INQUIRIES

The Parties will promote active interaction between the competent authorities in order to prevent unjustified restrictions on trade, achieve mutual understanding, and attend to matters related to the application of the SPS, through the use of different means such as emails, calls phone calls, official letters, among others.

In the event that the competent authorities do not reach an understanding in relation to the application of an SPS measure and that one of the Parties considers contrary to the obligations of this Chapter, it may request technical consultations within the framework of the Committee established in Article 6.11.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, when a dispute is the subject of consultations in the Committee in accordance with the provisions of the preceding paragraph, said consultations shall replace those provided for in Article 18.4 (Consultations) of this Agreement. Consultations in the Committee shall be considered concluded within a period of 60 days from the date of submission of the request, unless the Parties agree to continue with them.
ARTICLE 6.11: COMMITTEE ON SANITARY AND PHYTOSANITARY MEASURES

The Parties establish the Committee on Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures (hereinafter referred to as the "Committee"), made up of the competent national authorities of each Party, with the objective of addressing issues related to the application of this Chapter.

The functions of the Committee will include, inter alia :

monitor the implementation and application of the established SPS measures;

report to the Commission on the implementation and administration of this Chapter, when applicable;

serve as a forum to discuss problems related to the development or application of the SPS that affect or may affect trade between the Parties to establish solutions and determine their terms of attention;

respond to queries derived from Article 6.10;

promote cooperation, technical assistance, training, and the exchange of information on sanitary and phytosanitary matters;

consult on the position of the Parties on issues to be discussed at the meetings of the WTO Committee on Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures, the Codex Alimentarius committees and other forums of interest to the Parties; and

deal with any other matter related to this Chapter.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will meet at least once a year, on the date and according to the previously agreed agenda. The first meeting of the Committee shall be held no later than six months after the date of entry into force of this Agreement. By mutual agreement, the Parties may hold extraordinary meetings.

The meetings may be held by any means agreed by the Parties. When they are face-to-face, they will be held alternately in the territory of each Party, and it will be up to the Host Party to organize the meeting.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will be permanent and will develop its working rules during its first meeting.

All decisions of the Committee must be taken by mutual agreement.
ARTICLE 6.12: COMPETENT NATIONAL AUTHORITIES

The national authorities listed below are responsible for the application of this Agreement:
for Colombia, the Ministry of Commerce, Industry and Tourism, as coordinator, the Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Development, the Colombian Agricultural Institute (ICA), the Ministry of Health and Social Protection, and the National Institute for Food and Drug Surveillance ( INVIMA); and

for Costa Rica, the Ministry of Foreign Trade , as coordinator, the Ministry of Agriculture and Livestock, and the Ministry of Health .
ARTICLE 6.13: POINTS OF CONTACT

The contact points are:

for Colombia, the Ministry of Commerce, Industry and Tourism, the Colombian Agricultural Institute (ICA), and the National Institute for Food and Drug Surveillance (INVIMA); and

for Costa Rica, the General Directorate of Foreign Trade of the Ministry of Foreign Trade , the State Phytosanitary Service of the Ministry of Agriculture and Livestock , the National Animal Health Service of the Ministry of Agriculture and Livestock , and the Ministry of Health .

The Parties will exchange information regarding the contact points established upon the entry into force of this Agreement and will notify any modification thereof.
ARTICLE 6.14: SETTLEMENT OF DISPUTES

Once the consultation procedure in accordance with Article 6.10 has been exhausted, the Party that is not satisfied with the result of said consultations may resort to the dispute resolution procedure established in Chapter 18 (Dispute Resolution).

CHAPTER 7
TECHNICAL BARRIERS TO TRADE

ARTICLE 7.1: OBJECTIVES

The objectives of the Chapter are to facilitate and increase trade in merchandise and obtain effective access to the market of the Parties, through a better implementation of the TBT Agreement, avoiding the constitution or promoting the elimination of unnecessary technical obstacles to trade, as well as promoting cooperation between the Parties in matters covered by this Chapter.

ARTICLE 7.2: SCOPE OF APPLICATION

This Chapter applies to the preparation, adoption, and application of all standards, technical regulations, and conformity assessment procedures, including those related to metrology, of each Party, which may directly or indirectly affect trade in goods.

Notwithstanding the provisions of paragraph 1, this Chapter does not apply to:

sanitary and phytosanitary measures, which will be covered by Chapter 6 (Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures); and

the purchase specifications established by government institutions for the production or consumption needs of government institutions, which will be governed by Chapter 10 (Government Procurement).
ARTICLE 7.3: RIGHTS AND OBLIGATIONS OF THE PARTIES

The Parties reaffirm their rights and obligations under the TBT Agreement, which is incorporated into and forms an integral part of this Agreement, mutatis mutandis .

ARTICLE 7.4: TRADE FACILITATION

The Parties will seek to identify, develop, and promote trade facilitation initiatives in relation to standards, technical regulations, and conformity assessment procedures, taking into account the respective experience of the Parties in other appropriate bilateral, regional, or multilateral agreements.

The initiatives referred to in paragraph 1 may include:

intensify joint cooperation to facilitate access to their markets and increase knowledge and understanding of their respective systems;

simplify the procedures and administrative requirements established by a technical regulation;

work towards the possibility of converging, aligning, or establishing equivalence of technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures;

use accreditation or designation as a tool to recognize conformity assessment bodies established in the territory of the other Party in accordance with internationally accepted practices and standards;

promote and facilitate cooperation and the exchange of information between public or private organizations of the Parties;

promote convergence or harmonization with international standards, recognize and accept the results of conformity assessment procedures, as well as the supplier's declaration of conformity, and use accreditation to qualify conformity assessment entities, as well as cooperation through mutual recognition agreements.

When a Party detains a good originating in the territory of the other Party at the port of entry by virtue of having perceived non-compliance with a technical regulation, it must immediately notify the importer of the reasons for the detention.
ARTICLE 7.5: REFERENCE STANDARDS

The Parties shall use international standards, guidelines, and recommendations as a basis for the development of their technical regulations, except in the case that these international standards are an ineffective or inappropriate means to achieve the legitimate objective pursued. When international standards have not been used as a basis for the preparation of their technical regulations, at the request of one Party, the other Party will explain the reasons.

In determining whether there is an international standard, guide, or recommendation within the meaning of Article 2, Article 5, and Annex 3 of the TBT Agreement, each Party shall apply the Decision of the Committee on Principles for the Elaboration of Standards, Guides and international recommendations in accordance with Articles 2 and 5 and Annex 3 of the Agreement , adopted on November 13, 2000 by the Committee on Technical Barriers to Trade.

Each Party shall encourage its national standardization bodies to cooperate with the relevant national standardization bodies of the other Party in international standardization activities. Such cooperation may be effected through the activities of the Parties in regional and international standardization bodies of which the Parties are members.

The Parties shall exchange information on the use of standards in connection with technical regulations and shall ensure, to the extent possible, that standards referenced indicatively in draft technical regulations are provided at the request of the other Party.

The Parties shall recommend, as appropriate, that non-governmental standardization entities located in their territory comply with the provisions of this Article.
ARTICLE 7.6: TECHNICAL REGULATIONS

Each Party will consider favorably the possibility of accepting as equivalent the technical regulations of the other Party, even when they differ from their own, provided they are satisfied that they adequately comply with the legitimate objectives of their own technical regulations.

When a Party does not accept a technical regulation of the other Party as equivalent to one of its own, it shall, at the request of the other Party, explain the reasons for its decision.

At the request of a Party that has an interest in developing a technical regulation similar to the technical regulation of the other Party and to minimize duplication of costs, the other Party will provide any available information, technical or risk assessment studies or other relevant documents. , on which the development of this technical regulation has been based, except for confidential information.

The Parties will cooperate on regulatory matters by promoting rapprochement between regulatory bodies in order to establish more compatible, transparent and simple regulations and reduce unnecessary obstacles to trade.
ARTICLE 7.7: CONFORMITY ASSESSMENT

The Parties recognize that a wide range of mechanisms exist to facilitate the acceptance in the territory of one Party of the results of conformity assessment procedures carried out in the territory of the other Party. In particular, the Parties agree, among other mechanisms, to the following:

acceptance by the importing Party of the supplier's declaration of conformity;

the recognition of voluntary agreements between the conformity assessment bodies of the territory of the Parties;

acceptance of the results of conformity assessment procedures by bodies located in the territory of the other Party with respect to specific technical regulations;

the acceptance of internationally recognized accreditation procedures to qualify conformity assessment bodies located in the territory of another Party; and

the designation of conformity assessment bodies located in the territory of another Party.

The Parties will intensify the exchange of information in relation to these and other similar mechanisms, to facilitate the acceptance of results of conformity assessment procedures.

In the event that a Party does not accept the results of a conformity assessment procedure carried out in the territory of the other Party, the latter shall, at the request of that other Party, explain the reasons for its decision.

Each Party shall accredit, authorize, or otherwise recognize conformity assessment bodies in the territory of the other Party under terms no less favorable than those granted to conformity assessment bodies in its territory. If a Party accredits, authorizes, or otherwise recognizes a body that assesses the conformity of a specific standard or technical regulation in its territory and refuses to accredit, authorize, or otherwise recognize a body that assesses the conformity of that same standard or technical regulation in the territory of the other Party, the latter shall, at the request of that other Party, explain the reasons for its decision.

The Parties may enter into negotiations aimed at concluding mutual recognition agreements for the results of their respective conformity assessment procedures, following the principles of the TBT Agreement. In the event that a Party does not agree to start said negotiations, it must, at the request of that other Party, explain the reasons for its decision.
ARTICLE 7.8: TRANSPARENCY

Each Party shall electronically transmit to the contact point of the other Party, established under Article 10 of the TBT Agreement, at the same time as it submits its notification to the WTO Central Registry of Notifications in accordance with the TBT Agreement:

(a) its draft technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures; and

the technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures adopted to address urgent safety, health, environmental protection or national security problems that arise or threaten to arise under the terms of the TBT Agreement.

Each Party shall publish on the website of the competent national authority those technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures that are consistent with the technical content of any relevant international standard. This publication will remain available to the public while the aforementioned technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures are in force.

Each Party shall grant a period of at least 60 days from the date of the notification indicated in paragraph 1(a) for the other Party to make written comments on the proposal. A Party will give favorable consideration to reasonable requests for an extension of the period established for comments.

Each Party shall publish or make available to the public, either in print or electronically, its responses to significant comments it receives from the other Party pursuant to paragraph 3, no later than the date it publishes the final version. of the technical regulation or the conformity assessment procedure.

The notification of draft technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures will include an electronic link to, or a copy of, the full text of the notified document.

A Party shall, at the request of the other Party, provide information about the objective and basis of the technical regulation or conformity assessment procedure that said Party has adopted or proposes to adopt.

The Parties agree that the period between the publication and the entry into force of the technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures shall not be less than six months, unless it is not feasible to meet their legitimate objectives with that period. The Parties will give favorable consideration to reasonable requests for extension of time.

The Parties shall ensure that all adopted and current technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures are publicly available on a free official Internet page, in such a way that they are easily located and accessible.

Each Party shall implement the provisions of paragraph 4 as soon as possible and in no event later than three years from the entry into force of this Agreement.
ARTICLE 7.9: TECHNICAL COOPERATION

At the request of a Party, the other Party shall favorably consider any sector-specific proposals that the requesting Party makes to encourage further cooperation under this Chapter.

The Parties agree to cooperate and provide technical assistance in the field of standards, technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures, including metrology, with a view to facilitating access to their markets. In particular, the Parties shall consider the following activities, among others:

promote the application of this Chapter;

favor the application of the TBT Agreement;

Strengthen the capacities of their respective bodies for standardization, technical regulations, conformity assessment, metrology, and information and notification systems under the scope of the TBT Agreement, including training of human resources;

increase participation in international organizations, including those of a regional nature, related to standardization, technical regulation, conformity assessment, and metrology; and

encourage the agreement of common points of view on good regulatory practices such as transparency, the use of equivalence and the evaluation of the impact of regulations, and the use of mechanisms to facilitate the acceptance of the results of conformity assessment procedures made in the territory of the other Party.
ARTICLE 7.10: TECHNICAL INQUIRIES

The Parties shall make all efforts to reach a mutually satisfactory solution, regarding the consultations referred to in Article 7.11.2(g), within a period of 30 days.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, when a dispute is the subject of consultations in the Committee pursuant to the provisions of Article 7.11.2(g), such consultations shall supersede those provided for in Article 18.4 (Consultations) of this Agreement.
ARTICLE 7.11: COMMITTEE ON TECHNICAL BARRIERS TO TRADE

The Parties establish the Committee on Technical Barriers to Trade (hereinafter referred to as the “Committee”), made up of representatives of each Party and coordinated in accordance with Annex 7-A.

The functions of the Committee will include, inter alia :

monitor the implementation and administration of this Chapter;

report to the Commission on the implementation and administration of this Chapter, when applicable;

deal promptly with matters that a Party proposes regarding the development, adoption, application, or enforcement of standards, technical regulations, or conformity assessment procedures;

promote the joint cooperation of the Parties in the development and improvement of standards, technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures, including metrology;

facilitate sectoral cooperation between governmental and non-governmental organizations in the area of ​​standards, technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures, including metrology, in the territories of the Parties, as appropriate;

exchange information about the work carried out in non-governmental, regional and multilateral forums involved in activities related to standards, technical regulations and conformity assessment procedures;

resolve consultations on any matter that arises under this Chapter, at the request of a Party;

review this Chapter in light of any developments under the TBT Agreement, and decisions or recommendations of the WTO TBT Committee, and make suggestions on possible amendments to this Chapter;

take any other action that the Parties consider will assist them in the implementation of this Chapter and the TBT Agreement and in the facilitation of trade between the Parties;

recommend to the Commission the establishment of working groups to deal with specific matters related to this Chapter and the TBT Agreement; and

deal with any other matter related to this Chapter.

The representatives of each Party shall be responsible for coordinating with the relevant bodies and persons in their territory, as well as ensuring that such bodies and persons are convened.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will meet at least once a year, on the date and according to the previously agreed agenda. The first meeting of the Committee shall be held no later than one year after the date of entry into force of this Agreement. By mutual agreement, the Parties may hold extraordinary meetings.

The meetings may be held by any means agreed by the Parties. When they are face-to-face, they will be held alternately in the territory of each Party, and it will be up to the Host Party to organize the meeting.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will be permanent and will develop its working rules during its first meeting.

All decisions of the Committee must be taken by mutual agreement.
ARTICLE 7.12: EXCHANGE OF INFORMATION

Any information or explanation that is provided at the request of a Party in accordance with the provisions of this Chapter, must be provided in print or electronically within a period of 30 days, which may be extended with the prior justification of the reporting Party.

Regarding the exchange of information, in accordance with Article 10 of the TBT Agreement, the Parties must apply the recommendations indicated in the document Decisions and Recommendations adopted by the WTO TBT Committee since January 1, 1995, G/TBT/1 /Rev.10, June 9, 2011 Section VB Exchange of Information issued by the TBT Committee.
ARTICLE 7.13: DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this Chapter, the terms and definitions of Annex 1 of the TBT Agreement shall apply.

CHAPTER 8
TRADE DEFENSE

SECTION A: BILATERAL SAFEGUARD MEASURES

ARTICLE 8.1: IMPOSITION OF A BILATERAL SAFEGUARD MEASURE

During the transition period, if as a result of the reduction or elimination of a customs duty under this Agreement, an originating good of one of the Parties is being imported into the territory of the other Party, in quantities such that they have increased by absolute terms or in relation to domestic production and under conditions such that they constitute a substantial cause of serious injury or threat of serious injury to the domestic industry that produces a like or directly competitive good, the importing Party may adopt a safeguard measure bilateral described in paragraph 2.

If the conditions in paragraph 1 are met, a Party may, to the extent necessary to prevent or remedy serious injury or threat of serious injury and facilitate adjustment:

suspend the future reduction of any tariff rate established in this Agreement for the good; either

increase the rate of duty for the good to a level not to exceed the lesser of:

the applied MFN tariff rate at the time the measure is applied; either

the base duty rate as set forth in Annex 2-B (Tariff Elimination Schedule) 1
The adoption of a bilateral safeguard measure provided for in this Section will not affect the goods that are actually shipped on the effective date of the measure, as stated in the transport documents, provided that they are intended for final consumption or final importation within a period not exceeding 20 days from the end of unloading in the territory of the importing Party.
ARTICLE 8.2: STANDARDS FOR A BILATERAL SAFEGUARD MEASURE

No Party may maintain a bilateral safeguard measure:

except to the extent and for the period necessary to prevent or remedy serious injury and facilitate adjustment;

for a period exceeding two years; except that this period is extended for an additional two years, if the competent authority determines, in accordance with the procedures stipulated in Article 8.3, that the measure is still necessary to avoid or remedy serious injury and facilitate adjustment and that there is evidence that the domestic industry is in the readjustment process; either

after the expiration of the transition period.

In order to facilitate adjustment in a situation where the expected duration of a bilateral safeguard measure exceeds one year, the Party applying the measure shall liberalize it progressively, at regular intervals, during the period of application.

A Party may not apply a bilateral safeguard measure more than once against the same good until a period equal to the duration of the previous bilateral safeguard measure, including any extension, has elapsed, starting from the termination of the bilateral safeguard measure. above, provided that the period of non-application is at least one year.

Upon termination of the bilateral safeguard measure, the Party that has adopted it must apply the tariff rate in accordance with its tariff reduction list included in Annex 2-B (Tariff Elimination Program).
ARTICLE 8.3: INVESTIGATION PROCEDURES AND TRANSPARENCY REQUIREMENTS

A Party may only apply a bilateral safeguard measure after an investigation by the Party's competent authority in accordance with Articles 3 and 4.2(c) of the Agreement on Safeguards; and to this end, Articles 3 and 4.2(c) of the Agreement on Safeguards are incorporated into and made an integral part of this Agreement, mutatis mutandis .

In the investigation described in paragraph 1, the Party shall comply with the requirements of Articles 4.2(a) and 4.2(b) of the Agreement on Safeguards; and to this end, Articles 4.2(a) and 4.2(b) of the Agreement on Safeguards are incorporated into and made an integral part of this Agreement, mutatis mutandis .

Each Party shall ensure that its competent authorities complete this type of investigation within the deadlines established in its legislation.
ARTICLE 8.4: PROVISIONAL BILATERAL SAFEGUARD MEASURES

In critical circumstances, in which any delay entails damage that is difficult to repair, a Party may apply a provisional bilateral safeguard measure by virtue of a preliminary determination of the existence of clear evidence demonstrating that the increase in imports of goods originating in the another Party, as a result of the reduction or elimination of a customs duty under this Agreement, has caused or is threatening to cause serious injury to the domestic industry.

The duration of the provisional bilateral safeguard shall not exceed 200 days, it shall adopt any of the forms provided for in Article 8.1.2 and shall comply with the pertinent requirements of Articles 8.1 and 8.3. The guarantees or funds received by way of provisional measures will be released or reimbursed promptly, when the investigation does not determine that the increase in imports has caused or threatened to cause serious damage to the domestic industry. The duration of any provisional bilateral safeguard measure will be counted as part of the duration of a definitive bilateral safeguard measure.

Neither Party shall apply provisional measures before 45 days have elapsed from the date of initiation of the investigation.
ARTICLE 8.5: NOTIFICATION AND CONSULTATION

A Party shall promptly notify the other Party in writing when:

initiate a bilateral safeguard procedure in accordance with this Section;

apply a provisional bilateral safeguard measure; and

take the final decision to apply or extend a bilateral safeguard measure.

A Party shall provide the other Party with a copy of the public version of the report of its competent investigating authority, required in accordance with Article 8.3.1.

At the request of a Party whose good is subject to a bilateral safeguard procedure in accordance with this Chapter, the Party carrying out the procedure shall initiate consultations, within 15 days following the request, with the requesting Party to review the notifications. under paragraph 1 or any public notice or report issued by the competent investigating authority in connection with such proceedings.
ARTICLE 8.6: COMPENSATION

No later than 30 days after it applies a bilateral safeguard measure, a Party shall provide an opportunity for the other Party to consult with it regarding appropriate trade liberalization compensation, in the form of concessions having substantially equivalent trade effects. , or equivalent to the value of the additional duties expected as a result of the measure.

If the Parties fail to agree on compensation within 30 days after initiating consultations, the exporting Party may suspend the application of substantially equivalent concessions to the trade of the Party applying the bilateral safeguard measure.

The exporting Party shall notify the Party applying the bilateral safeguard measure in writing at least 30 days before suspending concessions under paragraph 2.

The right of suspension referred to in paragraph 2 shall not be exercised during the first two years of validity of the bilateral safeguard measure, provided that the safeguard measure has been adopted as a result of an increase in absolute terms of the imports and that such a measure is in accordance with the provisions of this Agreement.

The obligation to provide compensation under paragraph 1 and the right to suspend concessions under paragraph 2 shall terminate on the date the bilateral safeguard measure terminates.
ARTICLE 8.7: DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this Section:

threat of serious harm means the clear imminence of serious harm based on facts and not simply on the basis of allegation, conjecture or remote possibility;

competent investigating authority means:
in the case of Colombia, the Ministry of Commerce, Industry and Tourism ; and

in the case of Costa Rica, the Directorate of Trade Defense of the Ministry of Economy, Industry and Commerce ,

or their successors;

substantial cause means a cause that is important and not less than any other cause;

serious injury means a significant overall impairment of the position of a domestic industry;

transition period means the five-year period beginning on the date of entry into force of this Agreement, except for any good for which Annex 2-B (Tariff Elimination Schedule) of the Party applying the safeguard measure establishes that it eliminate its tariffs for the merchandise in a period of five years or more, where transition period means the period of tariff reduction of the merchandise established in Annex 2-B (Tariff Elimination Program) plus an additional period of two years .
SECTION B: GLOBAL SAFEGUARD MEASURES

ARTICLE 8.8: GLOBAL SAFEGUARD MEASURES

Each Party retains its rights and obligations under Article XIX of the GATT 1994 and the Agreement on Safeguards.

This Agreement does not confer any additional rights or obligations on the Parties with respect to actions taken pursuant to Article XIX of the GATT 1994 and the Agreement on Safeguards, except that the Party imposing a global safeguard measure may exclude imports of a originating merchandise of the other Party, if such imports do not constitute a substantial cause of serious injury or threat of serious injury. 3. For the purposes of paragraph 2, imports from the other Party will normally be considered not to constitute a substantial cause of serious injury or threat of serious injury, if this Party is not among the five main suppliers of the merchandise subject to the procedure,

Neither Party shall apply with respect to the same good and during the same period:

a bilateral safeguard measure under Section A; and

a measure under Article XIX of the GATT 1994 and the Agreement on Safeguards.

For the purposes of this Section, competent investigating authority means:

in the case of Colombia, the Ministry of Commerce, Industry and Tourism; and

in the case of Costa Rica, the Directorate of Trade Defense of the Ministry of Economy, Industry and Commerce, or its successors.

Except as provided in paragraphs 2, 3, and 4, Chapter 18 (Dispute Resolution) shall not apply to this Section.
SECTION C: ANTI-DUMPING AND COUNTERVAILING DUTIES

ARTICLE 8.9: ANTI-DUMPING AND COUNTERVAILING DUTIES

Each Party retains its rights and obligations under Article VI of the GATT 1994, the Anti-Dumping Agreement and the Subsidies Agreement, with respect to the application of anti-dumping and countervailing duties.

Except as provided in paragraphs 3 and 4, nothing in this Agreement shall be construed to impose any right or obligation on the Parties with respect to anti-dumping and countervailing duties.

After receiving a duly documented request for the application of an antidumping or countervailing measure, related to imports of goods originating from the other Party, and before initiating the investigation, the competent investigating authority shall notify the receipt of the request to the other Party. Party and will provide adequate opportunities to hold informative or other technical meetings regarding said request. These technical meetings will not interfere with the decision to initiate or not the dumping or subsidy investigation.

In the event that price or other undertakings have been proposed in an anti-dumping or subsidy investigation, the competent investigating authority shall provide adequate opportunities for the other Party to consult with respect to the proposed undertakings which, if accepted, could give rise to to the suspension of the investigation without the imposition of antidumping or countervailing duties, in accordance with the legislation of each Party.

For the purposes of this Section, competent investigating authority means:

in the case of Colombia, the Ministry of Commerce, Industry and Tourism ; and

in the case of Costa Rica, the Directorate of Trade Defense of the Ministry of Economy, Industry and Commerce ,

or their successors.

Except as provided in paragraphs 3 and 4, Chapter 18 (Dispute Resolution) shall not apply to this Section.
SECTION D: COOPERATION

ARTICLE 8.10: COOPERATION

The Parties agree to establish a cooperation mechanism between their investigating authorities. Cooperation between the Parties may include, inter alia, the following activities:
exchange of non-confidential information available on trade defense investigations, including circumvention investigations, that have been carried out with respect to imports originating in or coming from third countries, other than the Parties;

technical assistance in the field of commercial defense; and

exchange of information in order to improve understanding of this Chapter and of the trade defense regimes of the Parties.
CHAPTER 9
INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY

ARTICLE 9.1: BASIC PRINCIPLES

The Parties recognize that the protection and enforcement of intellectual property rights should contribute to the generation of knowledge, the promotion of innovation, transfer and diffusion of technology, and cultural progress, for the reciprocal benefit of knowledge producers and users. technological and cultural, favoring the development of social and economic well-being and the balance of rights and obligations.

The Parties recognize the need to maintain a balance between the rights of intellectual property rights holders and the interests of the general public, in particular, in education, culture, research, public health, food safety, the environment and access to information.

Considering the provisions of this Chapter, the Parties, when formulating or modifying their laws and regulations, may adopt the necessary measures to protect the public health and nutrition of the population, or to promote the public interest in sectors of vital importance for their development. socioeconomic and technological.

The Parties recognize that the transfer of technology contributes to the strengthening of national capacities that allow the establishment of a solid and viable technological base.

The Parties, when interpreting and implementing the provisions of this Chapter, shall observe the principles established in the Declaration on the Agreement on TRIPS and Public Health , adopted on November 14, 2001 at the Fourth Ministerial Conference of the WTO.

The Parties will contribute to the implementation and respect of the Decision of the General Council of the WTO of August 30, 2003 on paragraph 6 of the Declaration on the TRIPS Agreement and Public Health , and the Protocol amending the TRIPS Agreement , signed in Geneva on December 6, 2005. Likewise, they recognize the importance of promoting the gradual implementation of Resolution WHA61.21, Global strategy and action plan on public health, innovation and intellectual property , adopted by the 61st World Health Assembly, May 24, 2008.

The Parties shall ensure that the interpretation and implementation of the rights and obligations assumed under this Chapter shall be consistent with paragraphs 1 through 6.
ARTICLE 9.2: GENERAL PROVISIONS

Each Party shall apply the provisions of this Chapter and may, but is not required to, provide in its legislation for broader protection than that required by this Chapter, provided that such protection does not infringe the provisions of this Chapter.

The Parties reaffirm the rights and obligations provided for in the TRIPS Agreement, in the Convention on Biological Diversity (hereinafter referred to as “CBD”), and in any other multilateral agreement on intellectual property or in treaties administered by the World Organization of Intellectual Property (hereinafter referred to as "WIPO") of which the Parties are part. In this sense, no provision of this Chapter shall be detrimental to the provisions of said multilateral treaties.

Each Party, when formulating or modifying its national laws and regulations, may make use of the exceptions and flexibilities allowed by the multilateral treaties related to the protection of intellectual property to which the Parties are party.

A Party shall grant to the nationals of the other Party treatment no less favorable than that which it grants to its own nationals. Exceptions to this obligation must be in accordance with the relevant provisions referred to in Articles 3 and 5 of the TRIPS Agreement.

With respect to the protection and enforcement of the intellectual property rights referred to in this Chapter, any advantage, favour, privilege or immunity granted by one Party to the nationals of any other country shall be granted immediately and without conditions to the nationals of the other. Part. Exceptions to this obligation must be in accordance with the relevant provisions referred to in Articles 4 and 5 of the TRIPS Agreement.

Nothing in this Chapter shall prevent a Party from adopting the necessary measures to prevent the abuse of intellectual property rights by their holders, or the resort to practices that unreasonably limit trade, or are detrimental to the international transfer of technology. . Likewise, nothing in this Chapter shall be construed as diminishing the protections that the Parties agree or have agreed to for the benefit of the conservation and sustainable use of biodiversity and associated traditional knowledge, nor shall it prevent the Parties from adopting or maintaining measures to this end.

The Parties recognize the importance of the development of multilateral regulations in the field of intellectual property, for which reason they may agree on the exchange of opinions of experts in activities related to existing or future international agreements on Intellectual Property Rights and any other matter related to Property Rights. Intellectual, as agreed by the Parties.
ARTICLE 9.3: MARKS

The Parties will protect trademarks in accordance with the TRIPS Agreement.

Article 6 bis of the Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property shall apply, mutatis mutandis, to goods or services that are not identical or similar to those identified by a trademark that the competent authority of the country of registration or use deems as well-known, regardless of whether it is registered or not, provided that the use of such mark in connection with those goods or services indicates a connection between those goods or services and the owner of the mark, and provided that the interests of the owner of the brand could be injured by such use.

To determine if a mark is well-known 1, neither Party shall require that the reputation of the mark extends beyond the sector of the public that normally deals with the relevant goods or services. For greater certainty, the sector of the public that normally deals with the relevant goods or services is determined in accordance with the legislation of each Party.

Each Party shall provide a system for the registration of trademarks, which shall provide:

the written notification to the applicant indicating the reasons for the refusal to register the trademark. If your national legislation allows it, notifications may be made by electronic means;

an opportunity for interested parties to oppose a trademark registration application or to request the nullity of the trademark after it has been registered;

that the decisions in the registration and annulment procedures be motivated and in writing; and

the opportunity for interested parties to challenge administratively or judicially, as established by the legislation of each Party, the decisions issued in the trademark registration and nullity procedures.

Each Party shall use its best efforts to establish a system for the electronic application, electronic processing, registration and maintenance of trademarks 2, and to establish a publicly available electronic database, including an online database of trademark applications and registrations.

Each Party shall provide that the applications for registration, the publications of said applications and the registrations indicate the goods and services by their names, grouped according to the classes of the classification established by the Nice Agreement Concerning the International Classification of Goods and Services for the Registration of Trademarks , according to its revisions and amendments (hereinafter referred to as the "Nice Classification").

Products or services may not be considered as similar to each other, solely because, in some registry or publication, they appear in the same class of the Nice Classification. In the same way, each Party shall establish that the products or services may not be considered different from each other solely because, in any registry or publication, they appear in different classes of the Nice Classification.
ARTICLE 9.4: GEOGRAPHICAL INDICATIONS

The Parties to this Agreement shall ensure in their national legislation adequate and effective means to protect geographical indications, including appellations of origin.

For the purposes of this Agreement, "geographical indications" are indications that identify a product as originating in the territory of a Party, or of a region or locality of that territory, when a certain quality, reputation, or other characteristic of the product is fundamentally attributable to its origin. geographical.

Nothing in the provisions of this Article shall prevent the Parties from maintaining or adopting in their legislation measures related to homonymous geographical indications, provided that such measures comply with the provisions of numeral 3 of Article 23 of the TRIPS Agreement.

The names that appear in Section A of Annex 9-A are protected geographical indications in Colombia, in accordance with the provisions of paragraph 1 of Article 22 of the TRIPS Agreement. Costa Rica, prior compliance with the requirements and the internal procedure, and at the request of the interested parties, will recognize the geographical indications of Colombia included in Annex 9-A in accordance with its legislation.

The names that appear in Section B of Annex 9-A are protected geographical indications in Costa Rica, in accordance with the provisions of paragraph 1 of Article 22 of the TRIPS Agreement. Colombia, prior compliance with the requirements and the internal procedure, and at the request of the interested parties, will recognize in accordance with its legislation the geographical indications of Costa Rica included in Annex 9-A.

The Parties shall exchange information about the protections granted under paragraphs 4 and 5, through the contact points established in Article 19.1 (Contact Points).

The Parties shall protect the geographical indications, including appellations of origin, of the other Party registered and/or protected in their respective territories in accordance with the provisions of paragraphs 4, 5 and 6. Consequently, and without prejudice to the provisions of paragraph 3, the Parties shall not allow the import, manufacture or sale of products identified under protected geographical indications, including appellations of origin, by unauthorized third parties.

The use of geographical indications, including designations of origin, recognized and protected in the territory of a Party in relation to any type of product coming from the territory of said Party, is reserved exclusively for authorized and other authorized producers, manufacturers and artisans. in accordance with their national legislation, which have their production or manufacturing establishments in the locality or region of the Party designated or evoked by said geographical indication.

The Parties shall protect geographical indications against any use of a false or misleading indication likely to mislead the public, deceive or confuse the source, origin, nature or essential characteristics of the product, and any other practice that may mislead the consumer as to the true origin of the product.

The Parties may grant the agreed protection to other geographical indications, including appellations of origin, protected in the Parties. For this purpose, the interested Party shall notify the other Party regarding said protection, after which the provisions of paragraphs 4, 5 and 6 shall be followed.
ARTICLE 9.5: MEASURES RELATED TO THE PROTECTION OF BIODIVERSITY AND TRADITIONAL KNOWLEDGE

The Parties acknowledge and reaffirm their rights and obligations under the CBD relating to the Parties' sovereignty over their natural resources and the authority to determine access to biological and genetic resources and their derivative products, on mutually agreed terms, in accordance with the principles and provisions contained in relevant national and international standards. The Parties acknowledge paragraph 19 of the Doha Ministerial Declaration, adopted on November 14, 2001, on the relationship between the TRIPS Agreement and the CBD.

The Parties recognize the importance and value of the knowledge, innovations and practices of indigenous and local communities 3, as well as their past, present and future contribution to the conservation and sustainable use of biological and genetic resources and their derivative products, and in general, the contribution of the traditional knowledge of such communities to culture and economic development and social of nations. Each Party, in accordance with its legislation, reiterates its commitment to respect, preserve and maintain the traditional knowledge, innovations and practices of indigenous and local communities in the territories of the Parties.

Access to biological and genetic resources and their derivative products will be conditional on the prior informed consent of the Party that is the country of origin, on mutually agreed terms. Likewise, access to the traditional knowledge of indigenous and local communities associated with said resources will be conditioned to the prior informed consent of the holders or possessors, as appropriate, of said knowledge, in mutually agreed terms. Both cases will be subject to the provisions of the legislation of each Party.

The Parties shall take measures to ensure a fair and equitable sharing of benefits arising from the use of biological and genetic resources and derived products and the traditional knowledge of indigenous and local communities.

Each Party shall take political, legal and administrative measures, in order to ensure full compliance with the conditions of access to biological and genetic resources of biodiversity and associated traditional knowledge.

Any intellectual property right that is generated from the use of biological and genetic resources and their derived products, and/or traditional knowledge of indigenous and local communities, of which a Party is the country of origin, must observe compliance with the specific national and international standards on the matter.

In accordance with their legislation, the Parties will require that in patent applications developed from biological and genetic resources and/or associated traditional knowledge, of which they are the country of origin, legal access to said resources or knowledge is demonstrated, as well as such as the disclosure of the origin of the resource and/or accessed traditional knowledge.

The Parties may, through their competent national authorities, exchange information related to biodiversity and/or traditional knowledge and documented information related to biological and genetic resources and their derivatives, or if applicable, the traditional knowledge of their indigenous communities. and local, in order to serve as support in the evaluation of patents.

The Parties agree, at the request of any of them, to collaborate in the provision of public information that they have at their disposal for the investigation and monitoring of illegal access to genetic resources and/or knowledge, innovations and traditional practices in their territories.

The Parties will cooperate, on the basis of mutually agreed terms, with the exchange of information and experiences in relation to access to biological and genetic resources and their derivatives, and/or associated traditional knowledge.
ARTICLE 9.6: COPYRIGHT AND RELATED RIGHTS

The Parties will recognize the existing rights and obligations under the Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works ; of the Rome Convention on the Protection of Artists, Interpreters or Performers, Producers of Phonograms and Broadcasting Organizations ; of the WIPO Copyright Treaty , and the WIPO Performance and Phonograms Treaty 4.

In accordance with the international conventions indicated in paragraph 1 and with its national legislation, each Party shall grant adequate and effective protection to the authors of literary and artistic works and to performers, producers of phonograms and broadcasting organizations, in their artistic interpretations and performances, phonograms and broadcasts, respectively.

Regardless of the economic rights of the author, and even after the transfer of these rights, the author will retain, at least, the right to claim paternity of the work and to oppose any distortion, mutilation or other modification of it, or to any attack on it, which causes damage to their honor or reputation.

The rights recognized to the author in accordance with paragraph 3, will be maintained after his death, at least until the extinction of his patrimonial rights, and exercised by the persons or institutions to which the legislation of the country in which protection is claimed recognize rights.

The rights granted under paragraphs 3 and 4 shall be granted, mutatis mutandis , to performers in respect of their live performances or fixed performances.

The Parties shall cooperate, through dialogue, on the protection of rights in favor of audiovisual performers, including the direct or indirect use for broadcasting or communication to the public of performances fixed in audiovisual fixations. 5

The Parties may provide in their legislation limitations and exceptions to copyright and related rights, only in certain cases that do not violate the normal exploitation of the work, nor cause unjustified prejudice to the legitimate interests of the rights holder.
ARTICLE 9.7: OBSERVANCE

Without prejudice to the rights and obligations established under the TRIPS Agreement, particularly Part III, the Parties may develop in their legislation, measures, procedures and resources necessary to ensure compliance with intellectual property rights.

The Parties will adopt procedures that allow the holder of the right, who has valid reasons to suspect that the import, export, or transit of counterfeit trademark merchandise or pirated merchandise that infringes copyright 6 is being prepared ., submit to the competent authorities, a request or complaint, according to the legislation of each Party, so that the customs authorities suspend the clearance of such merchandise.

Each Party shall provide that any right holder initiating the procedure provided for in paragraph 2 shall be required to submit adequate evidence to the satisfaction of the competent authorities that under the law of the importing country there is a presumption of infringement of the intellectual property right of the right holder; and that it provides sufficient information on the goods that is reasonably known to the right holder so that they can be easily recognized by their competent authorities. The requirement to provide sufficient information shall not unreasonably deter recourse to such procedures.

Each Party shall provide that the competent authorities have the power to require the right holder, who initiates the procedure referred to in paragraph 2, to provide a bond or equivalent guarantee sufficient to protect the defendant and the competent authorities and to prevent abuse. The bond or equivalent guarantee shall not unduly deter access to such procedures.

When its competent authorities determine that the goods are counterfeit or pirated, the Party shall grant its competent authorities the power to inform the right holder, the name and address of the consignor, the importer and the consignee, as well as the quantity of the goods. concerned.

Each Party shall provide that the competent authorities are empowered to initiate border measures ex officio, without the need for a formal request from the right holder or a third party, when there are reasons to believe or suspect that the goods being imported, exported or in transit are counterfeit or pirated.
ARTICLE 9.8: COOPERATION AND SCIENCE, TECHNOLOGY AND INNOVATION

The Parties will exchange information and material in education and dissemination projects regarding the use of intellectual property rights, in accordance with their national laws, regulations and policies, with a view to:

improve and strengthen intellectual property administrative systems to promote efficient registration of intellectual property rights;

stimulate the creation and development of intellectual property within the territory of the Parties, particularly small inventors and creators, as well as micro, small and medium-sized companies;

promote dialogue and cooperation in relation to science, technology, entrepreneurship and innovation; and

other matters of mutual interest on intellectual property rights.

The Parties recognize the importance of promoting research, technological development, entrepreneurship and innovation, as well as the importance of disseminating technological information and creating and strengthening their technological capabilities; to this end, they will cooperate in said areas taking into consideration their resources.

The Parties will promote the establishment of incentives for research, innovation, entrepreneurship, transfer and dissemination of technology between the Parties, directed, among others, at companies, higher education institutions and research and technological development centers.

Cooperation activities in science, technology and innovation may take, among others, the following forms:

participation in joint education, research, technological development and innovation projects;

visits and exchanges of scientists and technical experts, as well as public, academic or private specialists;

joint organization of scientific seminars, congresses, workshops and symposia, as well as participation of experts in these activities;

promotion of scientific networks and training of researchers;

concerted actions for the dissemination of results and the exchange of experiences around joint science technology and innovation projects and for their coordination;

exchange and loan of equipment and materials, including sharing of advanced equipment;

exchange of information on procedures, laws, regulations and programs related to cooperative activities carried out in accordance with this Agreement, including information on science and technology policy; and

any other modality agreed by the Parties.

Likewise, the Parties may carry out cooperation activities regarding the exchange of:

information and experience on the legislative processes and legal frameworks related to intellectual property rights and relevant regulations for protection and enforcement;

experiences on the enforcement of intellectual property rights;

personnel and their training in the offices related to intellectual property rights;

information and institutional cooperation on intellectual property policies and developments;

information and experience on policies and practices in terms of promoting the development of the handicrafts sector; and

Experience in intellectual property management and knowledge management in higher education institutions and research centers.

Each Party designates the following entities as contact entities, responsible for compliance with the objectives of this Article, and for facilitating the development of collaboration and cooperation projects in research, innovation and technological development:

in the case of Colombia, to the Ministry of Commerce, Industry and Tourism in coordination with the Administrative Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (COLCIENCIAS); and

in the case of Costa Rica, to the Ministry of Foreign Trade , in coordination with the Ministry of Justice and Peace and the Ministry of Science, Technology and Telecommunications ,

or their successors.

CHAPTER 10
PUBLIC PROCUREMENT

ARTICLE 10.1: SCOPE OF APPLICATION

Chapter Application

This Chapter applies to any measure taken by a Party relating to covered government procurement.

For the purposes of this Chapter, covered public procurement means a public procurement of goods, services, or both:

not procured with a view to commercial sale or resale, or with a view to use in the production or supply of goods or services for commercial sale or resale;

made through any contractual means, including purchase, lease, with or without a purchase option; and public works concession contracts;

for which the value, as estimated in accordance with paragraph 4, is equal to or exceeds the corresponding threshold value stipulated in Annex 10-A;

that is carried out by a contracting entity; and

that is not expressly excluded from coverage.

This Chapter does not apply to:

non-contractual agreements or any form of assistance that a Party, including its contracting entities, grants, including cooperation agreements, donations, loans, subsidies, capital transfers, guarantees and fiscal incentives;

the contracting or acquisition of services of fiscal agencies or deposit services, liquidation and administration services for regulated financial institutions, or services related to the sale, redemption and distribution of public debt, including loans and government bonds and other securities. For greater certainty, this Chapter does not apply to public procurement of banking, financial or specialized services referring to the following activities:

public debt; either

public debt management;

procurement financed by donations, loans or other forms of international assistance;

the hiring of public employees and measures related to employment;

contracts made by a State entity or company to another government entity or company of that Party;

the acquisition or lease of land, existing real estate or other immovable property or rights thereon;

purchases made under exceptionally favorable conditions that only occur for a very short period, such as extraordinary disposals made by companies that are not normally suppliers or the disposal of assets of companies in liquidation or under judicial administration. For the purposes of this paragraph (g), the provisions of Article 10.11.3 shall apply; and

contracts made with the specific purpose of providing assistance abroad.

Assessment
In estimating the value of a procurement for the purpose of determining whether it is a covered procurement, a procuring entity:

shall not divide a procurement into separate procurements, or use a particular method to estimate the value of the procurement for the purpose of evading the application of this Chapter;

must take into account all forms of remuneration, including premiums, quotas, fees, commissions, interest, other income streams that could be stipulated in public contracting, and when public contracting stipulates the possibility of option clauses, the total maximum value public procurement, including optional purchases; and

shall, where procurement is to be carried out in multiple parts, and results in the award of contracts at the same time or in a given period to one or more suppliers, base its calculation on the maximum total value of the procurement throughout the period of its validity.

No contracting entity may prepare, design, structure or divide a public procurement, in order to evade the obligations of this Chapter.

Nothing in this Chapter shall prevent a Party from developing new procurement policies, procedures, or contractual means, provided they are consistent with this Chapter.
ARTICLE 10.2: SECURITY AND GENERAL EXCEPTIONS

Nothing in this Chapter shall be construed to prevent a Party from taking any action or refraining from disclosing any information deemed necessary for the protection of its essential interests for national security or national defense.

Provided that such measures are not applied in a manner that constitutes a means of arbitrary or unjustifiable discrimination between the Parties, where the same conditions exist or a disguised restriction on trade between the Parties, nothing in this Chapter shall be construed to prevent a Party adopts or maintains measures:

necessary to protect morality, order or public safety;

necessary to protect human, animal or plant life or health;

necessary to protect intellectual property; either

related to the goods or services of disabled people, charitable institutions or prison labor.

The Parties understand that paragraph 2(b) includes environmental measures necessary to protect human, animal or plant life or health.
ARTICLE 10.3: GENERAL PRINCIPLES

National Treatment and Non-Discrimination
With respect to any measure covered by this Chapter, each Party shall immediately and unconditionally accord to the goods and services of the other Party, and to the suppliers of the other Party who offer such goods or services, treatment no less favorable than the most favorable treatment granted by said Party to its own goods, services and suppliers.

With respect to any measure covered by this Chapter, a Party may not:

treat a locally established supplier less favorably than another locally established supplier, by reason of its degree of foreign affiliation or ownership; either

discriminate against a locally established supplier on the basis that the goods or services offered by said supplier for a particular public procurement are goods or services of the other Party.
Execution of Public Procurement
A procuring entity shall conduct covered procurement in a transparent and impartial manner so that:

be consistent with this Chapter;

avoid conflicts of interest; and

prevent corrupt practices.

Rules of origin
Each Party shall apply to covered public procurement of goods or services imported from or supplied by the other Party, the rules of origin that it applies in the normal course of trade in such goods or services.
Special Compensatory Conditions
A procuring entity shall not seek, consider, impose or use special compensatory conditions at any stage of a covered procurement.
Non-Specific Measures for Public Procurement
Paragraphs 1 and 2 shall not apply to: customs duties and charges of any kind that are imposed on importation or that are related to it; the method of collection of said duties and charges; other import regulations or formalities; nor to measures affecting trade in services, other than measures governing covered government procurement.
ARTICLE 10.4: USE OF ELECTRONIC MEANS IN PUBLIC PROCUREMENT

The Parties recognize the need and importance of the use of electronic means for the dissemination of information related to covered public procurement.

In order to facilitate business opportunities for suppliers of the other Party under this Chapter, each Party will maintain or make best efforts to adopt a single electronic point of entry for the purpose of allowing access to complete information on opportunities in matter of public contracting in its territory, as well as on the measures related to contracting, especially those indicated in Articles 10.5, 10.6.1, 10.6.3, 10.9.1, 10.9.7 and 10.14.2.

When covered public procurement is carried out through electronic means, a procuring entity must:

ensure that public procurement is carried out using information technology systems and software, including those related to authentication and encryption of information, that are generally accessible and compatible with other information technology systems and software generally accessible; and

maintain mechanisms that ensure the integrity of requests for participation and offers, including determining the moment of receipt and preventing inappropriate access.
ARTICLE 10.5: PUBLICATION OF INFORMATION ON PUBLIC PROCUREMENT

Each part:
will timely publish all the regulations of general application with respect to public contracting covered, and any modification to said regulations, in an electronic medium listed in Annex 10-A; and

at the request of the other Party, it will provide an explanation regarding said information.
ARTICLE 10.6: PUBLICATION OF NOTICES

Notice of Future Hiring
For each covered procurement, a procuring entity shall publish in a timely manner a notice inviting suppliers to bid, or where appropriate, a request to participate in the procurement, except in the circumstances described in Article 10.11.2. Said notice will be published in one of the electronic media or in print media of wide circulation and easily accessible to the public listed in Exhibit 10-C, and each such notice will be accessible to the public during the entire period established for the bidding. of the respective public procurement.

Each future hiring notice must include:

the description of the future public procurement, including the nature of the goods or services subject to the procurement;

the contracting method to be used and whether it will involve negotiation;

any conditions that suppliers must satisfy to participate in public procurement, including requirements regarding specific documents or certifications that suppliers must present in connection with their participation, unless such requirements are included in the tender documents that are made available available to all interested suppliers, at the same time that the notice of the planned contracting is made;

the name and address of the contracting entity and other information necessary to contact it and obtain all the relevant documentation related to public procurement, as well as its cost and payment terms, when applicable;

when applicable, the address and final date for the submission of applications for participation in public procurement;

the address and final date for the presentation of offers;

the delivery dates of the goods or services to be contracted, or the duration of the contract; and

an indication that public procurement is covered by this Chapter.

Notice on Hiring Plans
Each Party shall encourage its procuring entities to publish in electronic or printed media, as early as possible in each fiscal year, a notice regarding its future procurement plans. Such notices must include the object or category of goods and services to be contracted and the estimated period in which the public contracting will take place.
ARTICLE 10.7: CONDITIONS OF PARTICIPATION

When establishing the conditions of participation, a contracting entity:

You must limit these conditions to those that are essential to ensure that the supplier has the legal and financial capacities, and the commercial and technical skills, to comply with the technical requirements and specifications of public procurement on the basis of the commercial activities of the supplier, carried out both inside and outside the territory of the Party of the procuring entity;

will base its decision solely on the conditions that the procuring entity has specified in advance in the procurement notices or documents;

shall not impose as a condition that, for a supplier to participate in a public procurement or be awarded a contract, that the supplier has previously been awarded one or more contracts by a procuring entity of the Party in question;

may require relevant prior experience when essential to meet public procurement requirements; and

It will allow all national suppliers and suppliers of the other Party, who have satisfied the conditions for participation, to be recognized as qualified and can participate in public procurement.

When there is evidence to justify it, a Party, including its procuring entities, may exclude a supplier from a public procurement for reasons such as:

bankruptcy;

false statements;

significant or persistent deficiencies in the fulfillment of any substantive requirement or obligation derived from one or more previous contracts;

final sentences for felonies or other serious violations;

lack of professional ethics or acts or omissions that call into question the business integrity of the supplier; either

I don't pay taxes.

The contracting entities shall not adopt or apply a registration system or qualification procedure with the purpose or effect of creating unnecessary obstacles to the participation of suppliers of the other Party, in their respective public procurements.

A procuring entity shall promptly inform any supplier that has applied for qualification of its decision with respect to that application. When a procuring entity rejects a request for qualification or ceases to recognize a supplier as one that meets the conditions for participation, the procuring entity shall promptly inform the supplier, and upon request, provide a timely written explanation of the reasons for the qualification. the entity's decision.
ARTICLE 10.8: REGISTRATION AND QUALIFICATION OF SUPPLIERS

Registration systems and qualification procedures
The Parties, including their contracting entities, may maintain a supplier registration system, where interested suppliers register and provide certain information.

Each Party shall ensure that its contracting entities:

make efforts to minimize the differences between their respective qualification procedures;

when maintaining systems of record, make efforts to minimize differences between systems.

The Parties, including their procuring entities, shall not adopt or apply any registration system or qualification procedures with the purpose of creating unnecessary obstacles to the participation of suppliers of the other Party in their procurement, or having that effect.
ARTICLE 10.9: INFORMATION ON FUTURE RECRUITMENTS

Procurement Documents
A contracting entity will timely provide suppliers interested in participating in a public contracting, contracting documents that include all the necessary information that allows them to prepare and present adequate offers, in accordance with Annex 10-B. When a contracting entity does not publish the contracting documents by electronic means accessible to all interested suppliers, it must make them available to them without delay in written form.

A procuring entity shall promptly respond to any reasonable request for information from vendors participating in a covered procurement, provided that the entity does not make available information that would give the requesting vendor an advantage over its competitors in the specific procurement.

Technical specifications
A procuring entity shall not prepare, adopt, or apply any technical specification, or require any conformity assessment procedure, which has the purpose or effect of creating unnecessary obstacles to trade between the Parties.

When establishing any technical specifications for the goods or services to be procured, a procuring entity shall, where appropriate:

state the technical specification in terms of performance and functional requirements, rather than descriptive or design characteristics; and

base the technical specification on international standards, where applicable, or otherwise on national technical regulations, recognized national standards or building codes.

A procuring entity shall not prescribe technical specifications that require or refer to a certain brand or trade name, patent, copyright, design, type, specific origin, producer or supplier, unless there is no other sufficiently precise or intelligible way of describing the public procurement requirements, and provided that, in such cases, expressions such as "or equivalent" are also included in the procurement documents.

A procuring entity shall not solicit or accept, in a manner that may have the effect of preventing competition, advice that could be used in preparing or adopting any technical specification for a specific procurement from any person who may have a commercial interest in that procurement. .

For greater certainty, this Article is not intended to prevent a procuring entity from preparing, adopting, or applying technical specifications to promote the conservation of natural resources or protect the environment.

Modifications
When, in the course of a covered procurement, a procuring entity modifies the criteria or technical requirements set forth in a procurement notice or document provided to participating suppliers, or modifies a procurement notice or document, it shall transmit such modifications in writing. :

to all participating providers at the time the information is changed, if the identification of such providers is known, and in all other cases, in the same manner as the original information was transmitted; and

in sufficient time to allow suppliers to amend and resubmit their amended bids, as appropriate.
ARTICLE 10.10: DEADLINES

A procuring entity will provide suppliers with sufficient time to submit applications to participate in a procurement and to prepare and submit suitable bids, taking into account the nature and complexity of the procurement. A contracting entity will grant a term of not less than 40 days from the date on which the notice of future contracting is published and the final date for the presentation of offers.

Notwithstanding the provisions of paragraph 1, a procuring entity may establish a period of less than 40 days, but in no case less than 10 days, in the following circumstances:

where the procuring entity has published a separate notice containing a description of the procurement, approximate deadlines for submission of bids or, where appropriate, conditions for participation in a procurement, and the address where documentation relating to the procurement could be obtained , with at least 40 days and no more than 12 months in advance;

in the case of a new, second or subsequent publication of notices for public procurement of a recurring nature;

when an urgent situation duly justified by a contracting entity makes it impractical to comply with the term stipulated in paragraph 1; either

when the contracting entity acquires merchandise or commercial services.

A Party may provide that a procuring entity may reduce the period for submitting offers established in paragraph 1 by five days for each of the following circumstances:

when the notice of future recruitment is published by electronic means;

when all procurement documents that are made available to the public by electronic means are published from the date of publication of the future procurement notice; and

if the entity accepts offers by electronic means.

The use of this paragraph, in conjunction with paragraph 2, may not result in the reduction of the bidding deadlines established in paragraph 1 to less than 10 days from the date of publication of the notice of future procurement.
ARTICLE 10.11: PROCUREMENT PROCEDURES

Open Bidding

A procuring entity will award its contracts through open bidding procedures.
Direct hire
As long as this provision is not used to prevent competition between suppliers or in a manner that discriminates against suppliers of the other Party, or protects domestic suppliers, a procuring entity may use a direct procurement procedure and consider not application of Articles 10.6 to 10.10, 10.13, 10.14.1 to 10.14.4, 10.14.6 and 10.15.1, only in the following circumstances:

provided that the requirements of the procurement documents are not substantially modified, when:

no offer was submitted or no supplier has requested to participate;

no offer that complied with the essential requirements of the bidding documents was submitted;

no provider complied with the conditions of participation; either

there has been collusion in the submission of offers;

where the goods or services can only be supplied by a particular supplier and there is no reasonable alternative or substitute good or service due to any of the following reasons:

the requirement is for the realization of a work of art;

the protection of patents, copyrights or other exclusive intellectual property rights; either

due to the absence of competition for technical reasons, as in the case of contracting services intuitu personae ;

for additional supplies or services from the initial supplier of goods or services that were not included in the initial public procurement, when the change of supplier of those additional goods or services:

cannot be done for economic or technical reasons such as requirements for interchangeability or compatibility with existing equipment, software, services or facilities that are the subject of the initial contract; and

would cause significant inconvenience or substantial duplication of costs to the procuring entity,

In the case of construction services, the total value of the contracts awarded for said additional services will not exceed 50 percent of the amount of the initial contract, as long as said services have been contemplated in the objectives contained in the contracting documents. and have become necessary to complete the work due to unforeseen reasons;

to the extent that it is strictly necessary, when for reasons of extreme urgency caused by unforeseen events for the procuring entity, the goods or services cannot be obtained on time through open bidding procedures, and the use of such procedures could result in prejudice serious for the contracting entity;

for merchandise acquisitions made in a commodity market;

when a procuring entity acquires a prototype or first good in limited quantity or contracts for a service that is developed upon request in the course of, and for, a particular research, experiment, study, or original development contract; either

when a contract is awarded to the winner of a design competition, provided that:

the contest has been organized in a manner that is consistent with the principles of this Chapter, in particular with respect to the publication of the notice of future public procurement; and

the participants are qualified or evaluated by a jury or independent body with a view to entering into a design contract that is awarded to a winner.

A procuring entity shall maintain records or prepare a written report for each contract awarded in accordance with Article 10.15.3. When a Party prepares written reports in accordance with this paragraph, these shall include the name of the procuring entity, the value and nature of the contracted goods or services, and a justification indicating the circumstances and conditions described in paragraph 2 that justify the use of direct contracting. When a Party maintains records, these shall indicate the circumstances and conditions described in paragraph 2 that justify the use of other procurement procedures.
ARTICLE 10.12: ELECTRONIC AUCTIONS

When a contracting entity wants to carry out a covered public procurement using an electronic auction, the contracting entity will provide each participant, before the electronic auction starts, the following information:
The automatic evaluation method, including the mathematical formula, which is based on the evaluation criteria established in the procurement documents and which will be used in the automatic classification or reclassification during the auction;

the results of any initial evaluation of the elements of your offer when the contract is awarded on the basis of the most advantageous offer; and

any other pertinent information about the conduct of the auction.
ARTICLE 10.13. TALKS

A Party may provide that its contracting entities enter into negotiations:

when in the context of a contract in which such intention has been indicated in the notice of future contract; either

if the evaluation carried out shows that no offer is clearly the most advantageous according to the specific evaluation criteria established in the notice of future contracting or in the bidding documents.

In the course of negotiations, contracting entities will not discriminate between participating providers.

The contracting entities:

they will ensure that any elimination of suppliers participating in the negotiations is carried out in accordance with the evaluation criteria established in the notice of future procurement or in the bidding documents; and

at the end of the negotiations, they will grant all the participants, who have not been eliminated, the same maximum term to present new or revised offers.
ARTICLE 10.14: OPENING OF BIDS AND AWARD OF CONTRACTS

Treatment of Offers
A contracting entity will receive and process all offers under procedures that guarantee the equality and impartiality of the public contracting process.

A contracting entity will give confidential treatment to offers, at least until their opening. In particular, the contracting entity will avoid providing information to suppliers that could prejudice fair competition between suppliers.

Where a procuring entity provides suppliers the opportunity to correct any inadvertent errors of form between the bid opening period and contract award, the procuring entity shall provide the same opportunity to all participating suppliers.

Contract Award

A procuring entity will require that, in order to be considered for an award, the offer:

is submitted in writing, by a provider that meets all the conditions of participation; and

at the time of opening, it must be in accordance with the essential requirements specified in the notices and contract documents.

Unless a procuring entity determines that the award of a contract is against the public interest, the procuring entity will award the contract to the supplier that the procuring entity has determined meets the conditions for participation and is fully capable of performing the contract. and, whose offer is considered the most advantageous based solely on the requirements and the evaluation criteria specified in the notices and contracting documents, or when the price is the only evaluation criterion, the one with the lowest price.

When a contracting entity receives an offer whose price is abnormally lower than the prices of the other offers submitted, the entity may verify with the supplier if it complies with the conditions of participation and if it has the capacity to comply with the provisions of the contract.

A procuring entity may not cancel a public procurement or terminate or modify a contract that has been awarded in order to evade this Chapter.
ARTICLE 10.15: TRANSPARENCY OF INFORMATION ON PUBLIC PROCUREMENT

Information to be Provided to Suppliers
A procuring entity will promptly inform participating providers of its decision to award a contract, and upon request, will do so in writing. Subject to the provisions of Article 10.16, a procuring entity shall, upon request, provide the supplier whose bid has not been chosen, the reasons for such decision and the relative advantages of the winning bid.

Publication of Award Information

Not later than 60 days after an award, a procuring entity shall publish in a widely disseminated electronic or print media that is readily accessible to the public, a notice that includes, at a minimum, the following information about the contract award:

the name of the contracting entity;

a description of the contracted goods or services;

the award date;

the name of the supplier to whom the contract was awarded;

the value of the contract; and

the type of procurement method used and, in cases where direct procurement was used, an indication of the circumstances that justified the use of such procedure in accordance with Article 10.11.2.


Registry mantenance
A procuring entity shall maintain reports or records of the procurement procedures related to covered procurements, including the reports referred to in Article 10.11.3, and shall maintain such reports or records for a period of at least three years from the the date of award of a contract.
ARTICLE 10.16: DISCLOSURE OF INFORMATION

Delivery of Information to the other Party
At the request of one Party, the other Party shall promptly provide the information necessary to determine whether a public procurement has been carried out fairly, impartially and in accordance with this Chapter. This information will include information about the features and relative advantages of the winning bid. When the disclosure of this information could prejudice fair competition, the Party that receives the information will not disclose it to any supplier, unless it obtains the consent of the Party that provided that information, after consulting with it.

Non-Disclosure of Information
No Party, including its contracting entities, authorities or review bodies, may disclose information that the person who provided it has designated as confidential, in accordance with its law, except with the authorization of said person.

Nothing in this Chapter shall be construed to require a Party, including its procuring entities, authorities and review bodies, to disclose confidential information under this Chapter if such disclosure would:

impede compliance with the law;

harm fair competition between suppliers;

harm the legitimate business interests of individuals, including the protection of intellectual property; either

otherwise be contrary to the public interest.
ARTICLE 10.17: NATIONAL REVIEW PROCEDURES FOR THE LODGING OF APPEALS

Each Party shall ensure that its procuring entities consider, in an impartial and timely manner, any claim that its suppliers have regarding an allegation of non-compliance with this Chapter that arises in the context of a covered government procurement in which they have or have had an interest. . Each Party shall encourage its suppliers to seek clarification from their procuring entities through consultation with a view to facilitating the resolution of any such complaint.

Each Party shall provide an administrative or judicial review procedure that is timely, effective, transparent, and non-discriminatory, in accordance with the principle of due process, through which a supplier may file an appeal alleging a breach of this Chapter or, when the supplier is not entitled to directly allege a violation of the Chapter under the law of a Party, the lack of compliance with the measures aimed at the implementation of the Chapter adopted by a Party, arising in the context of public procurement covered in those that the supplier has or has had an interest in.

Each Party shall establish or designate at least one impartial administrative or judicial authority, independent of its procuring entities, to receive and review a challenge filed by a supplier within a covered public procurement, and issue the relevant resolutions and recommendations.

When an entity other than the authority referred to in paragraph 3 initially reviews a challenge, the Party shall ensure that the supplier can appeal the initial decision before an impartial administrative or judicial authority, which is independent of the procuring entity whose procurement is object of the challenge.

Each Party shall adopt or maintain procedures that establish:

quick provisional measures to preserve the supplier's ability to participate in public procurement, which are applied by the contracting entity or by the impartial authority referred to in paragraph 3. Such measures may have the effect of suspending the procurement process. The procedures may provide for the possibility that prevailing unfavorable consequences for the interests concerned, including the public interest, will be taken into account when deciding whether to apply those measures. The reason why such measures are not adopted shall be recorded in writing; and

when a review body has determined the existence of a non-compliance referred to in paragraph 2, corrective measures or compensation for the loss or damage suffered, in accordance with the legislation of each Party.
ARTICLE 10.18: MODIFICATIONS AND RECTIFICATIONS TO THE COVERAGE

When a Party modifies its procurement coverage under this Chapter, the Party:

notify the other Party in writing; and

shall include in the notification a proposal for the appropriate compensatory adjustments to the other Party to maintain a level of coverage comparable to that existing prior to the modification.

Notwithstanding paragraph 1(b), a Party need not provide compensatory adjustments when:

the modification in question is a minor amendment or a rectification of a purely formal nature; either

the proposed amendment covers an entity over which the Party has effectively eliminated control or influence.

If the other Party does not agree that:

a proposed adjustment within the scope of paragraph 1(b) is adequate to maintain a comparable level of mutually agreed coverage;

the proposed modification is a minor amendment or rectification within the scope of paragraph 2(a); either

the proposed modification covers a procuring entity over which the Party has effectively eliminated its control or influence under the scope of paragraph 2(b),

you must object in writing within 30 days of receipt of the notice referred to in paragraph 1 or agreement to the proposed change or modification will be deemed to have been reached even for purposes of Chapter 18 (Dispute Resolution).

When the Parties agree on the proposed modification, rectification or amendment, including when a Party has not objected within 30 days under the scope of paragraph 3, the Parties shall give effect to the agreement by immediately modifying Annex 10-A through of the Commission.
ARTICLE 10.19: INTEGRITY IN PUBLIC PROCUREMENT PRACTICES

Each Party shall establish or maintain procedures to declare ineligible to participate in the Party's procurement, either indefinitely or for a set period, suppliers that the Party determines have engaged in illegal or fraudulent procurement-related activities. Upon request by the other Party, the Party receiving the request will identify providers determined to be ineligible under these procedures and, where appropriate, will exchange information regarding these providers or fraudulent or illegal activity.

ARTICLE 10.20: ADDITIONAL NEGOTIATIONS

At the request of a Party, the other Party may consider additional negotiations for the purpose of expanding the scope and coverage of this Chapter. If, as a consequence of these negotiations, the Parties agree to modify the Annexes of this Chapter, the result will be submitted to the Public Procurement Committee established in Article 10.23 for its implementation.

ARTICLE 10.21: PARTICIPATION OF MICRO, SMALL AND MEDIUM ENTERPRISES

The Parties recognize the importance of the participation of micro, small and medium-sized enterprises in public procurement.

The Parties also recognize the importance of business alliances between suppliers of each Party, and in particular of micro, small and medium-sized companies, including joint participation in bidding procedures.
ARTICLE 10.22: COOPERATION

The Parties recognize the importance of cooperation as a way to achieve a better understanding of their respective public procurement systems, as well as better access to their respective markets, particularly for micro, small and medium-sized enterprises.

The Parties will make their best efforts to cooperate on issues such as:

exchange of experiences and information, including regulatory framework, best practices and statistics;

development and use of electronic means of information in public procurement systems;

training and technical assistance to providers in terms of access to the public procurement market; and

Institutional strengthening for compliance with this Chapter, including training for public officials.
ARTICLE 10.23: PUBLIC PROCUREMENT COMMITTEE

The Parties establish the Government Procurement Committee (hereinafter referred to as the “Committee”), composed of representatives of each Party.

The functions of the Committee will include, inter alia :

monitor the implementation and administration of this Chapter, including its use, and recommend the corresponding activities to the Commission;

report to the Commission on the implementation and administration of this Chapter, when applicable;

evaluate and monitor cooperation activities;

consider holding additional negotiations with the aim of expanding the coverage of this Chapter; and

deal with any other matter related to this Chapter.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will meet at least once a year, on the date and according to the previously agreed agenda. The first meeting of the Committee shall be held no later than one year after the date of entry into force of this Agreement. By mutual agreement, the Parties may hold extraordinary meetings.

The meetings may be held by any means agreed by the Parties. When they are face-to-face, they will be held alternately in the territory of each Party, and it will be up to the Host Party to organize the meeting.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will be permanent and will develop its working rules during its first meeting.

All decisions of the Committee must be taken by mutual agreement.
ARTICLE 10.24: DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this Chapter:

future procurement notice means a notice published by the procuring entity inviting interested suppliers to submit a request for participation, an offer, or both;

special compensatory conditions means any condition or commitment that promotes local development or improves the balance of payments accounts of a Party, such as local content requirements, technology licences, investment requirements, countertrade or similar requirements;

establish terminology, symbols, packaging, marking or labeling requirements, as they apply to a good or service;

commercial goods or services means goods or services of a kind that are generally sold or offered for sale in the commercial market to, and ordinarily purchased by, non-governmental purchasers for non-governmental purposes;

standard means a document approved by a recognized body, which provides, for common and repeated use, rules, guidelines or characteristics for goods, or services or related processes and production methods, compliance with which is not mandatory. It may also include or refer exclusively to terminology, symbols, packaging, marking or labeling requirements as they apply to a product, service, process or production method;

supplier means a person who provides or may provide goods or services to a procuring entity;

services includes construction services, unless otherwise specified;

construction service means a service whose object is the performance by any means of civil or construction works, based on Division 51 of the provisional version of the United Nations Central Product Classification (hereinafter referred to as “CPC”); and

CHAPTER 11
COMPETITION AND CONSUMER DEFENSE POLICY

ARTICLE 11.1: OBJECTIVES

The purpose of this Chapter is:
ensure that the benefits of trade liberalization under this Agreement are not undermined by anti-competitive practices;

promote cooperation between the Parties regarding the application of their respective competition laws; and

ensure compliance and defense of consumer rights.
ARTICLE 11.2: NATIONAL LEGISLATION AND AUTHORITIES

Each Party shall adopt or maintain national legislation that fully and effectively addresses anti-competitive practices and consumer protection.

Each Party shall establish or maintain an authority responsible for the application of its respective competition and consumer protection legislation.

Each Party will maintain its autonomy to develop and apply its respective legislation and policy on competition and consumer protection.

Each Party shall ensure that their respective national authorities act in accordance with the principles of transparency, non-discrimination and due process, in the application of their respective competition and consumer protection laws.
ARTICLE 11.3: COOPERATION

The Parties recognize the importance of cooperation and coordination between their respective national authorities to promote the effective application of their respective competition and consumer protection legislation.

Accordingly, the Parties will cooperate in matters related to the application of competition and consumer protection policy and legislation, including notification, information exchange and consultation, in accordance with Articles 11.4, 11.5 and 11.6, respectively.

The Parties, through their national authorities, may sign cooperation agreements or conventions in order to strengthen cooperation in matters related to the protection of competition and consumer protection.
ARTICLE 11.4: NOTIFICATIONS

The national authority of one Party shall notify the national authority of the other Party about enforcement activities of its competition and consumer protection laws, if it considers that these may affect important interests of the other Party.

As long as it is not contrary to the legislation of the Parties, nor does it affect any ongoing investigation, the notification will be made at an early stage of the administrative procedure. The national authority of the Party that carries out the enforcement activity of its competition or consumer defense legislation may, in its determinations, take into account the observations received from the other Party.
ARTICLE 11.5: EXCHANGE OF INFORMATION

In order to facilitate the effective application of their respective competition and consumer protection laws, the national authorities may exchange information at the request of one of them, as long as this is not contrary to their laws and does not affect any ongoing investigation.

ARTICLE 11.6: CONSULTATIONS

To foster understanding between the Parties or to address specific issues arising under this Chapter, each Party shall, at the request of the other Party, initiate consultations on the issues raised. The requesting Party shall indicate how the matter affects trade or investment between the Parties. The requested Party shall give the highest consideration to the concerns of the other Party.

ARTICLE 11.7: SETTLEMENT OF DISPUTES

Neither Party may resort to the dispute resolution procedures established in Chapter 12 (Investment) and Chapter 18 (Dispute Resolution), with respect to any matter derived from this Chapter.

ARTICLE 11.8: DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this Chapter:

national authorities are:
in the case of Colombia, the Superintendence of Industry and Commerce (SIC) and other authorities determined by the Law for the Defense of the Consumer ; and

in the case of Costa Rica, the Commission to Promote Competition and the National Consumer Commission of the Ministry of Economy, Industry and Commerce ,
or their successors;

competition law and consumer protection is:
in the case of Colombia, Law 155 of 1959, Law 1340 of 2009, Decree 2153 of 1992 and its regulations, Law 1480 of 2011 and other special regulations; and

in the case of Costa Rica, Law 7472 on Promotion of Competition and Effective Defense of the Consumer , and its modifications;

anti-competitive practices means:
any agreement, decision, recommendation, or concerted practice that has the purpose or effect of preventing, restricting, or distorting competition in accordance with the provisions of their respective competition laws;

the abuse of a dominant position or substantial market power in accordance with the provisions of their respective competition laws; and

concentrations of companies that significantly hinder effective competition in accordance with the provisions of their respective competition laws.
CHAPTER 12
INVESTMENT

SECTION A: SUBSTANTIVE OBLIGATIONS

ARTICLE 12.1: SCOPE OF APPLICATION1

This Chapter shall apply to measures adopted or maintained by a Party relating to:

investors of the other Party;

covered investments; and

in relation to Articles 12.6, 12.8 and 12.9, to all investments in the territory of the Party.

The obligations of a Party under this Section shall apply to a state enterprise or other person when it exercises a regulatory, administrative or other governmental authority delegated to it by that Party, such as the authority to expropriate, grant licences, approve business transactions or impose fees, rates or other charges.

For greater certainty, this Chapter does not bind a Party in relation to any act, fact or controversy that took place before the date of entry into force of this Agreement, even if its effects last after it.

For greater certainty, nothing in this Chapter shall be construed to impose an obligation on a Party to privatize any investment it owns or controls, or to prevent a Party from designating a monopoly.

Nothing contained in this Chapter shall oblige a Party to protect investments made with capital or assets derived from illegal activities, and shall not be construed to prevent a Party from adopting or maintaining measures aimed at preserving public order, compliance of its duties to maintain or restore international peace and security or the protection of its own essential security interests.

In the event of any inconsistency between this Chapter and another Chapter of this Agreement, the other Chapter shall prevail to the extent of the inconsistency.

A Party's requirement that a service provider of the other Party post a bond or other form of financial security as a condition of providing a

cross-border service, does not, in itself, make this Chapter applicable to measures adopted or maintained by the Party with respect to the cross-border supply of the service. This Chapter applies to measures taken or maintained by the Party in respect of a financial bond or guarantee, to the extent that such financial bond or guarantee constitutes a covered investment.

This Chapter does not apply to measures adopted or maintained by a Party to the extent that they are covered by Chapter 14 (Financial Services).
ARTICLE 12.2: NATIONAL TREATMENT

Each Party shall accord to investors of the other Party treatment no less favorable than that it accords, in similar circumstances, to its own investors in connection with the establishment, acquisition, expansion, administration, conduct, operation, and sale or other disposition of the investments in its territory.

Each Party shall accord to covered investments treatment no less favorable than it accords, in like circumstances, to the investments of its own investors in connection with the establishment, acquisition, expansion, administration, conduct, operation, and sale or other disposition of the covered investments. investments in its territory.
ARTICLE 12.3: MOST-FAVOURED-NATION TREATMENT

Each Party shall accord to investors of the other Party treatment no less favorable than that it accords, in similar circumstances, to investors of a non-Party with respect to the establishment, acquisition, expansion, administration, conduct, operation and sale or other form of disposition of investments in its territory.

Each Party shall accord to covered investments treatment no less favorable than that it accords, in similar circumstances, to investments of investors of a non-Party with respect to the establishment, acquisition, expansion, administration, conduct, operation and sale or other form of disposition of investments in its territory.

For greater certainty, the treatment with respect to the establishment, acquisition, expansion, administration, management, operation and sale or other form of disposition of investments, referred to in paragraphs 1 and 2, does not include dispute resolution procedures, such as the one provided in Section B of this Chapter, which are established in international treaties, including trade or investment agreements.
ARTICLE 12.4: MINIMUM LEVEL OF TREATMENT 2

Each Party shall accord to covered investments treatment in accordance with customary international law, including fair and equitable treatment, as well as full protection and security.

For greater certainty, paragraph 1 prescribes that the minimum level of treatment for aliens, under customary international law, is the minimum level of treatment that may be accorded to covered investments. The concepts of fair and equitable treatment and full protection and security do not require treatment in addition to or beyond that required by that standard and do not create significant additional rights. The obligation in paragraph 1 to provide:

fair and equitable treatment includes the obligation not to deny justice in criminal, civil or administrative proceedings, in accordance with the principle of due process incorporated in the main legal systems of the world; and

Full protection and security requires each Party to provide the level of police protection that in no case shall be higher than that granted to nationals of the Party where the investment has been made.

The determination that another provision of this Agreement or of a different international agreement has been violated does not establish that this Article has been violated.
ARTICLE 12.5: SENIOR EXECUTIVES AND BOARDS OF DIRECTORS

No Party may require that an enterprise of that Party that is a covered investment appoint natural persons of a particular nationality to senior management positions.

A Party may require that a majority of the members of the boards of directors, or any committee thereof, of an enterprise of that Party that is a covered investment, be of a particular nationality or resident in the Party's territory, provided that the requirement does not significantly impair the investor's ability to exercise control over its investment.
ARTICLE 12.6: PERFORMANCE REQUIREMENTS

No Party may, in connection with the establishment, acquisition, expansion, administration, conduct, operation, sale or other disposition of an investment of an investor of a Party or of a non-Party in its territory, impose or enforce any requirement or enforce any obligation or commitment of 3:

export a certain level or percentage of goods or services;

reach a certain degree or percentage of national content;

buy, use or give preference to goods produced in its territory, or buy goods from people in its territory;

relate in any way the volume or value of imports with the volume or value of exports, or with the amount of foreign currency inflows associated with said investment;

restrict the sales in its territory of the goods or services that said investment produces or provides, relating in any way said sales to the volume or value of its exports or to the earnings that they generate in foreign currency;

transfer a particular technology, a productive process or other knowledge of its property to a person in its territory, except when the requirement is imposed or the obligation or commitment is enforced by a judicial or administrative court or a competition authority, to remedy a practice that has been determined after judicial or administrative proceedings to be anticompetitive under the competition laws of Part 4; either

provide exclusively from the territory of a Party the goods produced by the investment or the services it provides for a specific regional market or the world market.

A measure that requires an investment to use a technology to comply with general regulations applicable to health, safety or the environment, will not be considered inconsistent with paragraph 1(f).

Paragraph 1(f) does not apply when a Party authorizes the use of an intellectual property right in accordance with Article 31 5of the TRIPS Agreement or to measures requiring the disclosure of proprietary information that fall within the scope of application of, and are consistent with, Article 39 of the TRIPS Agreement 6.

For greater certainty, nothing in paragraph 1 shall be construed to prevent a Party, in connection with the establishment, acquisition, expansion, management, conduct, operation, or sale or other disposition of a covered investment or an investment of an investor of a non-Party, in its territory, that imposes or enforces a requirement or enforces an obligation or commitment to train workers in its territory.

No Party may condition the receipt of an advantage, or the continued receipt of an advantage, in connection with the establishment, acquisition, expansion, management, conduct, operation, sale, or other disposition of an investment in its territory by an investor of a Party or a country that is not a Party, upon compliance with any of the following requirements:

reach a certain degree or percentage of national content;

buy, use or grant preferences to goods produced in its territory or to buy goods from people in its territory;

relate, in any way, the volume or value of imports with the volume or value of exports, or with the amount of foreign currency inflows associated with said investment; either

restrict the sales in its territory of the goods or services that said investment produces or provides, relating in any way such sales to the volume or value of its exports or to earnings generated in foreign currency.

Nothing in paragraph 5 shall be construed as preventing a Party from conditioning the receipt of an advantage, or the continued receipt of an advantage, in connection with an investment in its territory by an investor of a Party or of a country that is not a Party, to the fulfillment of a requirement that it locate production, provide services, train or employ workers, build or expand particular facilities or carry out research and development, in its territory.

Paragraphs 1 and 5 shall not apply to any requirement other than the commitment, obligation or requirements set forth in those paragraphs.

The provisions of:

paragraphs 1(a), (b) and (c), and 5(a) and (b) shall not apply to the requirements for qualification of goods or services with respect to export promotion programs and aid programs external; and

paragraphs 5(a) and (b) shall not apply to requirements imposed by an importing Party with respect to the content of goods necessary to qualify for preferential tariffs or quotas.

Provided that such measures are not applied in an arbitrary or unjustifiable manner and provided that such measures do not constitute a disguised restriction on international trade or investment, nothing in paragraphs 1(b), (c) and (f) and 5( a) and (b) shall be construed to prevent a Party from adopting or maintaining measures, including those of an environmental nature:

necessary to ensure compliance with laws and regulations that are not inconsistent with the provisions of this Agreement;

necessary to protect human, animal or plant life or health; either

related to the preservation of non-renewable natural resources alive or not.

Paragraphs 1(b), (c), (f) and (g), and 5(a) and (b) do not apply to public procurement.

This Article does not exclude the application of any commitment, obligation or requirement between private parties, when a Party did not impose or require the commitment, obligation or requirement.

ARTICLE 12.8: MEASURES RELATED TO HEALTH, SAFETY, THE ENVIRONMENT AND LABOR RIGHTS

The Parties recognize that it is inappropriate to promote investment by weakening or reducing the protections provided in their national health, safety, environmental and labor laws. Accordingly, each Party shall endeavor to ensure that it will not waive or repeal, or offer to waive or repeal, such legislation in a manner that weakens or reduces the protection afforded by that legislation, as a means of encouraging the establishment, acquisition, expansion, or retention of an investment in its territory.

Nothing in this Chapter shall be construed to prevent a Party from adopting, maintaining, or enforcing any measure otherwise consistent with this Chapter that it considers appropriate to ensure that investments in its territory are made taking into account concerns in health, safety, environmental and labor matters.
ARTICLE 12.9: CORPORATE SOCIAL RESPONSIBILITY

Each Party will encourage companies that operate in its territory or that are subject to its jurisdiction to voluntarily incorporate into their internal policies internationally recognized corporate social responsibility standards that have been approved by the Parties. These principles address issues such as labor rights, the environment, human rights, community relations and the fight against corruption.

ARTICLE 12.10: TREATMENT IN CASE OF DISPUTE

Notwithstanding the provisions of Article 12.7.5(a), each Party shall accord to investors of the other Party and to covered investments non-discriminatory treatment with respect to any measure it adopts or maintains in connection with losses suffered by investments in their territory as a result of armed conflict or civil strife.

Paragraph 1 shall not apply to existing measures related to subsidies or donations that may be inconsistent with Article 12.2, except for Article 12.7.5(a).
ARTICLE 12.11: EXPROPRIATION AND INDEMNIFICATION 7

Neither Party shall nationalize or expropriate a covered investment, either directly or indirectly, through measures equivalent to expropriation or nationalization ("expropriation"), unless it is:

for public purpose or social interest, in the case of Colombia; and

for reasons of public utility or public interest, in the case of Costa Rica, in accordance with due process, in a non-discriminatory manner and through the payment of prompt, adequate and effective compensation.

The compensation will be paid without delay and will be fully payable and freely transferable. Such compensation will be equal to the fair market value of the expropriated investment immediately before the expropriation took place (“expropriation date”), and will not reflect any change in value because the intent to expropriate was first known. prior to the expropriation date.

If the fair market value is denominated in a freely usable currency, the compensation referred to in paragraph 1 shall not be less than the fair market value on the date of expropriation, plus interest at a commercially reasonable rate for that currency, accumulated from the date of expropriation to the date of payment.

If the fair market value is denominated in a currency that is not freely usable, the compensation referred to in paragraph 1 - converted to the currency of payment, at the market exchange rate in force on the date of payment - will not be smaller than:

the fair market value on the date of expropriation, converted into a freely usable currency, at the current market exchange rate on that date, plus;

interest at a commercially reasonable rate for that freely usable currency, accrued from the date of expropriation to the date of payment.

The affected investor shall have the right, under the national legislation of the Party expropriating, to a review of his case by a judicial or other independent authority of said Party, and to the valuation of his investment in accordance with the established principles. in this Article.

The provisions of this Article shall not apply to the issuance of compulsory licenses granted in relation to intellectual property rights, or to the revocation, limitation or creation of intellectual property rights to the extent that such issuance, revocation, limitation or creation is compatible with the TRIPS Agreement.
ARTICLE 12.12: TRANSFERS

Each Party, in accordance with its legislation, will allow investors of the other Contracting Party to make transfers freely and without undue delay of:

capital contributions;

profits, dividends, interest, capital gains, royalty payments, administration expenses, technical assistance and other charges, returns in kind and other amounts derived from the investment;

products derived from the sale or total or partial liquidation of the covered investment;

payments made pursuant to a contract entered into by the investor, or the covered investment, including a loan agreement;

payments made pursuant to paragraph 1 of Articles 12.10 and 12.11; and

payments arising from the application of Section B.

Each Party shall permit transfers related to a covered investment to be made in freely usable currency at the prevailing market rate of exchange on the date of the transfer.

Notwithstanding paragraphs 1 and 2, a Party may prevent or delay a transfer of money or in kind through the equitable, non-discriminatory and good faith application of its laws relating to:

bankruptcy, insolvency or protection of the rights of creditors 8;

issuing, trading or dealing in securities, futures, options or derivatives;

criminal offences;

financial reporting or record keeping of transfers when necessary to assist law enforcement or financial regulatory authorities; and

guarantee compliance with awards or sentences issued in judicial or administrative proceedings.
ARTICLE 12.13: DENIAL OF BENEFITS

A Party may deny the benefits of this Agreement to:
an investor of the other Party that is an enterprise of that other Party and to investments of such investor if a person of a non-Party owns or controls the enterprise and the latter does not conduct substantial business activities in the territory of the other Party other part; either

an investor of the other Party that is an enterprise of that other Party and to investments of such investor if the enterprise does not carry on substantial business activities in the territory of any Party, other than the denying Party, and a person of the Party that denies owns or controls the business.
ARTICLE 12.14: SPECIAL FORMALITIES AND INFORMATION REQUIREMENTS

Nothing in Article 12.2 shall be construed to prevent a Party from adopting or maintaining a measure that prescribes special formalities associated with a covered investment, such as a requirement that investors be residents of the Party or that covered investments are constituted in accordance with the legislation or regulation of the Party, provided that such formalities do not significantly impair the protection granted by one Party to investors of the other Party and to the investments covered in accordance with this Chapter.

Notwithstanding the provisions of Articles 12.2 and 12.3, a Party may require an investor of the other Party or its covered investment to provide information regarding that investment, solely for informational or statistical purposes. A Party may only request information of a confidential nature, if its national legislation allows it. In such case, that Party shall protect the information that is confidential from any disclosure that could adversely affect the competitive situation of the investor or of the covered investment. Nothing in this paragraph shall be construed as preventing a Party from obtaining or disclosing information regarding the fair and good faith application of its national law.
ARTICLE 12.15: SUBROGATION

When a Party or a body authorized by it has granted insurance or some other financial guarantee against non-commercial risks, with respect to any investment of one of its investors in the territory of the other Party, the latter shall recognize the rights of the former. Party, or body authorized by it, to subrogate the rights of the investor, when it has made a payment by virtue of said insurance or guarantee. The subrogated or transferred right or claim shall not be greater than the investor's original right or claim.

Where a Party or a designated agency of the Party has made a payment to an investor of that Party and has assumed the investor's rights and claims, that investor may not, unless authorized to act on behalf of the Party or the designated agency of the Party that has made the payment, claim said rights and claims against the other Party.
SECTION B: RESOLUTION OF INVESTOR-STATE DISPUTES

ARTICLE 12.16: CONSULTATIONS AND NEGOTIATION

In the event of an investment dispute, the disputing parties must first attempt to resolve the dispute through consultation and negotiation, which may include the use of non-binding third party procedures. The consultation and negotiation procedure will begin with the request sent to the address designated in Annex 12-C. Said request shall be sent to the defendant before the notification of intent, referred to in Article 12.17, and must include the information indicated in paragraphs 12.17.2 (a), (b) and (c).

The consultations will be carried out for a minimum period of six months, extendable by agreement of the disputing parties, and may include face-to-face meetings in the capital of the defendant.
ARTICLE 12.17: SUBMISSION OF A CLAIM TO ARBITRATION


After the minimum term referred to in Article 12.16.2, in the event that a disputing party considers that a dispute relating to an investment cannot be resolved through consultation and negotiation:

the claimant, at its own expense, may submit a claim to arbitration alleging:

that the respondent has breached an obligation under Section A, other than one of the obligations under Articles 12.8, 12.9, or 12.14; and

that the claimant has suffered loss or damage by reason of or as a result of such violation; and

the claimant, on behalf of an enterprise of the respondent that is a legal person owned or controlled directly or indirectly by the claimant, may, in accordance with this Section, submit a claim to arbitration alleging:

that the respondent has breached an obligation under Section A, other than one of the obligations under Articles 12.8, 12.9, or 12.14; and

that the business has suffered loss or damage by reason of or as a result of such violation.

Upon completion of the consultation and negotiation process provided for in Article 12.16, the claimant shall provide the respondent with written notice of its intention to submit the claim to arbitration (“notice of intent”) at least 90 days before the submission of a claim. claim to arbitration under this Section. The notification will specify:

the name and address of the claimant and, if the claim is submitted on behalf of a company, the name, address and place of incorporation of the company;

for each claim, the provision of Section A that is alleged to have been violated and any other applicable provision;

the issues of fact and law on which each claim is based, including the measures at issue; and

the relief sought and the approximate amount of damages claimed.

The claimant must also submit, along with its notice of intent, evidence establishing that it is an investor of the other Party.

Once the conditions set out in paragraph 2 and in Article 12.19 have been met, the claimant may submit the claim referred to in paragraph 1:

in accordance with the ICSID Convention and the ICSID Rules of Procedure for Arbitration Proceedings, provided that both the respondent and the Party to the claimant are parties to the ICSID Convention;

under the UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules; either

if the disputing parties agree, before an ad hoc arbitration institution , or before any other arbitration institution or under any other arbitration rules.

When, after the submission of a claim to arbitration, an additional claim is submitted under the same arbitration proceeding, it shall be considered submitted to arbitration under this Section on the date of its receipt under the applicable arbitration rules and the time limitation shall apply. established in Article 12.19.

in accordance with the rules of procedure agreed by the disputing parties, when applicable.

the name of the arbitrator appointed by the claimant; either

the claimant's written consent to the Secretary General's appointment of said arbitrator.
ARTICLE 12.18: CONSENT OF EACH PARTY TO ARBITRATION

Each Party consents to submit a claim to arbitration, in accordance with this Section and in accordance with this Agreement.

Article II of the New York Convention, which requires a "written agreement"; and

ARTICLE 12.20: PROCEDURE REGARDING PRUDENTIAL MEASURES

Where an investor submits a claim to arbitration under this Section and the respondent invokes Article 12.12.3, or Article 21.5 (Exception to Safeguard the Balance of Payments) as a defense, the tribunal established pursuant to Article 12.21 shall request, at the respondent's request, a written report from the Parties, or from each Party, on the issue of whether and to what extent the indicated provisions are a valid defense to the investor's claim. The court may not proceed until it receives the report(s) in accordance with this paragraph, except as provided in paragraph 2.

ARTICLE 12.21: SELECTION OF ARBITRATORS

Unless the disputing parties agree otherwise, the court will be made up of three arbitrators, one arbitrator designated by each of the disputing parties and the third, who will be the president, will be appointed by agreement of the disputing parties.

have experience or specialized knowledge in public international law, international investment rules, or in the resolution of disputes derived from international investment agreements; and

ARTICLE 12.22: CONDUCT OF ARBITRATION

The disputing parties may agree on the legal seat where any arbitration is to be held in accordance with the applicable arbitration rules in accordance with Article 12.17.4. In the absence of agreement between the disputing parties, the court will determine said place in accordance with the applicable arbitration rules, provided that the place is in the territory of a State that is party to the New York Convention.

The tribunal shall be empowered to accept and consider amicus curiae written submissions from a person or entity that is a non-disputing party. Any non-disputing party wishing to make written submissions to a court (the applicant) may request the court's permission, in accordance with Exhibit 12-D.

Without prejudice to the tribunal's power to hear other objections as preliminary questions, such as an objection that the dispute is not within the jurisdiction of the tribunal, a court shall hear and decide as a preliminary question any objection by the respondent that, as As a matter of law, the claim submitted is not a claim in respect of which an award favorable to the claimant can be rendered in accordance with Article 12.28.

Such objection shall be filed with the court as soon as possible after the constitution of the court, and in no event later than the date the court sets for the respondent to file its answer to the complaint (or in the case of a modification of the notice of arbitration, referred to in Article 12.17.5, the date that the court sets for the respondent to submit its response to the modification).

At the time an objection is received under this paragraph, the court will suspend any proceedings on the merits of the dispute, establish a schedule for the consideration of the objection that will be compatible with any schedule that has been established for the consideration of any other preliminary matter and will issue a decision or award on the objection, stating the grounds thereof.

In deciding an objection under this paragraph, the tribunal will assume as true the allegations of fact advanced by the claimant in support of any claim in the notice of arbitration (or any amendment thereto) and, in disputes submitted in accordance with the UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules, the statement of claim referred to in Article 18 of said rules. The tribunal may also consider any other relevant fact that is not in dispute.

Respondent does not waive any jurisdictional or substantive objection simply because it has or has not raised an objection under this paragraph or makes use of the expedited procedure set out in paragraph 4.

If the respondent so requests, within 45 days from the date of constitution of the tribunal, the tribunal shall decide, in an expedited manner, an objection pursuant to paragraph 3 and any other objection in the sense of that the dispute is not within the jurisdiction of the court. The court will suspend any action on the merits of the litigation and will issue a decision or award on said objection, stating the grounds thereof, no later than 150 days after the date of the request. However, if a disputing party requests a hearing, the court may take an additional 30 days to issue the decision or award. Regardless of whether a hearing has been requested, the court may, by showing extraordinary cause,

When the court decides a respondent's objection under paragraph 3 or 4, it may, if warranted, award the successful disputing party reasonable costs and attorney's fees incurred in objecting or opposing the objection. is. In determining whether such award is justified, the tribunal will consider whether the claimant's claim or respondent's objection was frivolous, and will give the disputing parties a reasonable opportunity to comment.

The defendant shall not assert as a defense, counterclaim or compensatory right or for any other reason that the plaintiff has received or will receive compensation or other compensation for all or part of the alleged damages, pursuant to an insurance or guarantee contract.

The court may recommend an interim measure of protection to preserve the rights of a disputing party, or in order to ensure the full exercise of the jurisdiction of the court, including an order to preserve evidence in the possession or control of a disputing party or to protect the jurisdiction of the court. The court may not order the seizure or prevent the application of a measure that is alleged to be a violation mentioned in Article 12.17.

Within 60 days after such proposed decision or award is communicated, the disputing parties may submit written comments to the tribunal regarding any aspect of their proposed decision or award. The court will consider said comments and issue its decision or award no later than 45 days after the 60-day comment period has expired. ARTICLE 12.23: TRANSPARENCY IN ARBITRATION PROCEEDINGS

Subject to paragraphs 2 and 4, the respondent, upon receipt of the following documents, shall make them available to the non-disputing party and the public:

the notice of intent referred to in Article 12.17.2;

However, any disputing party that intends to use information classified as protected information in a hearing must so inform the court. The court will make appropriate arrangements to protect the information from disclosure.

Nothing in this Section requires a respondent to make available protected information or to provide or allow access to information that it may withhold in accordance with Article 21.2 (Essential Security) and Article 21.4 (Disclosure of Information).

Any protected information that is submitted to the court will be protected from disclosure in accordance with the following procedures:

pursuant to paragraph (d), neither the disputing parties nor the tribunal shall disclose any protected information to the complaining Party or to the public, where the disputing party providing the information clearly designates it so in accordance with paragraph (b). );

any disputing party claiming that certain information constitutes protected information will clearly designate it at the time it is submitted to the court;

a disputing party shall, at the same time that it submits a document that contains information claimed to be protected information, submit a redacted version of the document that does not contain the information. Only the redacted version will be provided to the non-disputing parties and will be made public in accordance with paragraph 1; and

the court shall rule on any objections to the designation of the alleged information as protected information. If the court determines that such information was not properly designated, the disputing party that submitted the information may:

remove all or part of the presentation containing such information; either

agree to resubmit completed and redacted documents with amended designations in accordance with the court's determination and paragraph (c).

In any event, the other disputing party shall, where necessary, resubmit complete and redacted documents, which omit the information withdrawn pursuant to paragraph (d)(i) by the disputing party that first submitted the information, or redesignate the information consistently with the designation made pursuant to paragraph (d)(ii) of the disputing party that first submitted the information.

Nothing in this Section requires the respondent to deny the public access to information that, according to its law, must be disclosed.
ARTICLE 12.24: APPLICABLE LAW

Subject to paragraph 2, where a claim is brought under Article 12.17.1(a) or 12.17.1(b), the tribunal shall decide the issues in dispute in accordance with this Agreement and prevailing rules of international law. and, when applicable, with the legislation of the Party in whose territory the investment was made.

A decision of the Commission declaring the interpretation of a provision of this Agreement, pursuant to Article 20.1.3(c) (The Free Trade Commission), shall be binding on a court established under this Section and any decision or award issued by a court must be compatible with that decision
ARTICLE 12.25: INTERPRETATION OF THE ANNEXES

When the defendant presents as a defense that the measure alleged to be in violation is within the scope of application of Annex I or Annex II, at the request of the defendant, the court will request an interpretation of the matter from the Commission. Within 60 days of delivery of the application, the Commission shall submit to the court in writing any decision declaring its interpretation pursuant to Article 20.1.3(c) (The Free Trade Commission).

The decision issued by the Commission under paragraph 1 shall be binding on the court, and any decision or award issued by the court shall be consistent with that decision. If the Commission does not issue such a decision within the 60-day period, the court will rule on the matter.
ARTICLE 12.26: EXPERT REPORTS

Without prejudice to the appointment of other types of experts when authorized by the applicable arbitration rules, the court, at the request of a disputing party or, on its own initiative, unless the disputing parties do not accept it, may designate one or more experts to report in writing on any matter of fact relating to environmental, health, safety or other scientific matters that a disputing party has raised in a proceeding, in accordance with the terms and conditions agreed upon by the disputing parties.

ARTICLE 12.27: ACCUMULATION OF PROCEDURES

In cases where two or more separate claims have been submitted to arbitration under Article 12.17.1, and the claims raise a common issue of fact or law and arise out of the same facts or circumstances, any disputing party may to obtain a consolidation order, pursuant to the agreement of all disputing parties in respect of whom the consolidation order is sought or pursuant to the terms of paragraphs 2 through 10.

The disputing party that seeks to obtain a consolidation order in accordance with this Article shall submit a written request to the Secretary General and to all the disputing parties in respect of which the consolidation order is sought and shall specify in the request the following:

the name and address of all disputing parties in respect of whom the consolidation order is sought;

the nature of the accumulation order sought; and

the grounds on which the request is based.

Unless the Secretary General determines, within 30 days after receipt of a request under paragraph 2, that the request is manifestly unfounded, a tribunal shall be established under this Article.

Unless all the disputing parties in respect of whom the consolidation order is sought agree otherwise, the tribunal established pursuant to this Article shall be composed of three arbitrators:

an arbitrator appointed by agreement of the claimants;

an arbitrator appointed by the defendant; and

the presiding arbitrator appointed by the Secretary General, who shall not be a national of any of the Parties.

If, within 60 days of the receipt by the Secretary-General of the request made under paragraph 2, the respondent or claimants fail to appoint an arbitrator under paragraph 4, the Secretary-General, at the request of any disputing party in respect of which the consolidation order is sought shall appoint the arbitrator or arbitrators that have not yet been appointed. If the respondent fails to appoint an arbitrator, the Secretary-General shall appoint a national of the respondent, and if the claimants fail to appoint an arbitrator, the Secretary-General shall appoint a national of a Party of the claimants.

In the event that the tribunal established pursuant to this Article has found that two or more claims have been submitted to arbitration pursuant to Article 12.17.1, which raise a common issue of fact or law, and which arise out of the same facts or circumstances, the tribunal may, in the interest of achieving a fair and efficient resolution of the claims and after hearing the disputing parties, by order:

assume jurisdiction, know and jointly determine all or part of the claims;

assume jurisdiction, know and determine one or more claims, the determination of which it considers would contribute to the resolution of the others; either

instruir a un tribunal establecido conforme al Artículo 12.21 a que asuma competencia, conozca y determine conjuntamente, sobre la totalidad o una parte de las reclamaciones, siempre que:

ese tribunal, a solicitud de cualquier demandante que no haya sido anteriormente parte contendiente ante ese tribunal, se reintegre con sus miembros originales, excepto que el árbitro por la parte de los demandantes se designe conforme al párrafo 4(a) y el párrafo 5; y

ese tribunal decida si se ha de repetir cualquier audiencia anterior.

In case a tribunal has been established under this Article, a claimant who has submitted a claim to arbitration under Article 12.17.1, and whose name is not mentioned in a request made under paragraph 2, may make a request in writing. to the court for the purpose of such plaintiff being included in any order made under paragraph 6 and shall specify in the application:

the name and address of the plaintiff;

the nature of the order sought; and

the grounds on which the request is based.

The claimant shall deliver a copy of its request to the Secretary General and to the disputing parties named in the request pursuant to paragraph 2.
Un tribunal que se establezca conforme al presente Artículo dirigirá las actuaciones conforme a lo previsto en el Reglamento de Arbitraje de la CNUDMI, excepto en cuanto sea modificado por la presente Sección.

Un tribunal que se establezca conforme al Artículo 12.21 no tendrá competencia para resolver una reclamación, o parte de una reclamación, respecto de la cual haya asumido competencia un tribunal establecido o instruido de conformidad con el presente Artículo.

A solicitud de una parte contendiente, un tribunal establecido de conformidad con el presente Artículo puede, en espera de su decisión conforme al párrafo 6, disponer que los procedimientos de un tribunal establecido de acuerdo al Artículo 12.21 se aplacen, salvo que ese último tribunal ya haya suspendido sus procedimientos.
ARTÍCULO 12.28: LAUDOS

When a court renders a final award unfavorable to the respondent, the court may award, separately or in combination, only:

pecuniary damages and the corresponding interests; and

property restitution, in which case the award will provide that the defendant may pay pecuniary damages, plus any applicable interest in lieu of restitution.

The court may also award costs and attorneys' fees in accordance with this Section and the applicable arbitration rules.

Subject to paragraph 1, when a claim under Article 12.17.1(b) is submitted to arbitration:

the award that provides for the restitution of the property, will provide that the restitution be granted to the company;

the award that grants pecuniary damages and applicable interest, will provide that the sum of money be paid to the company; and

The award will provide that it is issued without prejudice to any right that any person may have regarding reparation under applicable domestic law.

A court is not empowered to order punitive damages.

For greater certainty, a court will not have jurisdiction to rule on the legality of the measure under national law.
ARTICLE 12.29: PURPOSE AND ENFORCEMENT OF AN AWARD

For greater certainty, the award issued by a court will not have binding force, except for the disputing parties and only with respect to the specific case.

Subject to paragraph 3 and the review procedure applicable to an interim award, the disputing party shall abide by and comply with the award without delay.

The disputing party may not request enforcement of the final award until:

in the case of a final award rendered in accordance with the ICSID Convention:

120 days have elapsed from the date the award was rendered and no disputing party has requested its review or annulment; either

review or annulment procedures have been completed; and

in the case of a final award made pursuant to the ICSID Additional Facility Rules or the UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules or the rules selected pursuant to Article 12.17.4(d):

90 days have elapsed from the date the award was rendered and no disputing party has commenced a proceeding to review, revoke or annul it; either

a court has dismissed or admitted a request for review, revocation or annulment of the award and this decision cannot be appealed.

Each Party shall provide for the due enforcement of an award in its territory.

Where the respondent fails to comply with or comply with a final award, upon delivery of a request by the Party of the claimant, a panel shall be established in accordance with Article 18.6 (Establishment of a Panel). The requesting Party may request in said procedures:

a determination that the breach or contempt of the terms of the final award is contrary to the obligations of this Agreement; and

in accordance with the procedures established in Article 18.11 (Panel Report) a recommendation that the respondent abide by or comply with the final award.

A disputing party may resort to the enforcement of an arbitral award under the ICSID Convention, the New York Convention or the Inter-American Convention, regardless of whether or not the proceedings under paragraph 5 have been commenced.

For the purposes of Article I of the New York Convention and Article I of the Inter-American Convention, the claim submitted to arbitration under this Section shall be deemed to arise from a commercial relationship or transaction.

CHAPTER 13
CROSS-BORDER TRADE IN SERVICES

ARTICLE 13.1: SCOPE OF APPLICATION

This Chapter applies to measures adopted or maintained by a Party that affect cross-border trade in services supplied by service providers of the other Party. Such measures include measures affecting:

the production, distribution, marketing, sale and supply of a service;

the purchase or use of, or payment for, a service;

access to and use of distribution and transportation systems, or telecommunications networks and services related to the provision of a service;

the presence in its territory of a service provider of the other Party; and

the granting of a bond or other form of financial guarantee, as a condition for the provision of a service.

For the purposes of this Chapter, measures adopted or maintained by a Party means measures adopted or maintained by:

central or local governments or authorities; and

non-governmental institutions exercising powers delegated to them by central or local governments or authorities.

This Chapter shall not apply to:

air services 1, including national and international air transport services, scheduled and non-scheduled, as well as related support services for air services, except:

aircraft repair and maintenance services while the aircraft is out of service;

the sale and marketing of air transport services; and

computerized reservation system (SRI) services;

public procurement; and

subsidies or grants provided by a Party, including government-supported loans, guarantees, and insurance.

Articles 13.2, 13.5, 13.9 and 13.10 shall apply to measures by a Party that affect the supply of a service in its territory by a covered investment 2.

This Chapter does not impose any obligation on a Party with respect to a national of the other Party who intends to enter its labor market or who has permanent employment in its territory, nor does it confer any rights on that national with respect to such access or employment or apply to measures relating to citizenship or residence on a permanent basis.

Nothing in this Chapter shall be construed to impose any obligation on a Party with respect to its immigration measures.

This Chapter does not apply to services supplied in the exercise of governmental authority. A service supplied in the exercise of governmental authority means any service that is not supplied on a commercial basis or in competition with one or more service providers.

This Chapter does not apply to measures adopted or maintained by a party in relation to financial services 3, except as provided in Chapter 14 (Financial Services).

For the purposes of this Agreement, the extraction of natural resources, the generation of electricity, the refining of crude oil and its derivatives, hunting and fishing shall not be considered services.
ARTICLE 13.2: SUBSIDIES

Notwithstanding Article 13.1.3(c), if the results of the negotiations related to GATS Article XV.1 enter into force for each of the Parties, this Article shall be jointly revised, as appropriate, with a view to determining whether this Article should be modified for those results to be incorporated into this Agreement. The Parties agree to coordinate such negotiations, as appropriate.

ARTICLE 13.3: NATIONAL TREATMENT

Each Party shall accord to the other Party's service providers treatment no less favorable than it accords, in like circumstances, to its service providers.

ARTICLE 13.4: MOST-FAVOURED-NATION TREATMENT

Each Party shall accord to the service providers of the other Party treatment no less favorable than that which it accords, in similar circumstances, to the service providers of a non-Party.

ARTICLE 13.5: ACCESS TO MARKETS

No Party may adopt or maintain, on the basis of a regional subdivision or its entire territory, measures that:
impose limitations on: (i) the number of service providers, either in the form of

numerical quotas, monopolies or exclusive service providers or by requiring an economic needs test;

the total value of assets or service transactions in the form of numerical quotas or by requiring an economic needs test;

the total number of service transactions or the total amount of service output, expressed in designated numerical units, in the form of quotas or by requiring an economic needs test 4;

the total number of natural persons that can be employed in a certain service sector or that a service provider can employ and who are necessary for the supply of a specific service and are directly related to it, in the form of numerical quotas or by requirement an economic needs test; either

restrict or prescribe the specific types of legal entity or joint venture ( Joint Venture ) through which a service provider can supply a service.
ARTICLE 13.6: LOCAL PRESENCE

Neither Party may require a service provider of the other Party to establish or maintain a representative office or other form of business, or to reside in its territory, as a condition for the cross-border supply of a service.

ARTICLE 13.7: NON-CONFORMING MEASURES

Articles 13.3, 13.4, 13.5 and 13.6 shall not apply to:

any existing non-conforming measure that is maintained by a Party in:

the central level of government, as stipulated by that Party in its Schedule to Annex I; either

the local level of government;

the continuation or prompt renewal of any non-conforming measure referred to in paragraph (a); either

the modification of any non-conforming measure referred to in paragraph (a), provided that said modification does not diminish the degree of conformity of the measure, as it was in force immediately before the modification, with Article 13.3, 13.4, 13.5 or 13.6.

Articles 13.3, 13.4, 13.5 and 13.6 shall not apply to any measure that a Party adopts or maintains in relation to sectors, subsectors or activities as stipulated in its Schedule to Annex II.
ARTICLE 13.8: NOTIFICATION 5

In the event that a Party makes an amendment or modification to any existing non-conforming measure set out in its Schedule to Annex I, in accordance with Article 13.7.1(c), the Party shall notify the other Party of such amendment or modification, during Commission meetings.

In the event that a Party adopts a measure after the entry into force of this Agreement, with respect to the sectors, subsectors or activities established in its Schedule in Annex II, the Party shall, to the extent possible, notify the other Part on said measure.
ARTICLE 13.9: TRANSPARENCY IN THE DEVELOPMENT AND APPLICATION OF REGULATIONS 6

In addition to Chapter 19 (Transparency):
each Party shall establish or maintain adequate mechanisms to respond to inquiries from interested persons regarding its regulations relating to the matters covered by this Chapter 7 .;

At the time of adopting definitive regulations related to the subject matter of this Chapter, each Party shall respond in writing, to the extent possible, including upon request, to the substantive comments received from interested persons regarding the draft regulations; and (c) to the extent possible, each Party will allow a reasonable period of time between the publication of final regulations and the date they enter into force.
ARTICLE 13.10: NATIONAL REGULATIONS

The Parties shall ensure that all measures of general application to which this Chapter applies are administered in a reasonable, objective and impartial manner. This obligation shall not apply to the measures covered by Annex I nor to the measures covered by Annex II of each Party.

(a) Each Party shall maintain or establish as soon as practicable judicial, arbitral or administrative tribunals or procedures that allow, at the request of an affected service supplier of the other Party, the prompt review of administrative decisions affecting trade in services and, where warranted, the application of appropriate remedies. When such procedures are not independent of the agency in charge of the administrative decision in question, the Party shall ensure that they actually allow for an objective and impartial review.

(b) The provisions of paragraph 2(a) shall not be construed to impose an obligation on any Party to establish such courts or procedures where doing so is inconsistent with its constitutional structure or the nature of its legal system.

When a Party requires authorization for the supply of a service, the competent authorities of that Party, within a reasonable period of time after the submission of an application that is considered complete under its national laws and regulations, shall inform the applicant about the decision regarding your application. At the request of such an applicant, the competent authorities of the Party shall provide, without undue delay, information regarding the status of the application. This obligation will not apply to the authorization requirements that are covered by Article 13.7.2.

In order to ensure that measures relating to qualification requirements and procedures, technical standards and licensing requirements do not constitute unnecessary barriers to trade in services, each Party shall endeavor to ensure, as appropriate for each individual sector , that such measures:

are based on objective and transparent criteria, such as competence and ability to provide the service;

are not more burdensome than necessary to ensure the quality of the service; and

in the case of licensing procedures, do not in themselves constitute a restriction on the supply of the service.

To determine whether a Party is in compliance with its obligations under paragraph 4, the provisions of Article VI.5(b) of the GATS shall be taken into account.

The Parties acknowledge their mutual obligations related to domestic regulation in GATS Article VI.4 and affirm their commitment to the development of any necessary disciplines in accordance with Article VI.4. To the extent any such disciplines are adopted by WTO Members, the Parties shall jointly review them, as appropriate, with a view to determining whether this Article should be amended so that such results are incorporated into this Agreement.
ARTICLE 13.11: MUTUAL RECOGNITION

For the purposes of compliance, in whole or in part, with its standards or criteria for the authorization, certification, or licensing of service providers, and subject to the requirements of paragraph 4, a Party may recognize the education or experience obtained, the requirements fulfilled, or the licenses or certificates granted in a certain country. That recognition, which may be effected through harmonization or otherwise, may be based on an agreement or convention with the country in question, or may be granted autonomously.

When a Party recognizes, autonomously or through an agreement or agreement, the education or experience obtained, the requirements fulfilled or the licenses or certificates granted in the territory of a non-Party country, nothing in Article 13.4 shall be interpreted in the sense of require the Party to grant such recognition to education or experience obtained, requirements met, or licenses or certificates granted in the territory of the other Party.

A Party that is a party to an existing or future agreement or arrangement of the type referred to in paragraph 1 shall provide appropriate opportunities for the other Party, if the other Party is interested, to negotiate its accession to such agreement or arrangement or to negotiate with him other comparables. When a Party grants recognition autonomously, it shall provide the other Party with adequate opportunities to demonstrate that the education, experience, licenses or certificates obtained or requirements fulfilled in the territory of that other Party should be recognized.

No Party shall grant recognition in a manner that constitutes a means of discrimination between countries in the application of its standards or criteria for the authorization, certification, or licensing of service providers, or a disguised restriction on trade in services. .

The Parties shall endeavor, to the extent possible, to encourage relevant professional services bodies in their territory to consider the use of standards and criteria in Annex 13-A in discussions for a potential agreement or arrangement referred to in paragraph 1.
ARTICLE 13.12: TRANSFERS AND PAYMENTS

Each Party shall permit all transfers and payments related to the cross-border supply of services to be made freely and without delay to and from its territory.

Each Party shall allow all transfers and payments related to the cross-border supply of services to be made in freely circulating currency at the prevailing market rate of exchange on the date of the transfer.

Notwithstanding the provisions of paragraphs 1 and 2, a Party may prevent or delay the transfer or payment, through the equitable, non-discriminatory and good faith application of its laws with respect to:

bankruptcy, insolvency or protection of the rights of creditors;

issuing, trading or dealing in securities, futures, options or derivatives;

financial reporting or record keeping of transfers when necessary to assist with law enforcement or with financial regulatory authorities;

criminal or criminal offenses; either

guarantee of compliance with orders or judicial or administrative rulings.
ARTICLE 13.13: DENIAL OF BENEFITS

Subject to prior notification in accordance with Article 19.3 (Provision of Information) and inquiries 8, a Party may deny the benefits of this Chapter to:
a service provider of the other Party if the service provider is an enterprise owned or controlled by persons of a non-Party and the enterprise does not have substantial business activities in the territory of the other Party; either

a service provider of the other Party if the service provider is an enterprise owned or controlled by persons of the denying Party and the enterprise does not have substantial business activities in the territory of the other Party.
ARTICLE 13.14: IMPLEMENTATION

The Parties shall consult annually, or as otherwise agreed, to review the implementation of this Chapter and to consider other matters of trade in services of mutual interest.

ARTICLE 13.15: DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this Chapter:

from the territory of one Party to the territory of the other Party;

in the territory of a Party, by a person of that Party, to a person of the other Party; either

by a national of a Party in the territory of the other Party,

but does not include the supply of a service in the territory of a Party by a covered investment or by an investor of the other Party, as defined in Article 12.31 ( Definitions);

company means a company as defined in Article 1.4 (Definitions of General Application) and a branch of a company;

existing means current as of the date of entry into force of this Agreement;

service provider of a Party means a person of that Party who intends to supply or supplies a service 9;

aircraft maintenance and repair services means activities performed on an aircraft or part of an aircraft while the aircraft is out of service and does not include so-called line maintenance;

computerized reservation system (CRS) services means services provided by computerized systems that contain information about air carrier schedules, available seats, fares and fare-setting rules, and through which reservations can be made. make reservations or issue tickets;

professional services means services that require higher education for their rendering 10 or training or equivalent experiences and the exercise of which is authorized or restricted by a Party, but does not include the services provided by persons who practice a trade or to the crew of merchant ships and aircraft; and

sale or marketing of an air transport service means the opportunities for the air carrier concerned to freely sell and market its air transport services, and all aspects of marketing, such as market research, advertising and distribution, but it does not include the determination of prices of air transport services or the applicable conditions.

CHAPTER 14

ARTICLE 14.1: SCOPE OF APPLICATION

This Chapter shall apply to measures adopted or maintained by a Party related to:

financial institutions of the other Party;

investors of the other Party, and the investments of these investors, in financial institutions in the territory of the Party; and

cross-border trade in financial services.

activities or services that are part of a public retirement plan or a legal system of social security; either

activities or services performed on behalf of or with the guarantee of the Party or with the use of its financial resources, including its public entities, however, this Chapter shall apply if a Party allows any of the activities or services mentioned in paragraphs ( a) or (b) are carried out by their financial institutions in competition with a public entity or a financial institution.
ARTICLE 14.2: NATIONAL TREATMENT

Each Party shall accord to investors of the other Party treatment no less favorable than that it accords, in similar circumstances, to its own investors with respect to the establishment, acquisition, expansion, administration, conduct, operation and sale or other form of disposition of financial institutions and investments in financial institutions in its territory.

Each Party shall accord to financial institutions of the other Party and to investments of investors of the other Party in financial institutions treatment no less favorable than it accords, in like circumstances, to its own financial institutions and to investments of its own investors. in financial institutions, with respect to the establishment, acquisition, expansion, administration, conduct, operation and sale or other form of disposition of financial institutions and investments.

For the purposes of the national treatment obligations of Article 14.5.1, a Party shall accord to cross-border financial service providers of the other Party treatment no less favorable than it accords, in similar circumstances, to its own financial service providers. with respect to the provision of the relevant service.
ARTICLE 14.3: MOST FAVOURED NATION TREATMENT

Each Party shall accord to investors of the other Party, to financial institutions of the other Party, to investors' investments in financial institutions, and to cross-border financial service providers of the other Party, treatment no less favorable than that grant, in similar circumstances, to investors, financial institutions, investments by investors in financial institutions, and cross-border providers of financial services from a non-Party.

A Party may recognize prudential measures of a country that is not a Party in the application of the measures included in this Chapter. Such recognition may be:

unilaterally awarded;

achieved through harmonization or other means; either

based on a convention or agreement with a country that is not a Party.

A Party that grants prudential measures recognition under paragraph 2 shall provide the other Party with adequate opportunities to demonstrate that there are or will be circumstances in which there are or will be equivalent regulation, supervision and enforcement, and, if appropriate, that there are or will be procedures relating to the exchange of information between the Parties.

Where a Party grants prudential measures recognition pursuant to paragraph 2(c) and the circumstances set out in paragraph 3 exist, the Party shall provide appropriate opportunities for the other Party to negotiate adherence to the convention or agreement, or to negotiate a covenant or comparable agreement.
ARTICLE 14.4: RIGHT OF ESTABLISHMENT

A Party shall permit an investor of the other Party that does not control or own a financial institution in the Party's territory to establish a financial institution licensed to supply financial services that such institution may provide in accordance with the Party's domestic law. at the time of establishment, without the imposition of numerical restrictions or requirements of specific types of legal form. The obligation not to impose requirements of specific types of legal form does not preclude, subject to Article 14.2, a Party from imposing terms, conditions or other requirements in connection with the establishment of a particular type of entity chosen by an investor of The other part.

A Party shall permit an investor of the other Party that controls or owns a financial institution in the Party's territory to establish such additional financial institutions as may be necessary for the supply of the full scope of financial services permitted under the Party's territory. domestic law of the Party at the time of the establishment of the additional financial institutions. Subject to the provisions of Article 14.2, a Party may impose terms, conditions or other requirements for the establishment of additional financial institutions and determine the institutional and legal form to be used for the supply of specific financial services or carry out specific activities.

The right of establishment pursuant to paragraphs 1 and 2 shall include the acquisition of existing entities.

Subject to the provisions of Article 14.2, a Party may prohibit a specific financial activity or service. Such a ban will not apply to all financial services or to an entire subsector of financial services, such as banking services.

For the purposes of this Article, numerical restrictions means limitations imposed, either on the basis of a regional subdivision or over the entire territory of a Party, on the number of financial institutions, whether in the form of numerical quotas, monopolies, exclusive service providers or by requiring an economic needs test.
ARTICLE 14.5: CROSS-BORDER TRADE

Each Party shall permit, under terms and conditions that grant national treatment, cross-border financial service providers of the other Party to supply the services specified in Annex 14-A.

Each Party shall permit persons located in its territory, and its nationals wherever they may be located, to purchase financial services from cross-border financial service providers of the other Party located in the territory of the other Party. This does not oblige a Party to allow such suppliers to do business or advertise in its territory. Each Party may define doing business and advertising for the purposes of this obligation, provided that such definitions are not inconsistent with paragraph 1.

Without prejudice to other means of prudential regulation of cross-border trade in financial services, a Party may require the registration of the other Party's cross-border financial service providers and financial instruments.
ARTICLE 14.6: NEW FINANCIAL SERVICES 1

Each Party shall permit a financial institution of the other Party to supply any new financial service that that Party would permit its own financial institutions to supply in similar circumstances, without further legislative action by the Party.

A Party may determine the legal and institutional form through which the new financial service may be supplied and may require authorization for its supply. When a Party requires authorization to supply a new financial service, the decision will be made within a reasonable period of time and the authorization may only be refused for prudential reasons or for non-compliance with requirements.
ARTICLE 14.7: TREATMENT OF CERTAIN TYPES OF INFORMATION

Nothing in this Chapter requires a Party to disclose or allow access to:
information relating to the financial business and accounts of individual clients of financial institutions or cross-border providers of financial services; either

any confidential information the disclosure of which would prevent compliance with the law or otherwise be contrary to the public interest or harm the legitimate business interests of particular companies.
ARTICLE 14.8: SENIOR EXECUTIVES AND BOARDS OF DIRECTORS

Neither Party may require financial institutions of the other Party to hire persons of a certain nationality for senior executive positions or other essential personnel.

Neither Party may require that more than a minority of the Board of Directors of a financial institution of the other Party consist of nationals of the Party, persons residing in the territory of the Party, or a combination of both.
ARTICLE 14.9: NON-CONFORMING MEASURES

Articles 14.2 to 14.5 and 14.8 shall not apply to:

any existing non-conforming measure that is maintained by a Party in:

the central level of government, as stipulated by that Party in its Schedule to Annex III; either

the local level of government;

the continuation or prompt renewal of any non-conforming measure referred to in paragraph (a); either

the modification of any non-conforming measure referred to in paragraph (a) provided that said modification does not diminish the degree of conformity of the measure, as it was in force:

immediately before the modification, with Article 14.2, 14.3, 14.4 or 14.8; either

on the date of entry into force of this Agreement, with Article 14.5.

Articles 14.2 to 14.5 and 14.8 shall not apply to any measure that a Party adopts or maintains in relation to sectors, subsectors or activities, as indicated in its Schedule to Annex III.

A non-conforming measure established in a Party's Schedule to Annex I or II as a measure to which Article 12.2 (National Treatment), 12.3 (Most Favored Nation Treatment), 13.3 (National Treatment) or 13.4 ( Most Favored Nation Treatment) will be treated as a non-conforming measure to which Article 14.2 or 14.3, as the case may be, does not apply, to the extent that the measure, sector, subsector or activity set out in the Schedule is covered by this Chapter.
ARTICLE 14.10: EXCEPTIONS

Nothing in this Chapter or in this Agreement shall be construed as preventing a Party from adopting or maintaining measures for prudential reasons 2, among them, the protection of investors, financial market participants, depositors, policyholders, insured or beneficiaries of policies or persons with whom a financial institution or a cross-border provider of financial services has contracted a fiduciary obligation or to guarantee the integrity and stability of the financial system. When such measures are not in accordance with the provisions of this Chapter or of this Agreement, they shall not be used as a means of avoiding the obligations contracted by the Party in accordance with said provisions.

Nothing in this Chapter or in this Agreement applies to non-discriminatory measures of a general nature adopted by any public entity in compliance with monetary, credit, related or exchange policies. This paragraph shall not affect the obligations of a Party under Article 12.6 (Performance Requirements) with respect to measures covered by Chapter 12 (Investment) or under Articles 12.12 (Transfers) or 13.12 (Transfers and Payments).

Notwithstanding the provisions of Articles 12.12 (Transfers) and 13.12 (Transfers and Payments) in the terms in which they are incorporated into this Chapter, a Party may prevent or limit transfers from a financial institution or a cross-border provider of financial services to , or for the benefit of, a person affiliated or related to said institution or provider, through the equitable, non-discriminatory and good faith application of measures related to maintaining the security, solvency, integrity or financial responsibility of financial institutions or from cross-border providers of financial services. This paragraph is without prejudice to any other provision of this Agreement that allows the Party to restrict transfers.

For greater certainty, nothing in this Chapter shall be construed to prevent a Party from adopting or applying measures necessary to ensure compliance with laws or regulations that are not inconsistent with this Chapter, including those relating to the prevention deceptive and fraudulent practices or to address the effects of a breach of financial service contracts, subject to the requirement that such measures not be applied in a manner that could constitute a means of arbitrary or unjustified discrimination between countries where similar conditions prevail, or a disguised restriction on investment in financial institutions or cross-border trade in financial services.
ARTICLE 14.11: TRANSPARENCY

The Parties recognize that transparent regulations and policies governing the activities of financial institutions and cross-border financial service providers are important to facilitate financial institutions and cross-border financial service providers both access to each Party's market, and to operations on them. Each Party undertakes to promote regulatory transparency in financial services.

In lieu of Article 19.2 (Publication), each Party, to the extent practicable:

will publish in advance any regulation of general application related to matters of this Chapter that it proposes to adopt; and

provide interested persons and the other Party a reasonable opportunity to comment on the proposed regulations.

The regulatory authorities of each Party shall make available to the public the requirements, including any necessary documentation, to complete the requests related to the supply of financial services.

At the request of the interested party, the regulatory authority of a Party will inform him of the status of his request. When the authority requires additional information from the applicant, it will be notified without undue delay.

Within 120 days, the regulatory authority of a Party shall make an administrative decision on a complete application of an investor in a financial institution, of a financial institution or of a cross-border financial service provider of the other Party related to the provision of a financial service, and promptly notify the applicant of the decision. An application will not be considered complete until all relevant hearings have been held and all necessary information has been received. Where it is not practicable to make a decision within 120 days, the regulatory authority will notify the data subject without undue delay and will attempt to make the decision subsequently within a reasonable time.

Each Party shall maintain or establish appropriate mechanisms to respond to inquiries from interested parties with respect to measures of general application covered by this Chapter.

Each Party shall ensure that standards of general application adopted or maintained by self-regulatory organizations of the Party are published in a timely manner or otherwise available so that interested persons may become aware of them.

To the extent practicable, each Party shall allow a reasonable period of time between the publication of the final regulations and their entry into force.

In adopting final regulations, the Party shall, to the extent practicable, consider substantive written comments received from stakeholders regarding the proposed regulations.
ARTICLE 14.12: SELF-REGULATED ENTITIES

Where a Party requires a financial institution or cross-border financial service provider of another Party to be a member of, participate in, or have access to a self-regulated entity for the purpose of providing a financial service in or to that Party's territory Party, the Party shall ensure that such self-regulatory entity complies with the obligations of Article 14.2.

ARTICLE 14.13: PAYMENT AND CLEARING SYSTEMS

Each Party shall grant, under terms and conditions that grant national treatment, to financial institutions of another Party established in its territory, access to payment and clearing systems managed by public entities and to official means of financing and refinancing available in the course of normal business operations. This paragraph is not intended to grant access to the Party's lender of last resort facilities.

ARTICLE 14.14: FINANCIAL SERVICES COMMITTEE

The Parties establish the Financial Services Committee (hereinafter referred to as the “Committee”), made up of representatives of each Party. The principal representative of each Party shall be an official of the authority of the Party responsible for financial services established in Annex 14-B.

The functions of the Committee will include, inter alia :

oversee the implementation of this Chapter and its subsequent development;

consider matters related to financial services referred to it by a Party; and

participate in dispute resolution procedures in accordance with Article 14.17.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will meet at least once a year, on the date and according to the previously agreed agenda, to evaluate the operation of this Agreement as it relates to financial services. By mutual agreement, the Parties may hold extraordinary meetings. The Committee will report to the Commission on the results of each meeting.

The meetings may be held by any means agreed by the Parties. When they are face-to-face, they will be held alternately in the territory of each Party, and it will be up to the Host Party to organize the meeting.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will be permanent and will develop its work rules.

All decisions of the Committee must be taken by mutual agreement.
ARTICLE 14.15: CONSULTATIONS

A Party may request consultations with the other Party with respect to any matter related to this Agreement that affects financial services. The other Party shall give due consideration to the request. The Parties shall inform the Committee of the results of the consultations.

Consultations pursuant to this Article shall include officials of the authorities established in Annex 14-B.

Nothing in this Article shall be construed to require regulatory authorities participating in consultations pursuant to paragraph 1 to disclose information or to act in a manner that could interfere in specific matters of regulation, supervision, administration, or enforcement. measures.

Nothing in this Article shall be construed to require a Party to derogate from its relevant legislation relating to the exchange of information between financial regulators or the requirements of an agreement or arrangement between the financial authorities of the Parties.
ARTICLE 14.16: SETTLEMENT OF DISPUTES

Chapter 18 (Dispute Settlement) shall apply, under the terms modified by this Article, to the settlement of disputes arising from the application of this Chapter.

When a Party claims that a dispute arises under this Chapter, Article 18.9 (Panel Selection) shall apply, except:

where the disputing Parties so agree, the panel shall be composed entirely of panelists who meet the qualifications set forth in paragraph 3; and

In any other case:

each disputing Party may select panelists who meet the qualifications established in paragraph 3 or in Article 18.8 (Qualifications of Panelists); and

if the Party complained against invokes Article 14.10, the chairperson of the panel shall meet the qualifications set out in paragraph 3, unless the disputing Parties agree otherwise.

Financial services panelists will:

have specialized knowledge or experience in financial law or the practice of financial services, which may include the regulation of financial institutions;

be selected strictly on the basis of objectivity, reliability, and good judgment;

be independent and not be bound by or receive instructions from any Party; and

comply with the Code of Conduct that will be established by the Commission.

Notwithstanding Article 18.14 (Breach – Suspension of Benefits), when a panel considers that a measure is inconsistent with this Agreement and the measure in dispute affects:

only to the financial services sector, the complaining Party may suspend benefits only in the financial services sector;

only to a sector other than financial services, the complaining Party may not suspend benefits in the financial services sector; either

to the financial services sector and to any other sector, the complaining Party may suspend benefits in the financial services sector that have an effect equivalent to the effect of the measure in the Party's financial services sector.
ARTICLE 14.17: DISPUTES REGARDING INVESTMENT IN FINANCIAL SERVICES

Where an investor of a Party submits a claim pursuant to Section B (Investor-State Dispute Settlement) of Chapter 12 (Investment) and the respondent invokes Article 14.10, the tribunal, at the respondent's request, shall remand the matter in writing. to the Committee for a decision. The court may not proceed pending receipt of a decision or report in accordance with this Article.

In the referral made pursuant to paragraph 1, the Committee will decide whether, and to what extent, Article 14.10 is a valid defense against the investor's claim. The Committee will send a copy of its decision to the court and to the Commission. The decision will be binding on the court.

Where the Committee has not decided the matter within 60 days of receipt of the referral pursuant to paragraph 1, the respondent or the complaining Party may request the establishment of a Panel pursuant to Article 18.6 (Establishment of a Panel). Panel). The Panel will be composed in accordance with Article 14.16. The Panel will send its final report to the Committee and to the Tribunal. The report will be binding on the court.

Where the establishment of a Panel has not been requested in accordance with paragraph 3 within 10 days of the expiration of the 60-day period indicated in paragraph 3, the court may proceed to decide the case.

For the purposes of this Article, tribunal means a tribunal established in accordance with Article 12.21 (Selection of Arbitrators).

CHAPTER 15
TELECOMMUNICATIONS

ARTICLE 15.1: SCOPE OF APPLICATION


This Chapter shall apply to:

the measures adopted or maintained by a Party, related to the access and use of public telecommunications networks and services;

the measures adopted or maintained by a Party related to the obligations of network providers 1and public telecommunications services; and

other measures adopted or maintained by a Party related to public telecommunications networks and services.

The foregoing in accordance with the applicable regulatory principles and subject to the reservations of each Party in Annexes I and II. 2

This Chapter shall not apply to measures related to radio broadcasting and cable distribution of radio or television programming. 3

Nothing in this Chapter shall be construed to:

require a Party to authorize an enterprise of the other Party to establish, build, acquire, lease, operate or supply telecommunications networks or services, except as specifically provided in this Agreement; either

require a Party (or require a Party to bind any company) to establish, build, purchase, lease, operate or provide telecommunications networks or services not offered to the general public; either

prevent a Party from prohibiting persons who operate private networks from using their networks to provide public telecommunications networks or services to third parties.
ARTICLE 15.2: ACCESS TO AND USE OF PUBLIC TELECOMMUNICATIONS NETWORKS OR SERVICES 4


Subject to the reservations entered by the Parties in Annexes I and II, each Party will guarantee that the companies of the other Party have access to and can make use of the public telecommunications networks or services offered in its territory or across its borders in terms and reasonable and non-discriminatory conditions. This obligation must be applied, inter alia , including what is specified in paragraphs 2 to 6.

Each Party shall ensure that such companies are permitted:

purchase or lease and connect terminals or equipment that interface with public telecommunications networks;

connect private circuits, leased or owned, with the public telecommunications networks or services of that Party or with owned or leased circuits or owned by another company;

perform switching, signalling, processing and conversion functions; and

use operating protocols of your choice.

Each Party shall guarantee that companies of the other Party may use public telecommunications networks or services to transmit information in its territory or across its borders, and to have access to information contained in databases or stored in a form that is legible. by a machine in the territory of any Party.

In addition to the provisions of Article 21.1 (General Exceptions), and notwithstanding the provisions of paragraph 3, a Party may take measures that are necessary to:

guarantee the security and confidentiality of messages; either

protect the privacy of non-public data of users of public telecommunications services,

subject to the requirement that such measures are not applied in such a way that they could constitute a means of arbitrary or unjustifiable discrimination or a disguised restriction on trade in services.
Each Party shall ensure that conditions are not imposed on access to and use of public telecommunications networks or services, other than those necessary to:

safeguard the public service responsibilities of providers of public telecommunications networks or services, in particular their ability to make their networks or services available to the general public;

protect the technical integrity of public telecommunications networks or services; either

guarantee that the service providers of the other Party do not supply services that are limited by the reservations listed by the Parties in Annexes I and II.

Provided that the conditions for access to and use of public telecommunications networks and services satisfy the criteria set out in paragraph 5, such conditions may include:

requirements to use specific technical interfaces, including interface protocols, for interconnection with such networks and services;

requirements, when necessary for the interoperability of said services;

the approval of the terminal equipment or other equipment that interfaces with the network and technical requirements related to the connection of such equipment to those networks;

restrictions on the connection of private circuits, leased or owned, with those networks or services, or with owned circuits or leased by another service provider; and

notification, registration, permits and licensing.
ARTICLE 15.3: COMPETITIVE SAFEGUARDS

(a) Each Party shall maintain appropriate measures 5to prevent suppliers who, individually or jointly, are major suppliers in their territory, from using or continuing to use anti-competitive practices.

(b) The anti-competitive practices referred to in paragraph (a) include:

the use of anti-competitive cross-subsidies;

the use of information obtained from competitors with anti-competitive results; and

failure to make available to other providers of public telecommunications services in a timely manner technical information on essential facilities and commercially relevant information that they need to provide public telecommunications services. 6
ARTICLE 15.4: INTERCONNECTION

General Terms and Conditions

Each Party shall ensure that major providers in its territory provide interconnection to providers of public telecommunications services of the other Party:

at any economically and technically feasible point in your network;

under non-discriminatory terms, conditions (including technical standards and specifications), and rates;

of a quality no less favorable than that supplied to its own like services, to like services of unaffiliated service providers or their subsidiaries or other affiliates;

in a timely manner, on terms, conditions (including technical standards and specifications) and cost-oriented rates that are transparent, taking into account economic feasibility, and sufficiently unbundled such that the provider does not need to pay for network components or facilities that it does not require for the service to be provided; and

upon request, at points additional to the network termination points subject to charges reflecting the cost of construction of the necessary additional facilities.

Interconnection Mechanisms with Important Suppliers

The mechanisms for providers of public telecommunications services of a Party to interconnect their facilities and equipment with those of major providers in the territory of the other Party are:

an agreement negotiated between operators or providers, through:

a reference interconnection offer containing terms, rates and conditions that major providers offer to providers of public telecommunications services;

the terms and conditions of a current interconnection agreement; either

the negotiation of a new interconnection agreement; either

the order of the competent authority, through which the interconnection obligation or its conditions are imposed.
ARTICLE 15.5: INDEPENDENT REGULATORY BODIES

Each Party shall ensure that its regulatory body is separate from and not accountable to any provider of public telecommunications networks or services.

Each Party shall ensure that decisions and procedures are impartial with respect to all market participants.
ARTICLE 15.6: UNIVERSAL SERVICE

Each Party has the right to define the type of universal service obligations that it wishes to adopt or maintain. Such obligations will not be considered anti-competitive per se, provided that they are administered in a transparent, non-discriminatory and competitively neutral manner, and will ensure that universal service obligations are not more burdensome than necessary for the type of universal service defined by the Part.

ARTICLE 15.7: AUTHORIZATIONS

When a Party requires a provider of public telecommunications networks or services to have an authorization, the Party shall make available to the public:

all applicable criteria and procedures for granting the authorization;

the period of time normally required to make a decision regarding the application for an authorization; and

the terms and conditions of any authorization you have issued.

When a Party requires a provider of public telecommunications networks or services to have an authorization, the Party shall make a decision on the request for the granting of said authorization within the period of time required by it, and in the event that it denies the request. , will inform the interested party of the reasons for the refusal if they are requested.
ARTICLE 15.8: ALLOCATION, ALLOCATION AND USE OF SCARE RESOURCES

Each Party shall administer its procedures for the allocation and use of scarce telecommunications resources, including frequencies, numbers, and rights of way, in an objective, timely, transparent, and non-discriminatory manner.

The measures of a Party related to spectrum allocation and assignment and frequency management shall not be considered inconsistent with Article 13.5 (Market Access), as applied to Chapters 12 (Investment) or 13 (Cross-Border Trade in Services). . Consequently, each Party retains the right to establish and apply spectrum and frequency management policies that may limit the number of providers of public telecommunications services. Likewise, each Party retains the right to allocate frequency bands taking into account present and future needs and spectrum availability.
ARTICLE 15.9: COMPLIANCE

Each Party shall maintain appropriate procedures and power to enforce the Parties' measures relating to the obligations set forth in Articles 15.2, 15.3 and 15.4. Said procedures will include the ability to impose appropriate sanctions or measures, which may include fines, injunctive relief (temporarily), corrective orders, or the modification, suspension, or revocation of authorizations.

ARTICLE 15.10: SETTLEMENT OF NATIONAL DISPUTES REGARDING TELECOMMUNICATIONS

Disputes between network providers or public telecommunications services

Each Party shall ensure the following:

the providers of public telecommunications networks or services of the other Party may go before their regulatory body to resolve disputes that are related to the matter covered in Articles 15.2, 15.3, and 15.4, and that according to the legislation of the Party, are under the jurisdiction of the regulatory body;

providers of public telecommunications networks or services of another Party that have requested interconnection from a major provider in the territory of the Party, may appeal to the telecommunications regulatory body, within a specific reasonable and public term after the request for interconnection. interconnection by the provider, to resolve disputes regarding the terms, conditions and rates for interconnection with said major provider.


Reconsideration 7

Each Party shall ensure that any provider of public telecommunications networks or services harmed by a resolution or decision of its regulatory body may request such body to reconsider the resolution or decision.


Judicial Review

Each Party shall ensure that any provider of public telecommunications networks or services aggrieved by a ruling or decision of the Party's regulatory body may request judicial review of such ruling or decision by an independent judicial authority. It is understood that this obligation does not go beyond the obligations established in Article 19.5 (Review and Appeal).
ARTICLE 15.11: TRANSPARENCY

In addition to Articles 19.2 (Publication) and 19.3 (Provision of Information), and in addition to other provisions in this Chapter related to the publication of information, each Party shall make its measures related to public telecommunications networks or services publicly available, including:

the regulation of the telecommunications regulatory body, including the rationale for said regulation, and the rates presented before said body;

information on the bodies responsible for the development, modification and adoption of measures;

the current status of the assigned frequency bands, with the exception of frequencies for specific government use and other related information;

the provisions related to interconnection;

the conditions that affect the access and use of networks or public telecommunications services;

measures relating to technical interface specifications; and

measures relating to conditions for connecting terminals or other equipment to a public telecommunications network.

Each Party shall ensure that major providers make their current interconnection agreements or interconnection offers by reference publicly available.
ARTICLE 15.12: FLEXIBILITY IN THE CHOICE OF TECHNOLOGIES

No Party shall prevent providers of public telecommunications services from having the flexibility to choose the technologies they use to supply their services, subject to the necessary requirements to satisfy legitimate public policy interests.

ARTICLE 15.13: RELATIONSHIP WITH OTHER CHAPTERS

In the event of any inconsistency between this Chapter and another Chapter in this Agreement, this Chapter shall control to the extent of the inconsistency.

ARTICLE 15.14: DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this Chapter:

authorization means the authorization to operate networks or provide public telecommunications services in the territory of each Party, including licenses, concessions, permits, registrations and any other authorizations that Party may require, for such purpose;

leased circuits means telecommunications facilities between two or more designated points that are intended for the dedicated use or availability of a particular customer or for other users chosen by that customer;

company means a “company” as defined in Article 1.4 (Definitions of General Application) and includes the branch of a company;

essential facilitiesmeans facilities of a public telecommunications network or service that:
are supplied exclusively or predominantly by a single or a limited number of suppliers; and

it is not economically or technically feasible to replace them in order to provide a service;

interconnection means the link between providers that provide public telecommunications networks or services in order to allow users of one provider to communicate with users of another provider and access services provided by another provider;

reference interconnection offer means an interconnection offer offered by a major provider registered or approved by the telecommunications regulatory body, which establishes the technical, economic and legal conditions under which a provider of public telecommunications networks or services offers access and the interconnection;

regulatory body means the body or authority of a Party responsible for the regulation of telecommunications;

cost-oriented means based on costs (including reasonable profit) and may involve different cost calculation methodologies for different facilities or services;

major supplier means a provider of public telecommunications services that has the ability to materially affect the conditions of participation (from a price and supply point of view) in the relevant market for public telecommunications networks or services, as a result of of:
control of essential facilities; either

the use of its position in the market;

public telecommunications network means the public telecommunications infrastructure that enables the provision of public telecommunications services between specified network termination points;

public telecommunications service or publicly available telecommunications service means any telecommunications service required, explicitly or in fact, by a Party to be offered to the general public in accordance with its respective legislation, which involves the real-time transmission of information supplied by the user between two or more points without any change from point to point in the form or content of the user information;

telecommunicationsmeans any emission, transmission or reception of signs, signals, writings, images, sounds, data or information of any nature by wire, conductors, radio waves, optical media or other electromagnetic systems; and

user means an end user or a provider of public telecommunications services.
CHAPTER 16
ELECTRONIC COMMERCE

ARTICLE 16.1: SCOPE OF APPLICATION

This Chapter applies to measures that affect trade carried out by electronic means, subject to the pertinent provisions of Chapters 12 (Investment), 13 (Cross-Border Trade in Services), 14 (Financial Services) and 15 (Telecommunications), as well as to any of the exceptions or non-conforming measures established in this Agreement, which are applicable to said obligations.

ARTICLE 16.2: GENERAL PROVISIONS

The Parties recognize the economic growth and opportunities that electronic commerce generates and the applicability of the WTO rules to measures that affect electronic commerce.

Considering the potential of electronic commerce as an instrument for social and economic development, the Parties recognize the importance of:

the clarity, transparency and predictability of their regulatory frameworks to facilitate, as far as possible, the development of electronic commerce;

encourage self-regulation in the private sector to promote trust in electronic commerce, taking into account the interests of users, through initiatives such as industry guidelines, model contracts, codes of conduct and trust seals;

interoperability, innovation and competition to facilitate electronic commerce; and

ensure that in the multilateral forums in which they participate and in the development of their internal electronic commerce policies, the interest of all users is taken into account, including businessmen, consumers, non-governmental organizations and relevant public institutions.

The Parties recognize the importance of avoiding unnecessary barriers to trade conducted by electronic means. Taking into account its national policy objectives, each Party shall endeavor to avoid measures that:

restrict trade carried out by electronic means; either

have the effect of treating trade conducted by electronic means more restrictively than trade conducted by other means.
ARTICLE 16.3: CUSTOMS DUTIES

No Party may impose customs duties, taxes, fees or any other charges on the import or export of digital products transmitted by electronic means or in connection with such import or export.

For greater certainty, this Chapter does not prevent a Party from imposing internal taxes or other internal charges on electronically transmitted digital products, provided that they are imposed in a manner consistent with this Agreement.

For the purposes of determining the applicable customs duties, each Party shall determine the customs value of an imported carrier medium that incorporates a digital product based solely on the cost or value of the carrier medium, regardless of the cost or value of the digital product stored on the carrier medium. .
ARTICLE 16.4: CONSUMER PROTECTION

The Parties recognize the importance of maintaining and adopting transparent and effective measures to protect consumers from fraudulent and deceptive business practices in electronic commerce.

To this end, the Parties will make their best efforts to exchange information and experiences on national systems for the protection of consumers involved in electronic commerce.

The Parties may evaluate the use of alternative dispute resolution mechanisms that arise from cross-border trade carried out by electronic means.
ARTICLE 16.5: PAPERLESS TRADE MANAGEMENT

Each Party shall endeavor to make all trade administration documents electronically available to the public.

Each Party shall endeavor to accept electronically filed trade administration documents as the legal equivalent of the paper version of such documents.
ARTICLE 16.6: PROTECTION OF PERSONAL INFORMATION

The Parties shall endeavor to adopt or maintain laws, regulations or administrative measures for the protection of the personal information of users who participate in electronic commerce. The Parties may take into account international standards and the criteria of relevant international organizations on the matter.

The Parties will make their best efforts to exchange information and experiences regarding their domestic regimes for the protection of personal information.
ARTICLE 16.7: COOPERATION

Recognizing the global nature of electronic commerce, the Parties affirm the importance of:

work together to facilitate the use of electronic commerce by micro, small and medium-sized companies;

share information and experiences on laws, regulations, and programs in the field of electronic commerce, including those related to data privacy, consumer trust, security in electronic communications, authentication, intellectual property rights, and electronic government;

work to maintain cross-border information flows as an essential element in fostering a dynamic environment for electronic commerce;

encourage electronic commerce by promoting the adoption of codes of conduct, model contracts, seals of trust, guidelines and enforcement mechanisms in the private sector;

actively participate in regional and multilateral forums, to promote the development of electronic commerce;

recognize electronic signature certificates issued to the public and facilitate the use of cross-border certification services; and

protect users from unsolicited commercial electronic messages.

To achieve the objectives of this Chapter, the Parties may work together through various means, including through information and communication technologies, face-to-face meetings, or expert working groups.
ARTICLE 16.8: RELATIONSHIP WITH OTHER CHAPTERS

In the event of an incompatibility between this Chapter and another Chapter, the other Chapter shall prevail to the extent of the incompatibility.

ARTICLE 16.9: DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this Chapter:

commerce carried out by electronic means means commerce carried out through telecommunications alone, or in conjunction with other information and communication technologies;

trade administration documents means forms that a Party issues or controls that must be completed by a person in connection with the import or export of goods;

personal information means any information about an identified or identifiable natural person;

medium carriermeans any physical object designed primarily for the storage of a digital product by any method now known or later developed, and from which a digital product can be directly or indirectly perceived, reproduced, or communicated, including, but not limited to, a optical media, diskettes and magnetic tapes; and

digital products means computer programs, text, video, images, sound recordings and other products that are digitally encoded. 1

CHAPTER 17
TEMPORARY ENTRY OF BUSINESS PERSONS

ARTICLE 17.1: GENERAL PRINCIPLES

In addition to the provisions of Annex 1-B (Objectives of the Agreement), this Chapter reflects the preferential commercial relationship that exists between the Parties, the mutual objective of facilitating the temporary entry of business persons in accordance with their national legislation 1and the provisions of Annexes 17-B and 17-C, based on the principle of reciprocity and the need to establish transparent criteria and procedures for the temporary entry of business persons. Likewise, this Chapter reflects the need to guarantee border security and protect the national labor force and permanent employment in their respective territories.

This Chapter shall not apply to measures that affect natural persons of one Party seeking access to the labor market of the other Party 2, nor to measures related to citizenship, nationality, permanent residence, or permanent employment.
ARTICLE 17.2: GENERAL OBLIGATIONS

Each Party shall apply its measures relating to the provisions of this Chapter in accordance with Article 17.1, and in particular, shall apply them expeditiously to avoid undue delay or impairment in trade in goods or services or in the performance of investment activities of accordance with this Agreement.

For greater certainty, nothing in this Chapter shall be construed to prevent a Party from applying measures to regulate the entry of natural persons or their temporary stay in its territories, including those measures necessary to protect the integrity of its borders and guarantee the orderly movement of natural persons through them, provided that such measures are not applied in a manner that unduly delays or impairs trade in goods or services or the conduct of investment activities in accordance with this Agreement. The mere fact of requiring a visa for natural persons will not be considered as an undue impairment or impediment in the trade of goods or services, or investment activities in accordance with this Agreement.
ARTICLE 17.3: TEMPORARY ENTRY AUTHORIZATION

Pursuant to the provisions of this Chapter, including the provisions contained in Annex 17-B and Annex 17-C, each Party shall authorize temporary entry to business persons who comply with the immigration measures applicable to temporary entry and other measures related to public health and safety, as well as those related to national security.

Each Party shall establish the value of the fees for processing applications for temporary entry of business persons, in such a way that they do not unduly delay or impair trade in goods or services or the conduct of investment activities in accordance with this Agreement and do not exceed approximate administrative costs.

The authorization of temporary entry under this Chapter does not replace the requirements required for the exercise of a profession or activity in accordance with the specific regulations in force in the territory of the Party that authorizes temporary entry.
ARTICLE 17.4: EXCHANGE OF INFORMATION

In addition to Article 19.2 (Publication), and recognizing the importance to the Parties of the transparency of information on the temporary entry of business persons, each Party shall:

provide the other Party with the relevant materials that allow it to know its measures related to this Chapter; and

no later than six months after the date of entry into force of this Agreement, prepare, publish and make available to interested parties, material explaining the requirements for temporary entry of business persons, including references to applicable national legislation , in accordance with this Chapter, so that business persons of the other Party can know them.

Each Party shall collect, maintain and make available to the other Party, upon request and in accordance with their respective legislation, the information related to the granting of authorizations for temporary entry of business persons, in accordance with this Chapter, to business persons. of the other Party to whom immigration documentation has been issued, in order to include specific information regarding each authorized category in Annex 17-B.
ARTICLE 17.5: TEMPORARY ENTRY COMMITTEE FOR BUSINESS PERSONS

The Parties establish the Committee for the Temporary Entry of Business Persons (hereinafter referred to as the "Committee"), made up of the Contact Points and other competent authorities of each Party, as established in Annex 17-D.

The Contact Points will be in charge of exchanging information in accordance with Article 17.4, and must process the information generated from this Article.

The functions of the Committee will include, among other matters of mutual interest:

review the implementation and administration of this Chapter;

report to the Commission on the implementation and administration of this Chapter, when applicable;

establish the procedures for the exchange of information on the measures that affect the temporary entry of business persons in accordance with this Chapter;

consider developing measures to further facilitate the temporary entry of business persons;

observance of matters established in accordance with Article 17.6; and

deal with any other matter related to this Chapter.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will meet at least once a year, on the date and according to the previously agreed agenda. By mutual agreement, the Parties may hold extraordinary meetings.

The meetings may be held by any means agreed by the Parties, including teleconferences, videoconferences, or any other virtual means. When they are face-to-face, they will be held alternately in the territory of each Party, and it will be up to the Host Party to organize the meeting.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Committee will be permanent and will develop its work rules.

All decisions of the Committee must be taken by mutual agreement.
ARTICLE 17.6: COOPERATION

Taking into account the principles established in Article 17.1, the Parties shall endeavor to the extent possible:
cooperate to strengthen institutional capacity and promote technical assistance among migration authorities;

exchange information and experiences on regulations and implementation of programs and technology in the framework of migration issues, including those related to the use of biometric technology, advanced passenger information systems, frequent flyer programs and security in travel documents; and

strive to actively coordinate in multilateral forums, to promote the facilitation of temporary entry of business persons.
ARTICLE 17.7: SETTLEMENT OF DISPUTES

1. A Party may not initiate proceedings under Chapter 18 (Dispute Settlement) of this Agreement, in respect of a refusal to grant temporary entry under this Chapter unless: (a) the matter concerns a recurring practice; and (b) the affected business person has exhausted, in accordance with applicable national law, the administrative remedies available to him in respect of that particular matter. 2. The remedies referred to in paragraph 1(b) shall be considered exhausted when the competent authority has not issued a final resolution within one year from the start of an administrative proceeding, and the resolution has been delayed for reasons that are not attributable to the affected business person.

ARTICLE 17.8: RELATIONSHIP WITH OTHER CHAPTERS

No provision of this Agreement shall be interpreted in the sense of imposing any obligation on the Parties with respect to their migratory measures, however, the provisions of this Chapter and those pertinent of Chapter 1 (Initial Provisions and General Definitions) shall apply. Chapter 18 (Dispute Settlement), Chapter 19 (Transparency), Chapter 20 (Administration of the Agreement), Chapter 21 (Exceptions) and Chapter 22 (Final Provisions).

Nothing in this Chapter shall be construed in the sense that it imposes obligations or commitments with respect to other Chapters of this Agreement.
ARTICLE 17.9: TRANSPARENCY IN THE PROCESSING OF APPLICATIONS

In addition to Chapter 19 (Transparency), each Party shall establish or maintain adequate mechanisms to respond to inquiries from persons interested in applications and procedures regarding the temporary entry of business persons.

Each Party will make its best efforts to, in the shortest time possible in accordance with its legislation, inform the applicant about the decision taken regarding his application for temporary entry, after considering that said application is complete in accordance with its legislation. At the request of the applicant, the Party will make its best efforts to provide, without undue delay, the information regarding the status of the application through the institutional channels established in its legislation.
ARTICLE 17.10. FUTURE WORK

In order to develop and deepen their relations under this Chapter, the Parties agree that every three years from the date of entry into force of this Agreement, they will review developments related to temporary entry to explore the possibility of expanding disciplines. in this area.

ARTICLE 17.11: DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this Chapter:

business activities means those legitimate activities of a commercial nature created and operated in order to obtain profits in the market. It does not include the possibility of obtaining employment, or salary or remuneration from a labor source in the territory of a Party;

labor certification means any procedure prior to the application for migratory authorization that implies a government permit or authorization related to the labor market;

executivemeans a business person in an organization who primarily directs the management of the organization, exercises extensive decision-making, and receives only general supervision or direction from senior-level executives, the board of directors, and/or shareholders of the business;

temporary entry means the entry of a business person of one Party into the territory of the other Party, without the intention of establishing permanent residence;

specialist means an employee who possesses specialized knowledge of the company's products or services, technical expertise, or an advanced level of experience or knowledge of the company's processes and procedures;

managermeans a business person in an organization who principally directs the organization or a department or subdivision of the organization, supervises and controls the work of other supervisory, professional, or managerial employees, has the authority to hire and fire, or take other related actions with personnel (such as authorization for promotions or furloughs) and exercises discretionary authority in day-to-day operations;

migratory measure means any law, rule, regulation, decision, procedure or any other administrative action that affects the entry and stay of foreigners;

national means national as defined in Article 1.4 (Definitions of General Application), but does not include permanent residents;

business person means a national of a Party who is engaged in trade in goods or supply of services, or in investment activities in the other Party; and

recurring practice means a practice carried out by the migration authorities of a Party on a repetitive basis during a representative period prior to and immediately after its execution.

CHAPTER 18
DISPUTE RESOLUTION

ARTICLE 18.1: COOPERATION

The Parties will at all times endeavor to reach an agreement on the interpretation and application of this Agreement and will make every effort, through cooperation, consultation or otherwise, to reach a mutually satisfactory solution to any matter that could affect its operation.

ARTICLE 18.2: SCOPE OF APPLICATION

Except as otherwise provided in this Agreement, the dispute settlement provisions of this Chapter shall apply to the prevention or settlement of disputes between the Parties relating to the interpretation or application of this Agreement, or where a Party considers that:
an existing or proposed measure of the other Party could be inconsistent with the obligations of this Agreement;

the other Party has in any way breached its obligations under this Agreement; either

an existing or proposed measure of the other Party causes or may cause nullification or impairment within the meaning of Annex 18-A.
ARTICLE 18.3: CHOICE OF THE FORUM

In the event of any dispute arising under this Agreement and under another free trade agreement to which the disputing Parties are parties or the WTO Agreement, the complaining Party may choose the forum to resolve the dispute.

Once the complaining Party has requested the establishment of a panel under one of the treaties referred to in paragraph 1, the selected forum will be exclusive of the others.
ARTICLE 18.4: CONSULTATIONS

A Party may request in writing to the other Party consultations regarding any current or proposed measure or any other matter that could affect the operation of this Agreement, in accordance with the provisions of Article 18.2.

The requesting Party shall deliver the written request to the other Party, explaining the reasons for its request, including identification of the current or proposed measure or other matter at issue and an indication of the legal basis for the claim.

The other Party will respond in writing, and except as provided in paragraph 4, will hold consultations with the requesting Party within a maximum period of 30 days from the date of receipt of the request, unless the Parties agree on another period.

In cases of urgency, including those related to perishable goods or goods or services that rapidly lose their commercial value, such as certain seasonal goods or services, consultations will begin within 15 days from the date of receipt of the request by the other Part.

The requesting Party may require the other Party to make available to it the personnel of its governmental institutions or other regulatory entities that have technical knowledge of the subject matter of the consultations.

The Parties will make every effort to reach a mutually satisfactory solution to any matter through consultations, in accordance with the provisions of this Article. For these purposes, each Party:

provide sufficient information to allow a complete examination of the current or proposed measure or of any other matter that could affect the operation and application of this Agreement; and

shall give confidential or proprietary information received during consultations the same treatment as the Party that provided it.

The consultations will be confidential and without prejudice to the rights of the Parties in proceedings carried out under this Chapter.

Consultations may be carried out in person or through any technological means agreed by the Parties. In the event that the consultation is in person, it must be held in the capital of the consulted Party, unless the Parties agree otherwise.
ARTICLE 18.5: GOOD OFFICES, CONCILIATION OR MEDIATION

The Parties may agree at any time to use methods such as good offices, conciliation or mediation. Such proceedings may begin at any time and may be suspended or terminated at any time by either Party.

The procedures established in accordance with this Article shall be conducted in accordance with the procedures agreed by the Parties.

The procedures that involve good offices, conciliation and mediation will be confidential and without prejudice to the rights of the Parties in any other procedure.
ARTICLE 18.6: ESTABLISHMENT OF A PANEL

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, and without prejudice to paragraph 5, if a matter referred to in Article 18.4 has not been resolved within:

40 days after receiving the request for consultations;

25 days after receipt of the request for consultations in the case of matters referred to in Article 18.4.4; either

any other term that the consulting Parties agree, the complaining Party may refer the matter to a panel.

The complaining Party will deliver to the other Party the written request for the establishment of a panel, in which it must indicate the reason for the request, indicate the specific measures or other matter that is the subject of the complaint and provide a brief summary of the legal bases of the complaint. the claim with enough information to present the problem clearly.

With the delivery of the application, it will be understood that the panel has been established.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the panel shall be composed and perform its functions in accordance with the provisions of this Chapter.

A panel may not be established to review a proposed measure.
ARTICLE 18.7 PANELIST LISTS

Each Party shall designate, no later than six months after the entry into force of this Agreement, to integrate its "Indicative List of Panelists", five people who have the necessary qualities and disposition to exercise the role of panelist. Each Party may modify the panelists on its list when it deems it necessary, prior notification to the other Party. Such designations will be sent to the Commission.

Likewise, the Parties will select by mutual agreement, no later than six months after the entry into force of this Agreement, to integrate the "Indicative List of Panelists from Non-Party States", 10 people who are not nationals or permanent residents of any of the Parties, to exercise the role of chairperson of the panel. At the request of any of the Parties, the Commission may modify the "Indicative List of Panelists from Non-Party States" at any time. Such designations will be sent to the Commission.

The members of the lists prepared in accordance with paragraphs 1 and 2, will meet the qualities stipulated in Article 18.8.1.

Parties may use the lists of panelists developed in accordance with paragraphs 1 and 2, even if these have not been completed.
ARTICLE 18.8: QUALIFICATIONS OF THE PANELISTS

All panelists must:

have specialized knowledge or experience in law, international trade, other matters related to this Agreement or in the settlement of disputes arising from international trade agreements;

be selected strictly on the basis of their objectivity, impartiality, reliability and good judgment;

be independent, have no relationship with any of the Parties and not receive instructions from them; and

comply with the Code of Conduct established by the Commission, in accordance with Article 20.1.2(d) (The Free Trade Commission).

Persons who have been involved in any of the proceedings referred to in Article 18.5 may not serve as panel members in the same dispute.
ARTICLE 18.9: PANEL SELECTION

The panel will consist of three members.

Each Party, within 15 days after the date of receipt of the request for the establishment of the panel, shall designate a panelist, propose up to four non-national and non-permanent resident candidates of the Parties for the position of chairperson of the panel, and notify by writing to the other Party of such appointment and of its proposed candidates for the office of chair of the panel.

If a Party does not designate a panelist within the term stipulated in paragraph 2, such designation shall be made by the other Party within five days following the expiration of said term, among the panelists that make up the "Indicative List of Panelists" of the Party that did not make the designation.

The Parties, within 30 days from the date of receipt of the request for the establishment of a panel, shall endeavor to reach an agreement and designate the chair among the candidates that have been proposed. If in that time the Parties fail to agree on the chair, the chair will be selected by lot among the members of the "Indicative List of Panelists from Non-Party States", within a period of seven additional days after the expiration of the term. 30 days. The non-attendance of a Party to said raffle will not prevent it from being held.

If a Party-appointed panelist resigns, is removed, or is unable to serve, that Party shall appoint a new panelist within 15 days, otherwise the new panelist shall be appointed in accordance with paragraph 3. If the chairperson of the panel resigns, is removed or is unable to serve, the Parties shall agree to the appointment of a replacement within 15 days, otherwise the replacement shall be appointed in accordance with paragraph 4. In either In these cases, any term will be suspended as of the date the panelist or chairperson resigns, is removed or is unable to fulfill their role, and the suspension will end on the date of selection of the replacement.

Each Party may designate a panelist who is not on the "Indicative List of Panelists", provided that they meet the requirements established in Article 18.8.1.

The Parties may mutually agree to designate a panelist who is not on the "Indicative List of Panelists from Non-Party States", provided that they meet the requirements established in Article 18.8.1.
ARTICLE 18.10: RULES OF PROCEDURE

The Commission shall establish the Rules of Procedure, in accordance with Article 20.1.2(d) (The Free Trade Commission).

Any panel established pursuant to this Chapter will follow the Rules of Procedure. A panel may establish, in consultation with the Parties, supplementary rules of procedure that do not conflict with the provisions of this Chapter.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Rules of Procedure will ensure:

that the procedures will guarantee the right, at least to a hearing before the panel, as well as the opportunity to present arguments and replies in writing;

that the hearings before the panel, the deliberations, as well as all the writings and communications presented in the procedure, will be confidential;

that all submissions and comments made by a Party to the panel will be forwarded to the other Party;

the protection of information that either Party designates as confidential information; and

the possibility of using technological means to carry out the proceedings, provided that the means used does not diminish the right of a Party to participate in the proceedings and that their authenticity can be guaranteed.

Unless otherwise agreed by the Parties within 15 days of the establishment of the panel, the panel's mandate shall be:
“To examine, objectively and in light of the relevant provisions of this Agreement, the matter referred to in the request for the establishment of the panel and formulate conclusions, resolutions and recommendations in accordance with the provisions of Article 18.11.”

If the complaining Party alleges in the panel request that a matter has caused nullification or impairment of benefits within the meaning of Article 18.2(c), the terms of reference shall so indicate.

If a Party wishes the panel to make findings as to the level of adverse trade effects on a Party resulting from any measure found to be inconsistent with the obligations of this Agreement, or a measure by a Party found to have caused nullification or impairment within the meaning of Article 18.2(c), the mandate shall state so.

At the request of a Party or on its own initiative, the panel may gather information and request technical advice from the experts it deems necessary, provided that the Parties so agree, and in accordance with the terms and conditions that the Parties agree upon, in accordance with the Rules of procedure.

The panel may delegate to the chair the authority to make administrative and procedural decisions.

The panel may, in consultation with the Parties, modify any term corresponding to its proceedings and make other administrative or procedural adjustments that may be required for the transparency and efficiency of the procedure.

The conclusions, determinations and recommendations of the panel, in accordance with the provisions of Article 18.11, shall be adopted by a majority of its members.

Panelists may submit separate opinions on issues where a unanimous decision was not reached. The panel will not be able to reveal the identity of the panelists who have expressed their opinion in majority or minority.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the expenses of the panel, including the remuneration of its members, will be assumed in equal parts, in accordance with the Rules of Procedure.
ARTICLE 18.11: PANEL REPORT

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the panel shall base its report on the relevant provisions of this Agreement, on the pleadings and submissions of the Parties, or on any information received by the panel pursuant to Article 18.10.

Unless the Parties agree otherwise, the panel will present the initial report to the Parties, within 90 days or 60 days in urgent cases, counted from the appointment of the last panelist.

Only in exceptional cases, if the panel considers that it cannot issue its initial report within 90 days or 60 days in urgent cases, it must inform the Parties in writing of the reasons that justify the delay, together with an estimate of the time in which will issue its report. Any delay shall not exceed an additional period of 30 days, unless the Parties agree otherwise.

The report will contain:

the conclusions, with factual and legal grounds;

determinations as to whether or not a Party has complied with its obligations under this Agreement and any other determinations requested in the mandate; and

its recommendations for the implementation of the decision, when any of the Parties so requests.

The panel shall not disclose confidential information in any of its reports, but may state conclusions derived from such information.

A Party to the dispute may submit to the panel written observations or request written clarifications on the initial report, within 15 days of the submission of said report, or within any other period established by the panel. After considering the comments and requests, the panel will endeavor to respond to such comments and requests and, to the extent it deems appropriate, will develop additional analysis. For these purposes, the panel may, ex officio or at the request of any Party:

request observations from any Party;

perform any diligence it deems appropriate; either

reconsider the initial report.

The Panel will present the final report to the Parties within 30 days of the presentation of the initial report, unless the Parties agree to a different period.

Unless the Parties agree on a different term, the Parties shall make the final report available to the public within 15 days of its presentation to the Parties, subject to the protection of confidential information.
ARTICLE 18.12: REQUEST FOR CLARIFICATION OF THE REPORT

Within 10 days of the submission of the final report, a Party may request the panel in writing to clarify its final report. The panel will respond to such request within 10 days of its submission. The clarification of the panel may not modify the substance of its conclusions, determinations or recommendations.

Submitting a request pursuant to paragraph 1 will not affect the time limits described in Articles 18.13 and 18.14, unless the panel decides otherwise.
ARTICLE 18.13: COMPLIANCE WITH THE REPORT

Upon receipt of a panel report, the Parties will reach an agreement on the resolution of the dispute, which will be in accordance with the panel's findings and recommendations, if any, unless the Parties agree otherwise.

If possible, the remedy will consist of the removal of any measure that does not comply with the provisions of this Agreement or the removal of nullification or impairment within the meaning of Article 18.2(c).

If the Parties do not agree to a solution within 30 days after the report is filed, or such other period as the Parties agree, the complained Party, at the request of the complaining Party, shall enter into negotiations with a view to agreeing compensation. Said compensation will be temporary and will be granted until the controversy is resolved.
ARTICLE 18.14: BREACH – SUSPENSION OF BENEFITS

If the Parties:

they have not reached an agreement on the resolution of the dispute and compensation has not been requested in accordance with Article 18.13, within 30 days of the presentation of the report; either

fail to agree to compensation pursuant to Article 18.13, within 30 days of the filing of the complaining Party's request; either

have reached an agreement on the settlement of the dispute or compensation in accordance with Article 18.13 and the complaining Party considers that the Party complained against has not complied with the terms of the agreement,

the complaining Party may, upon notice to the complained Party, suspend benefits of equivalent effect to such complained Party. In the notification, the complaining Party will specify the level of benefits it proposes to suspend.

When considering the benefits to be suspended pursuant to paragraph 1:

the complaining Party shall first seek to suspend benefits within the same sector or sectors that are affected by the measure or other matter that the panel has concluded is inconsistent with the obligations under this Agreement or has caused nullification or impairment within the meaning of Article 18.2 (c); and

the complaining Party that considers it impracticable or ineffective to suspend benefits within the same sector or sectors may suspend benefits in other sectors.

The suspension of benefits will be temporary and the complaining Party will only apply it until:

that the measure considered inconsistent with the obligations of this Agreement is brought into conformity with it or the required adjustments are made in the case of nullification or impairment of benefits within the meaning of Article 18.2(c);

the moment in which the Parties reach an agreement on the solution of the controversy; either

that the panel described in Article 18.5 conclude in its report that the Party complained against has complied.
ARTICLE 18.15: COMPLIANCE REVIEW AND SUSPENSION OF BENEFITS

A Party may, by written communication to the other Party, request that the panel established pursuant to Article 18.6 be reconstituted to determine:

if the level of suspension of benefits applied by the complaining Party in accordance with Article 18.14.1 is manifestly excessive;

about any disagreement as to the existence of the measures taken to comply with the panel report originally established or regarding the compatibility of such measures with this Agreement.

In the written communication, the Party will indicate the specific measures or issues in dispute and provide a brief summary of the legal basis for the claim that is sufficient to present the problem clearly.

If the original panel or any of its members are unable to reconvene, the provisions of Article 18.9 shall apply mutatis mutandis .

The provisions of Articles 18.10 and 18.11 govern mutatis mutandis for procedures adopted and reports issued by a panel that is reconstituted under this Article, except that, pursuant to Article 18.10.9, The panel will submit an initial report within 60 days from the appointment of the last panelist, if the request refers only to paragraph 1(a) and within 90 days, when the request refers only to paragraph 1(a). 1(b) or both paragraphs.

A panel reconstituted pursuant to paragraph 1(b) will determine whether to terminate any suspension of benefits. If the panel is reconstituted pursuant to paragraph 1(a) and determines that the level of suspended benefits is manifestly excessive, it will set the level of benefits it considers to be of equivalent effect.
ARTICLE 18.16: ISSUES REFERRING TO JUDICIAL AND ADMINISTRATIVE PROCEDURES

The Commission will endeavor to issue, as soon as possible, an appropriate non-binding interpretation or response, when:

a Party considers that a matter of interpretation or application of this Agreement, which arises in a domestic judicial or administrative proceeding of the other Party, merits the interpretation of the Commission; either

notified by a Party of receipt of a request for an opinion on a matter of interpretation or application of this Agreement in a judicial or administrative proceeding of that Party.

The Party in whose territory the court or administrative body is located shall present the interpretation agreed upon by the Commission before the court or administrative body, in accordance with the procedures of the instance in question.

If the Commission fails to reach an agreement, each Party may present its own opinions before the court or administrative body, in accordance with the procedures of the instance in question.
ARTICLE 18.17: SUSPENSION AND TERMINATION OF THE PROCEDURE

The Parties may agree to suspend the work of the panel at any time for a period not exceeding 12 months following the date of such agreement. If the work of the panel remains suspended for more than 12 months, the panel's authority will lapse, unless the Parties agree otherwise. If the authority of the panel lapses and the Parties have not reached an agreement on the resolution of the dispute, nothing in this Article shall prevent a Party from requesting a new proceeding on the same matter.

The Parties may agree to terminate the proceedings before a panel by means of a joint notification to the chair of the panel, at any time prior to the notification of the report.

CHAPTER 19
TRANSPARENCY

ARTICLE 19.1: POINTS OF CONTACT

Each Party shall designate, within 60 days following the date of entry into force of this Agreement, a contact point to facilitate and receive all communications, notifications and information provided by the Parties, on any matter covered by this Agreement.

At the request of the other Party, the contact point will indicate the office or official responsible for the matter and will provide the necessary support to facilitate communication with the requesting Party.
ARTICLE 19.2: PUBLICATION

Each Party will ensure, in the terms in which its legislation allows it, that its laws, regulations, procedures and administrative resolutions of general application that refer to any matter included in this Agreement, are published promptly, to the extent possible. , or in another way are made available for the knowledge of the interested persons and the other Party.

To the extent possible, and to the extent permitted by its law, each Party:

publish any measures referred to in paragraph 1 that it proposes to adopt, related to matters covered by this Agreement; and

provide interested persons and the other Party with an opportunity to comment on such measures.
ARTICLE 19.3: SUPPLY OF INFORMATION

At the request of a Party, and to the extent permitted by its law, the other Party will promptly provide information and respond to questions regarding any matter that could materially affect this Agreement.

Any provision of information provided under this Article shall be made without prejudice to whether or not the measure is consistent with this Agreement.

When a Party that provides information in accordance with this Agreement designates such information as confidential, the other Party will maintain the confidentiality of the same.
ARTICLE 19.4: ADMINISTRATIVE PROCEDURES

In order to administer in a compatible, impartial and reasonable manner all measures of general application that affect the matters covered by this Agreement, each Party shall ensure that, in its administrative procedures in which the measures mentioned in Article 19.2.1 are applied with respect to particular persons, goods or services of the other Party in specific cases:
whenever possible, persons of the other Party who are directly affected by a proceeding receive, in accordance with the law, reasonable notice of the commencement of the proceeding, including a description of its nature, a statement of the legal basis on which the proceeding is initiated and an overview of all the issues in dispute;

when time, the nature of the proceeding and the public interest permit, said persons are given a reasonable opportunity to present facts and arguments in support of their claims, prior to any final administrative action; and

their procedures conform to their legislation.
ARTICLE 19.5: REVIEW AND CHALLENGE

Each Party shall establish or maintain judicial or administrative tribunals or procedures for the purposes of prompt review and, when warranted, correction of final administrative actions related to matters covered by this Agreement. These courts will be impartial and will not be linked to the agency or authority in charge of the administrative application of the law, and will not have a substantial interest in the outcome of the matter.

Each Party shall ensure that, before such courts or in such proceedings, the parties have the right to:

a reasonable opportunity to support or defend their respective positions; and

a resolution based on evidence and arguments or, in cases where required by national legislation, on the file compiled by the administrative authority.

Each Party shall ensure that, subject to the means of challenge or subsequent review that could be resorted to in accordance with its national legislation, said resolutions are implemented by its dependencies or authorities and govern their practice in relation to administrative action. in question.
ARTICLE 19.6: SPECIFIC RULES

The provisions of this Chapter are without prejudice to the specific rules established in other Chapters of this Agreement.

ARTICLE 19.7: DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this Chapter:

administrative resolution of general application means an administrative resolution or interpretation that applies to all persons and factual situations that generally fall within its scope, and that establishes a rule of conduct, but does not include:
resolutions or rulings in an administrative procedure that applies to a particular person, good or service of the other Party in a specific case; either

a resolution that resolves with respect to a particular act or practice.
CHAPTER 20
AGREEMENT ADMINISTRATION

ARTICLE 20.1: THE FREE TRADE COMMISSION

The Parties establish the Free Trade Commission, made up of representatives at the Ministerial level of each Party, in accordance with Annex 20-A, or by the persons they designate.

The Commission shall:

ensure compliance and correct application of this Agreement;

monitor the implementation of this Agreement and evaluate the further development of the Agreement;

supervise the work of all bodies established under this Agreement;

approve at their first meeting, unless the Parties agree otherwise, the Rules of Procedure and the Code of Conduct referred to in Chapter 18 (Dispute Settlement), as well as modify them as necessary;

set the amount of remuneration and expenses that will be paid to panelists, panelist assistants and experts, contemplated in Chapter 18 (Dispute Resolution);

know about any other matter that could affect the operation of the Agreement; and

establish and modify its rules of procedure.

The Commission may:

establish and delegate responsibilities to the bodies established under this Agreement;

modify in compliance with the objectives of this Agreement:

the Schedules established in Annex 2-B (Tariff Elimination Program) by improving tariff conditions for market access, which includes the possibility of accelerating tariff elimination and including one or more goods excluded in the Elimination Program Tariff;

the uniform regulations mentioned in Article 3.29 (Uniform Regulations), the rules of origin established in Annex 3-A (Specific Rules of Origin), Annex 3-B (Certificate of Origin) and Annex 3-C (Procedure for Sending and Receiving the Certificate of Electronic Origin); and

Annex 10-A (Coverage) and Annex 10-C (Means of Publication);

issue interpretations of the provisions of this Agreement, which shall be binding on panels established under Article 18.6 (Establishment of a Panel) and tribunals established under Chapter 12 (Investment);

analyze any proposed amendment to this Agreement to make a recommendation to the Parties;

review the impacts of this Agreement on the micro, small and medium-sized enterprises of the Parties;

request the advice of persons or groups without governmental ties; and

adopt any other action for the exercise of its functions as agreed by the Parties.

Each Party shall implement, in accordance with its legislation, any modification referred to in paragraph 3(b), within the period agreed by the Parties.

All decisions of the Commission will be adopted by mutual agreement.

The Commission shall meet at least once a year in regular session, unless the Commission decides otherwise, or at the request of any of the Parties. The ordinary sessions of the Commission will be held alternately in the territory of the Parties or through any technological means.
ARTICLE 20.2: AGREEMENT COORDINATORS

Each Party shall designate an Agreement Coordinator, in accordance with Annex 20-B.

The Coordinators of this Agreement will jointly develop the following functions:

work on the development of agendas, as well as other preparations for Commission meetings;

prepare and review the technical files necessary for decision-making within the framework of this Agreement;

follow up on the decisions made by the Commission;

on instructions from the Commission, support the supervision of the work of all bodies established under this Agreement; and

hear about any other matter that could affect the operation of this Agreement, which is entrusted to it by the Commission.

CHAPTER 21
EXCEPTIONS

ARTICLE 21.1: GENERAL EXCEPTIONS

For the purposes of Chapter 2 (Merchandise Market Access), Chapter 3 (Rules of Origin and Origin Procedures), Chapter 4 (Trade Facilitation and Customs Procedures), Chapter 5 (Technical Cooperation and Mutual Assistance in Customs Matters), Chapter 6 (Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures) and Chapter 7 (Technical Barriers to Trade), Article XX of the GATT 1994 and its interpretative notes are incorporated into and form an integral part of this Agreement, mutatis mutandis. The Parties understand that the measures referred to in Article XX(b) of the GATT 1994 include environmental measures necessary to protect human, animal or plant life or health, and that Article XX(g) of the GATT of 1994 applies to measures relating to the conservation of exhaustible living or non-living natural resources.

For the purposes of Chapter 12 (Investment), Chapter 13 (Cross-Border Trade in Services), Chapter 14 (Financial Services), Chapter 15 (Telecommunications), Chapter 16 (Electronic Commerce) and Chapter 17 (Temporary Entry of Business Persons), GATS Article XIV (including footnotes) are incorporated into and form an integral part of this Agreement, mutatis mutandis . The Parties understand that the measures referred to in GATS Article XIV(b) include environmental measures necessary to protect human, animal or plant life or health. The Parties understand that the measures referred to in GATS Article XIV(a) include measures necessary to maintain internal public order.
ARTICLE 21.2: ESSENTIAL SECURITY

Nothing in this Agreement shall be construed to:
compel a Party to provide or give access to information the disclosure of which it considers contrary to its essential security interests; either

prevent a Party from applying measures it considers necessary to comply with its obligations with respect to the maintenance or restoration of international peace or security, or to protect its essential security interests.
ARTICLE 21.3: TAXATION

Except as provided in this Article, nothing in this Agreement shall apply to tax measures.

Nothing provided in this Agreement shall affect the rights and obligations of the Parties arising from any tax agreement. In the event of any inconsistency between this Agreement and any of these agreements, the agreement shall control to the extent of the inconsistency. In the case of a tax agreement between the Parties, the competent authorities under that agreement will have the exclusive responsibility to determine if there is any inconsistency between this Agreement and that agreement.

Notwithstanding the provisions of paragraph 2:

Article 2.2 (National Treatment) and those other provisions in this Agreement necessary to give effect to said Article, shall apply to tax measures to the same degree as Article III of the GATT 1994; and

Article 2.11 (Taxes and other Export Charges) shall apply to tax measures.

Subject to the provisions of paragraph 2:

Articles 13.3 (National Treatment) and 14.2 (National Treatment) shall apply to tax measures on income, capital gains, or taxable capital of companies relating to the acquisition or consumption of specific services, except that nothing of The provisions of this paragraph shall prevent a Party from conditioning the receipt or continued receipt of an advantage related to the acquisition or consumption of specific services, to the requirement to supply the service in its territory; and

Articles 12.2 (National Treatment) and 12.3 (Most Favored Nation Treatment), 13.3 (National Treatment) and 13.4 (Most Favored Nation Treatment) and 14.2 (National Treatment) and 14.3 (Most Favored Nation Treatment) shall apply to all tax measures, except those on income, capital gains, or taxable capital of companies, taxes on wealth, inheritance, donations, and generation-skipping transfers .

Nothing in paragraph 4 shall apply:

to any MFN obligation with respect to the benefit granted by a Party in pursuance of any tax treaty;

to any non-conforming provision of any existing tax measure;

to the continuation or prompt renewal of a non-conforming provision of any existing tax measure;

to an amendment to a non-conforming provision of any existing tax measure, as long as such amendment does not reduce, at the time it is made, its degree of conformity with any of the articles mentioned in paragraph 4;

to the adoption or application of any tax measure aimed at ensuring the equitable or effective application or collection of taxes (as permitted under GATS Article XIV(d)); either

to a provision conditioning the receipt, or continued receipt of an advantage in respect of contributions to, or income from, pension trusts or pension plans, on the requirement that the Party maintain continuing jurisdiction over the pension trust or plan pension.

Subject to paragraph 2 and without prejudice to the rights and obligations of the Parties under paragraph 3, paragraphs 3, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9 and 10 of Article 12.6 (Performance Requirements) shall apply to measures tax.

Article 12.11 (Expropriation and Compensation and 12.17 (Submission of a Claim to Arbitration) shall apply to a tax measure that is alleged to be expropriatory. However, no investor may invoke Article 12.11 (Expropriation and Compensation) as the basis of a claim when it has been determined in accordance with this paragraph that the measure does not constitute an expropriation 1. An investor who intends to invoke Article 12.11 (Expropriation and Compensation) with respect to a tax measure, must first submit the matter to the competent authorities of the defendant and claimant Party, indicated in paragraph (b), at the time of delivering in writing notification of its intention to submit a claim to arbitration pursuant to Article 12.17 (Submission of a Claim to Arbitration), so that said authorities may determine whether the tax measure constitutes an expropriation. If the competent authorities do not agree to examine the matter or if, having agreed to examine it, they do not agree that the measure does not constitute an expropriation, within a period of six months after the matter has been submitted to them, the investor may submit its claim to arbitration,

For the purposes of this paragraph, competent authorities means:

in the case of Colombia, the Technical Vice Ministry of the Ministry of Finance and Public Credit ; and

in the case of Costa Rica, the Ministry of Finance ,

or their successors.

For the purposes of this Article:


tax agreement means an agreement to avoid double taxation or another international agreement or arrangement in tax matters; and

taxes and tax measures do not include:

a customs duty as defined in Article 1.4 (Definitions of General Application); either

the measures listed in exceptions (b) and (c) of the definition of customs duty in Article 1.4 (Definitions of General Application).
ARTICLE 21.4: DISCLOSURE OF INFORMATION

Nothing in this Agreement shall be construed to oblige a Party to provide or give access to confidential information, the disclosure of which would impede compliance with its laws, or which would be contrary to the public interest, or which could harm the legitimate commercial interest. private companies, whether public or private.

ARTICLE 21.5: EXCEPTION TO SAFEGUARD THE BALANCE OF PAYMENTS

Nothing in this Agreement shall be construed to prevent a Party from adopting or maintaining restrictive measures with respect to trade in goods and services and with respect to payments and movements of capital, including those related to investment:

in cases of serious difficulties or threats of the balance of payments or external financial difficulties; either

when, in special circumstances, the payments of current transactions and payments and movements of capital, cause or threaten to cause serious difficulties in macroeconomic management, especially for the management of monetary policy or exchange policy of any of the Parties.

All the measures indicated in the previous paragraph must comply with the terms and conditions established in the Agreement on the WTO and in the Constitutive Agreement of the International Monetary Fund .
CHAPTER 22
FINAL PROVISIONS

ARTICLE 22.1: ANNEXES, APPENDICES AND FOOTNOTES

The Annexes, Appendices, and footnotes to this Agreement constitute an integral part thereof.

ARTICLE 22.2: AMENDMENTS

The Parties may agree to any amendment to this Agreement.

When the amendment is agreed and approved in accordance with the legal procedures of each Party, the amendment will constitute an integral part of this Agreement and will enter into force in accordance with Article 22.5, unless the Parties agree on a different term.
ARTICLE 22.3: AMENDMENTS TO THE WTO AGREEMENT

If any provision of the WTO Agreement that has been incorporated into this Agreement is amended, the Parties shall consult with a view to amending the corresponding provision of this Agreement, as appropriate, in accordance with Article 22.2.

ARTICLE 22.4: RESERVATIONS AND INTERPRETATIVE DECLARATIONS

This Agreement may not be subject to reservations or unilateral interpretative declarations.

ARTICLE 22.5: ENTRY INTO FORCE

The Parties will exchange written notifications confirming compliance with the internal legal requirements necessary for the entry into force of this Agreement. This Agreement shall enter into force 60 days after the second of such notifications has been produced, or on the date that the Parties so agree.

ARTICLE 22.6: PROVISIONAL APPLICATION FOR COLOMBIA

Notwithstanding the provisions of Article 22.5, Colombia may provisionally apply this Agreement before its entry into force and until it enters into force in accordance with Article 22.5. The provisional application will also cease when Colombia notifies Costa Rica of its intention not to become a Party to this Agreement, or of its intention to suspend the provisional application.

ARTICLE 22.7: COMPLAINT

Any Party may terminate this Agreement. The denunciation will take effect 180 days after its written notification to the other Party, without prejudice to the fact that the Parties may agree on a different term to make the denunciation effective.
